    What accounts is suggested for payroll in USA?

    In the USA, common accounts used for payroll include:

    1. Salaries and Wages Payable: This account is used to record the amount of salaries and wages owed to employees for work performed but not yet paid.
    2. Payroll Taxes Payable: This account is used to record the amounts of federal, state, and local payroll taxes that are withheld from employees' paychecks and are owed to the appropriate tax authorities.
    3. Employee Benefits Payable: This account is used to record the amounts owed to employees for benefits such as health insurance, retirement plans, and other employee benefits.
    4. Accrued Vacation and Sick Pay: This account is used to record the amount of paid time off that employees have earned but not yet taken.
    5. Advances from Employees: This account is used to record any amounts advanced to employees and will be deducted from future paychecks.
    6. Payroll Clearing: This account is used to reconcile the amounts paid to employees with the amounts recorded in the various payroll accounts.

    It is important to note that the specific accounts used may vary depending on the company's accounting practices and chart of accounts. Companies should consult with their accountants or financial advisors to determine the appropriate accounts for their specific circumstances.

    Which of these accounts is expense accounts and which accounts is current liabilities?

    Of the accounts listed in the previous answer, the following are expense accounts:

    1. Salaries and Wages Expense: This account is used to record the amount of salaries and wages earned by employees during a specific period.
    2. Payroll Taxes Expense: This account is used to record the amounts of federal, state, and local payroll taxes that are incurred by the employer based on employee wages.
    3. Employee Benefits Expense: This account is used to record the cost of providing employee benefits such as health insurance, retirement plans, and other benefits.
    4. Accrued Vacation and Sick Pay Expense: This account is used to record the expense associated with paid time off that employees have earned but not yet taken.

    The following account is a current liability:

    Salaries and Wages Payable: This account is used to record the amount of salaries and wages owed to employees for work performed but not yet paid. It represents a current liability because it is a debt that is expected to be paid within one year.

    The following accounts could be either an expense account or a current liability depending on their balance:

    • Payroll Taxes Payable: This account is used to record the amounts of federal, state, and local payroll taxes that are withheld from employees' paychecks and are owed to the appropriate tax authorities. It represents a current liability because it is a debt that is expected to be paid within one year, but it could also be considered an expense account if the employer is required to pay these taxes on its own.
    • Advances from Employees: This account is used to record any amounts advanced to employees and will be deducted from future paychecks. It could represent a current liability if the amount advanced is expected to be repaid within one year, but it could also be considered an expense account if the amount advanced is not expected to be repaid.

    What s FUTA Payable?

    FUTA stands for the Federal Unemployment Tax Act, which is a federal law that requires employers to pay a tax to fund unemployment benefits for eligible workers. FUTA Payable refers to the amount of federal unemployment tax that an employer owes to the government but has not yet paid. The FUTA tax rate is a percentage of the first $7,000 of each employee's wages, and the rate may vary depending on the state where the employer operates. Employers are required to report their FUTA liability on their quarterly federal tax return, and any unpaid FUTA liability may result in penalties and interest charges.

    What is SUTA?

    SUTA stands for State Unemployment Tax Act, which is a state-level tax that employers must pay to fund unemployment benefits for eligible workers in their state. Each state has its own SUTA tax rate and wage base, which may vary depending on the size of the employer and the industry in which they operate. Employers are typically required to register with their state's workforce agency and pay SUTA taxes on a quarterly basis. The SUTA tax is based on a percentage of the wages paid to employees during a certain period, and the funds collected are used to provide unemployment benefits to eligible workers who have lost their jobs through no fault of their own. Employers who fail to pay their SUTA taxes or who do not comply with state reporting requirements may be subject to penalties and interest charges.

    1030 Payroll Checking Account - Which transactions is processed in this account?

    The Payroll Checking Account (1030) is typically used to process transactions related to payroll. This account is used to record the deposits of funds from the employer's bank account to cover payroll expenses, as well as the payments made to employees via direct deposit or checks. The following transactions are typically processed in this account:

    1. Deposit of funds to cover payroll expenses: This includes the deposit of funds from the employer's bank account to cover the total payroll expense for a specific period.
    2. Payment of wages to employees: This includes the payment of wages and salaries to employees via direct deposit or checks.
    3. Payment of payroll taxes: This includes the payment of federal, state, and local payroll taxes that are withheld from employees' paychecks and are owed to the appropriate tax authorities.
    4. Payment of employee benefits: This includes the payment of employee benefits such as health insurance premiums, retirement plan contributions, and other benefits.
    5. Reconciliation of account balances: This includes the reconciliation of the Payroll Checking Account balance with the total payroll expense for the period, as well as the verification of the accuracy of individual employee paychecks and the payroll tax calculations.

    It is important to note that the specific transactions processed in this account may vary depending on the company's payroll processes and accounting practices. Companies should consult with their accountants or financial advisors to determine the appropriate transactions to process in this account.
