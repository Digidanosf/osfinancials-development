    Batches (Journals)

    What are Journals (Batches) and the Role of Source Documents?

    Journals, also known as batches, are a fundamental concept in accounting. They refer to a chronological record of financial transactions that are systematically recorded in the accounting system. Journals serve as the initial entry point for capturing and documenting the details of each transaction before they are posted to the general ledger.

    One crucial aspect of maintaining accurate journals is the use of source documents. Source documents are original records or paperwork that provide concrete evidence of a financial transaction. These documents play a pivotal role in the accounting process, as they support and validate the entries made in journals. Here's how source documents fit into the journaling process:

    1. Source Documents: Whenever a financial transaction occurs, it generates a source document. These documents can take various forms, such as invoices, receipts, purchase orders, contracts, bank statements, and more. Source documents contain essential information about the transaction, including the parties involved, the date, the nature of the transaction, and the monetary amount.
    2. Recording in Journals: Accountants use these source documents as the basis for creating journal entries. Each transaction is meticulously recorded in the appropriate journal, ensuring that all relevant details from the source document are accurately transcribed. The columns in the journal capture key information, such as the date of the transaction, the accounts involved (debit and credit), a description of the transaction, and the monetary amount. 

    Types of Journals:

    Various types of journals exist, each designed for specific transaction types. Common examples include the Sales Journal, Purchases Journal, Cash Receipts Journal, Cash Payments Journal, and General Journal. Specialised journals may also be used for specific transactions, such as the Payroll journal or Inventory journal.
    1. Batch Processing: Entries within a journal are often processed and posted collectively as a batch. This batch processing approach helps streamline the accounting process and ensures that transactions are recorded accurately and efficiently. Batch processing is especially useful when dealing with a high volume of transactions.
    2. Posting to the General Ledger: After recording transactions in the appropriate journals, the entries, along with their supporting source documents, are transferred or posted to the general ledger. This step summarizes the journal entries and updates the account balances in the general ledger accounts.
    3. Audit Trail: Source documents, along with the journal entries they support, provide an essential audit trail. This audit trail allows accountants and auditors to trace and verify the accuracy and completeness of transactions recorded in the accounting system. It serves as supporting documentation for financial statements and facilitates reconciliation and analysis.

    By incorporating source documents into the accounting process and maintaining organized and accurate journals, businesses can effectively track and analyse their financial transactions, ensure compliance with accounting principles, and generate reliable financial statements.

    What is the purpose of source documents and journals? 

    The purpose of source documents and journals in accounting is to maintain an accurate and systematic record of financial transactions and events in a business. Here is a more detailed explanation of the purpose of these elements in the accounting process: 

    1. Origin of Transactions: Source documents, such as invoices, receipts, bank statements and contracts, serve as the original information indicating when a financial transaction took place. These documents help to maintain a transparent trail of all financial activities. 
    2. Proof of Transactions: Source documents serve as proof of the actual transactions that have taken place in a business. This is important for proving income, expenses and other financial events, especially for tax purposes and financial reporting.
    3. Ledger Entries: Journals are where financial information from source documents is entered into the ledger of a business. These entries are essential to maintaining an organized and structured accounting system, such as osFinancials. 
    4. Preparation of Financial Statements: Source documents and journals serve as the basis for the preparation of financial statements, such as the balance sheet, income statement and cash flow statement. These financial statements provide an overview of the financial health and performance of a business. 
    5. Tax compliance: Source documents are essential to comply with tax obligations. This includes the proof of income, expenses, and deductions required by the tax authorities to process tax applications. 
    6. Decision Making and Analysis: Financial information provided by source documents and journals helps managers make decisions about the financial strategy of an enterprise. This enables them to evaluate the financial performance and determine future goals. 

    In a nutshell, the purpose of source documents and journals is to maintain an orderly and faithful record of financial activities, which is essential for management, financial reporting and compliance with legal requirements. These elements form the backbone of an efficient accounting process and contribute to the transparency and credibility of a company's financial information.

    What is the importance of source documents and journals? 

    Source documents and journals play a critical role in the field of accounting, and their importance cannot be underestimated. Here are some reasons why source documents and journals are so important in accounting: 

    1. Compliance with Financial Rules and Regulations: Accounting source documents, such as invoices, receipts and bank statements, serve as proof of financial transactions. These documents are essential to ensure that a business complies with the financial rules and regulations. 
    2. Accurate Financial Reports: Source documents are the basis on which financial reports, such as a balance sheet and an income statement, are drawn up. Journals are used to record the information from these documents in the ledger of the business. This ensures an accurate registration of financial events. 
    3. Tax returns: Income tax returns, Tax (VAT/GST/Sales tax) returns and other tax obligations require a careful record of income, expenses and deductions. Source documents are essential to substantiate the fidelity of this information. 
    4. Financial Analysis: Financial analysts and managers use source documents and journals to evaluate the financial health of a business. These documents provide a clear picture of the company's performance and financial position. 
    5. Decision Making: Managers use financial information provided by source documents and journals to make decisions about the future of a business. This information can help determine strategies and set financial goals. 
    6. Forensic Investigation: In cases of financial malpractice, fraud or corruption, source documents can play a critical role in forensic investigations. These documents serve as evidence to prove irregularities. 

    In short, source documents and journals are indispensable in the world of accounting to maintain an accurate and reliable record of financial transactions. These documents not only ensure that a business meets legal requirements, but also play a central role in the management and analysis of accurate financial information.

    Journal (Batch) Feature in osFinancials

    The Batch Entry / Journal Entry feature in osFinancials provides versatile functionality for creating customized batch or journal types tailored to your specific requirements. These batch types enable structured transaction organization, ensuring precise allocation to the appropriate ledger and debtor accounts. Here's an overview of this feature:

    1. Batch Types Customization: In osFinancials, you can define various batch types to match your unique needs. While many Sets of Books and templates include standard basic batches like sales journal, purchase journal, payments journal, receipts journal, and general journals (accessible through the "Let osFinancials help you to create a Set of Books?" option), osFinancials allows unlimited specific batch types. Examples of specialise journals, include Payroll journals, Depreciation journals, Fixed assets journals, Accrual journals, or any custom type necessary. Each batch type is designed for specific transaction categories or sets of related transactions, granting precise control over your accounting processes.
    2. Batch / Journal Configuration: The Batch Entry / Journal Entry feature offers options to configure settings, reducing errors during batch transaction entry and processing: Journal configuration: The Standard tab and Advanced tab in the "Options for this batch" offer several options to configure default settings such as the default Contra account, Tax (VAT/GST/Sales tax) settings and other parameters for each type of journal. Configuring these settings, which reduce errors when processing transactions in journals:
      • Contra Account: Assign each batch (journal) to a specific contra account for easy identification and assignment of transactions to the corresponding contra account. This account will be the balancing account. When you balance the batch, the contra transactions will be generated to this contra account in the journal.
      • Lookup Accounts Configuration: You can set a journal so that you can select all accounts for the general ledger, debtors, creditors or only accounts for the general ledger or only debtors or only creditors in the journal. So for example, in the Sales journal you can choose to list only the debtor accounts in the Lookup and can choose when you enter invoices in the Sales journal. This speeds up the allocation of transactions so that only the relevant account type can be listed and selected.
    1. Open Item Transactions: This is particularly useful for matching receipts or payments transactions with outstanding amounts. When you allocate transactions against open item accounts, you can choose to link the transactions against outstanding transactions. Link specific transactions to open item accounts for extra accuracy. This is especially useful for linking receipts or payment transactions with outstanding amounts. For example, when a payment is received from a debtor, you can assign the payment to a specific outstanding invoice.
    2. Repeating Transactions: The Repeating transactions feature allows you to enter transactions occurring on a periodic basis (weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually, etc.). Enter transactions once in the Edit → Repeating transactions menu and, when due, process them into the standard batch, edit (if amounts have changed), delete, or add an entry. This time-saving feature simplifies recurring transaction handling.
    3. Export and Import Feature: Export posted or unposted batches using delimited file options. osFinancials5 also supports Spreadsheet export and Spreadsheet Import options for batch transactions:
      • Posted Batches: Use the "Import a posted batch" option (F9:Process) of the "Batch entry" screen to select a posted batch on the Posted batches tab for import.
      • Spreadsheet Import/Export: osFinancials5 supports Excel Workbook (*.xlsx) file type Spreadsheet import and export features. You can export unposted batch transactions and save them as Excel Workbook files, or import batch transactions from saved Excel Workbook files.
      • Unposted Batches: Export batch transactions using the "Write delimited file" option (F9:Process) to create a Comma Separated Value (.csv) file. Import batch transactions via the "Delimited file import" option (F9:Process) by selecting the Comma Separated file (.csv) file.
    1. Ledger Updates: You don't need to post or update transactions in batches every time. Close a batch and reopen it as needed to add or edit transactions. When finalizing batch processing and posting to the ledger, osFinancials automatically updates ledger accounts, including debtor and creditor accounts where applicable. This ensures financial statements, such as the trial balance, balance sheet and the income statement including relevant reports accurately include and reflect these transactions.

    By utilizing the Batch Entry / Journal Entry feature in osFinancials, you can efficiently manage and allocate transactions across various batch types, maintaining accurate and up-to-date accounting records with precision.

    2. Custom journal types: In addition to the default journals, osFinancials provides you with the flexibility to add custom journal types that align with your specific requirements. Here are some examples of additional custom journal types you can create:
      • Payroll journal: This journal type can be used specifically for recording payroll entries, including salary expenses, tax withholdings, benefits, and other related transactions. It allows for accurate tracking and reporting of payroll-related costs.

    What is the general journal used for?

    The general journal is a book or computer program used in accounting to record all transactions in chronological order. It is also known as the "journal" or "journal entry book".

    Each transaction recorded in the general journal includes a date, a brief description of the transaction, the accounts involved, and the amounts debited or credited. These transactions are then later transferred to the appropriate ledgers for posting and summarization.

    The general journal is used to record a wide range of transactions, such as sales, purchases, expenses, and other financial transactions. It is also used to record adjusting entries and closing entries at the end of an accounting period.

    By recording all transactions in the general journal, a business can keep a complete and accurate record of its financial activities. This information is then used to prepare financial statements and to analyze the financial performance of the business. The general journal is an essential tool for any business that wants to maintain accurate financial records and make informed decisions about its financial activities.

By creating an adjustments journal, you can specifically configure the options for recording month-end or year-end adjustments and easily track these adjustments within your Set of Books.

    Using a dedicated adjustments journal allows you to separate these specific transactions from other routine transactions and provides better organization and visibility for accrual-based adjustments. This ensures that these adjustments are properly captured and reflected in your financial statements.

    Steps when entering and processing transactions in journals

    When entering and processing transactions in journals or batches, there are typically seven steps involved. These steps ensure accurate data entry, balancing, and posting to the ledger. Here is a breakdown of each step:

    1. Set the options or defaults for the batch: Before entering transactions, you may need to configure certain options or defaults for the batch. This could include setting the default currency, account codes, or any other relevant parameters. This step is typically done once, unless there are changes in your requirements.
    2. Enter the alias (name for the batch): Assign a name or alias to the batch to help identify it later when generating reports or exporting/importing data. This allows for easy reference and organisation of your batches.
    3. Select VAT/GST/Sales Tax treatment: If your transactions are subject to VAT/GST/Sales Tax, specify whether they are inclusive or exclusive of these taxes. This ensures accurate tax calculations and reporting. If no tax is applicable to the transactions, this option can be skipped.
    4. Enter the transactions in the batch: Input the transaction details into the batch. This involves capturing the relevant information from source documents, such as invoices or receipts, and entering it into the appropriate fields in the batch. Ensure accuracy and completeness during data entry.
    5. Balance the batch: Once all transactions are entered, ensure that the batch is balanced. This means the total debits and credits in the batch should match. Balancing the batch is crucial to maintaining accurate financial records and identifying any data entry errors.
    6. List the batch and retain the batch listing: Generate a listing of the transactions within the batch and retain it along with the source documents. This serves as a record of the transactions entered and aids in reconciling the data at a later stage.
    7. Post the batch to the ledger: The final step is to post the batch to the appropriate ledger accounts. This action updates the accounts with the transaction data and ensures the financial records are accurate and up to date. Posting transfers the information from the batch to the general ledger for proper accounting.

    In osFinancials, you have the flexibility to enter and capture transactions without immediately posting the batch to the ledger. This allows you to capture transactions on a daily basis and exit the batch screen, proceeding to steps 5 and 7 at a later time. The data will be saved within the batch, ensuring it is not lost and can be balanced and posted at a more convenient time.
