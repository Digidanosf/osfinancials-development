    Goods and Services Tax (GST) - New Zealand

    Goods and Services Tax (GST) in New Zealand

    As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, New Zealand applies a Goods and Services Tax (GST). The GST is a broad-based consumption tax levied on the supply of most goods, services, and other items in New Zealand.

    Here are some key points about the GST in New Zealand:

    1. GST Rate: The standard GST rate in New Zealand is 15%. This means that when consumers purchase most goods and services, they pay an additional 15% of the purchase price as GST.
    2. Taxable Supplies: The GST applies to most goods and services supplied by businesses, including sales of goods, services, imports, and certain intangible supplies. Some supplies may be GST-exempt, meaning GST is not charged on them, or they may be zero-rated, in which case they are subject to GST at a 0% rate.
    3. GST Registration: Businesses with an annual turnover of NZD 60,000 or more are required to register for GST with the Inland Revenue Department (IRD). Once registered, businesses must charge GST on their taxable supplies and can claim input tax credits for the GST paid on their business expenses.
    4. GST Returns: Registered businesses must file regular GST returns with the IRD, typically on a bi-monthly, six-monthly, or annual basis, depending on their GST turnover. The GST returns report the GST collected (output tax) and GST paid on expenses (input tax). The net amount is either remitted to the IRD or refunded.
    5. Input Tax Credits: Registered businesses can claim input tax credits for the GST they have paid on their business expenses. This allows businesses to offset the GST they pay on purchases against the GST they collect on sales, resulting in a net GST liability or refund.
    6. GST on Imported Goods and Services: Goods and services imported into New Zealand are generally subject to GST. For low-value imported goods (valued up to NZD 1,000), offshore suppliers may be required to register for and collect GST on behalf of the New Zealand Customs Service.
    7. GST on Digital Services: As of October 1, 2016, non-resident suppliers of digital services (e.g., streaming services, software downloads) to New Zealand consumers are required to register for and charge GST on these services.

    It's important for businesses operating in New Zealand to understand and comply with the GST regulations, including registration, reporting, and remittance requirements. As tax laws can change, businesses should consult with the Inland Revenue Department or tax professionals for the most up-to-date information regarding the GST in New Zealand.

    Is there any GST Schemes in New Zealand?

    Yes, there are several Goods and Services Tax (GST) schemes in New Zealand. These schemes are designed to simplify GST reporting for specific types of businesses and transactions. The main GST schemes in New Zealand include:

    1. Standard GST Scheme: This is the standard GST scheme that applies to most businesses in New Zealand. Under this scheme, businesses charge GST on their sales (output GST) and claim GST on their purchases (input GST). GST returns are usually filed on a regular basis, either monthly, two-monthly, or six-monthly, depending on the business's turnover.
    2. Payments Basis Scheme: Businesses with an annual turnover of less than NZD $2 million can opt for the payments basis scheme. Under this scheme, businesses only account for GST when they receive payment for their sales or when they make payments for their purchases. This can help with cash flow management for small businesses.
    3. Invoice Basis Scheme: This is the default scheme for businesses with an annual turnover of NZD $24 million or more. Under this scheme, GST is accounted for when an invoice is issued or received, regardless of whether payment has been made. Most larger businesses in New Zealand use this scheme.
    4. Hybrid Basis Scheme: Some businesses may use a hybrid of the payments basis and invoice basis schemes. This means they account for GST on a payments basis for some transactions and on an invoice basis for others.
    5. Non-Residents GST Scheme: This scheme applies to non-resident businesses that make taxable supplies in New Zealand. Non-resident businesses are required to register for GST under this scheme if they exceed the registration threshold for non-residents.
    6. Margin Scheme: The margin scheme is applicable to certain transactions involving land or other specified assets. Under this scheme, GST is calculated based on the margin (the difference between the selling price and the purchase price) rather than the full selling price.
    7. Tourist Refund Scheme (TRS): The TRS allows tourists and visitors to New Zealand to claim a refund of GST paid on goods purchased in New Zealand, subject to certain conditions.

    It's important for businesses to select the most appropriate GST scheme based on their turnover, the nature of their transactions, and their cash flow requirements. Businesses are advised to seek guidance from accounting professionals or tax advisors to determine the best GST scheme for their specific circumstances.

    Structure of GST Accounts in the Chart of Accounts for New Zealand

    In the Chart of Accounts for businesses in New Zealand, Goods and Services Tax (GST) accounts are structured to accurately record and report GST-related transactions. 

    Standard GST Scheme

    The structure of GST accounts for the Standard GST Scheme may vary depending on the size and complexity of the business, but here is a common framework:

    1. GST Control Account (Current Liabilities):
      • 251 - GST Control: This is the main GST Control account that tracks the GST amounts payable or refundable to the New Zealand Inland Revenue Department (IRD). It represents the net balance due to or from the IRD. A credit balance implies that the GST is recoverable from the IRD, while a debit balance indicates GST is payable to the IRD.
    1. GST on Sales (Output Tax):
      • 252 - GST on Sales: This account records the GST charged on sales made by the business. It represents the GST collected from customers and is payable to the IRD.
    1. GST on Purchases (Input Tax Credit):
      • 253 - GST on Purchases: This account records the GST paid on purchases made by the business. It represents the GST that can be claimed as input tax credits (ITC) from the IRD.
    1. GST on Imports:
      • 254 - GST on Imports: This account records the GST paid on goods imported into New Zealand from overseas. The GST on imports is usually paid to the New Zealand Customs Service.
    1. GST Adjustments:
      • 255 - GST Adjustments: This account is used to record any adjustments or corrections to previously reported GST amounts, such as bad debts, errors, or changes in taxable supplies.
    1. GST on Capital Assets:
      • 256 - GST on Capital Assets: This account is used to record GST on the acquisition of capital assets, such as machinery, equipment, or property.
    1. GST on Disposals of Capital Assets:
      • 257 - GST on Disposals: This account records GST on the disposal of capital assets.
    1. GST on Acquisitions from Overseas Suppliers:
      • 258 - GST on Acquisitions: This account records the GST on goods and services acquired from overseas suppliers. The GST on acquisitions is self-assessed by the business and included in the GST return.
    1. GST Bad Debt Relief:
      • 259 - GST Bad Debt Relief: This account records the GST amounts that are claimed as bad debt relief when customers fail to pay their invoices.

    It's important to note that the above structure is a general framework, and businesses may have additional sub-accounts or variations based on their specific needs and the complexity of their GST transactions. Properly organizing and maintaining GST accounts in the Chart of Accounts ensures accurate GST reporting and compliance with IRD regulations in New Zealand. Businesses are encouraged to consult with accounting professionals or tax advisors to set up their Chart of Accounts effectively and ensure compliance with GST requirements.

Therefore, there are typically no specific GST accounts in the Chart of Accounts for businesses related to the TRS.

Businesses will charge GST on the goods sold to eligible tourists, and these GST amounts will be recorded in the regular GST accounts as part of their normal GST reporting.

    GST return in New Zealand

    What blocks or fields appears on the GST return in New Zealand?

    As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, the GST (Goods and Services Tax) return in New Zealand typically includes the following blocks or fields:

    1. Business Information: This includes the business name, IRD (Inland Revenue Department) number, and the GST registration status.
    2. Income: This section requires you to report the total value of taxable supplies (sales) made during the period.
    3. Expenses: Here, you declare the total value of GST-exclusive expenses incurred for business purposes during the period.
    4. Output Tax: This field is calculated automatically based on the sales made (Income) and the applicable GST rate.
    5. Input Tax: This field is calculated automatically based on the expenses claimed (Expenses) and the applicable GST rate.
    6. GST Payable (or Refundable): The difference between Output Tax and Input Tax will determine whether you owe GST to the IRD (GST Payable) or are eligible for a GST refund (GST Refundable).
    7. Payments or Refunds: In this section, you will enter any additional tax payments made or GST refunds received during the return period.
    8. Other GST adjustments: Any adjustments to GST that need to be made, such as corrections for previous periods, should be included here.
    9. Final Amount Payable (or Refundable): This is the net amount of GST that you will need to pay to the IRD or that will be refunded to you.

    It's important to note that tax regulations can change over time, and there might have been updates or changes to the GST return in New Zealand since my last update. I recommend checking with the New Zealand Inland Revenue or a tax professional to get the most up-to-date and accurate information.
