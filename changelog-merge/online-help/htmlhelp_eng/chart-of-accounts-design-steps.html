    Designing a Chart of Accounts

    Designing a chart of accounts from scratch is a crucial step in setting up a robust and efficient accounting system for your business. The chart of accounts organizes and categorises your financial transactions, making it easier to track and analyse your company's financial performance. Here are some steps to consider when designing a Chart of Accounts in osFinancials:

    1. Understand Accounting Standards and Framework and Legislation: Begin by familiarizing yourself with the accounting standards and frameworks, Legal Requirements and Tax Regulations, Legislation Regarding Business Ownership and Structure, Labour Laws and Employment Regulations and any applicable matters regarding the nature of your business and the industry and jurisdiction in which it operates. Let's delve into the significance of each aspect:
      • Accounting Standards and Frameworks: Different countries may follow specific accounting principles and frameworks, such as Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) or International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). Complying with these standards ensures that your financial statements are prepared in a consistent and transparent manner, making it easier for stakeholders to understand your company's financial performance. Adhering to recognized accounting standards also facilitates comparisons with other companies and builds credibility with investors, creditors, and regulatory authorities.
      • Legal Requirements and Tax Regulations: Each country has its own set of legal requirements and tax regulations that businesses must follow. Understanding these regulations is crucial for ensuring compliance and avoiding potential legal and financial risks. Specific attention should be given to areas such as Value Added Tax (VAT), Goods and Services Tax (GST), Sales Tax, Income Tax, and other taxes applicable to your business activities. Non-compliance with tax regulations can lead to penalties and other consequences, so it's essential to be well-informed and stay up-to-date with changes in tax laws.
      • Legislation Regarding Business Ownership and Structure: Depending on the type of business ownership and legal structure (e.g., sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation), there may be specific legal requirements and obligations to consider. The Companies Act or other relevant legislation governs the formation, management, and dissolution of companies. Similarly, for non-profit organizations (NGOs/NPOs), there may be additional regulatory requirements specific to their operations and funding sources.
      • Labour Laws and Employment Regulations: Compliance with labour laws and employment regulations is essential for businesses with employees. These laws cover areas such as wages, working hours, benefits, health and safety, and employment contracts. Failure to comply with labour laws can result in legal liabilities and damage to the company's reputation.

    Incorporating these considerations into your accounting and financial management processes ensures that your business operates ethically and within the legal framework of the country and industry. It also helps you maintain accurate financial records, prepare reliable financial statements, and comply with tax obligations, providing a solid foundation for sustainable growth and success. Seeking professional advice from accountants or legal experts familiar with your country's regulations can be beneficial in ensuring compliance and proper financial management.

    1. Creating a Chart of Accounts from scratch: When creating a Set of Books from (Using the "Customise your own Set of Books (Advanced)?" option in osFinancials, The basic Chart of Accounts will only include three basic control accounts (i.e. Debtors control account, Creditors control account and Retained earnings). You need to add an Inventory Control account into the Chart of Accounts when setting up osFinancials. These are essential control accounts that help track and manage specific financial activities within your business. Let's take a closer look at each of these control accounts:
      • Debtors Control Account: The Debtors Control Account is used to track the amounts owed to your business by customers who have purchased goods or services on credit. It serves as a summary of all outstanding accounts receivable balances. Each individual customer's outstanding balance is recorded in a subsidiary ledger, while the total of all outstanding balances is reflected in the Debtors Control Account in the general ledger. This control account helps monitor and manage your company's accounts receivable effectively.
      • Creditors Control Account: The Creditors Control Account is used to track the amounts your business owes to suppliers or vendors for goods or services purchased on credit. Similar to the Debtors Control Account, individual supplier balances are recorded in a subsidiary ledger, while the total of all outstanding balances is maintained in the Creditors Control Account in the general ledger. This control account enables you to manage and keep track of your company's accounts payable.
      • Retained Earnings Account: The Retained Earnings Account is used to track the accumulated profits or losses earned by the company since its inception, minus any dividends or distributions paid to shareholders. It represents the portion of the company's profits that have been retained within the business rather than distributed to shareholders. The Retained Earnings Account is an essential component of the equity section in the balance sheet.
      • Inventory Control Account: The Inventory Control Account is used to monitor the value of your company's inventory or stock items. It represents the total value of all inventory items held by the company and is a critical account for managing inventory levels and cost of goods sold (COGS). Like the other control accounts, individual item details are maintained in a subsidiary ledger, while the summarized total is reflected in the Inventory Control Account in the general ledger.

    Incorporating these control accounts in osFinancials ensures that your financial data is well-organized, easy to track, and provides valuable insights into your business's financial health. Subsidiary ledgers help provide more detailed information for each account, while control accounts in the general ledger provide a summarised view, allowing for better financial management and reporting.

    1. Add Accounts to the Chart of Accounts 
      • Identify Account Groups and Classifications: Create account groups based on your accounting standards and framework. Common account groups include assets, liabilities, equity, income, and expenses. Within each group, establish relevant classifications to further organize the accounts. For example, under assets, you may have subgroups like current assets, fixed assets, and intangible assets. These groups are added in the Setup → Groups menu on the Setup ribbon).
      • Align with Your Business Plan and Products: Ensure that your Chart of Accounts aligns with your business plan and reflects the nature of your business operations. Consider the products or services you offer and the revenue and expense categories associated with them. For instance, if you sell multiple product lines, you may want separate income accounts for each product to track their individual performance.
      • Add Accounts and Link to Account Groups: Once you have defined your account groups and classifications, start adding specific accounts. Include accounts for cash, accounts receivable, accounts payable, inventory, sales, salaries, rent, etc. Assign each account to its appropriate group and classification. These Accounts are added in the Setup → Accounts menu on the Setup ribbon).
      • Map Accounts to Financial Categories: Map your accounts to the five main financial categories: assets, liabilities, equity, income, and expenses. This step ensures that transactions are correctly classified in financial statements generated by osFinancials. These groups is can be linked to the financial categories in the Setup → Financial categories menu on the Setup ribbon).

    After you have created the accounts and linked them to account groups, and linked accounting groups to financial categories, you can print the structure of your ledger accounts according to Reporting group 1 or Reporting group 2 in a list of accounts or trial balance, income statement and balance sheet. If any adjustments are needed, you can do them at this stage.

    1. Customise language: osFinancials offers the flexibility to customise the language used within your Set of Books to cater to specific industries and regional requirements. This customisation allows businesses to tailor the terminologies and labels used in osFinancials to match their industry jargon, corporate preferences, and local language requirements. By setting your preferred language or customizing the language settings through the Setup → Access control menu on the Setup ribbon, osFinancials will display all labels, terminologies, and interface elements in the selected language whenever you access your Set of Books and print reports or documents. This makes it more convenient and user-friendly, especially when you have customised the language to suit your industry or regional requirements.
    2. Review and Refine: Periodically review and refine your chart of accounts as your business grows or undergoes changes. Ensure that it remains relevant and useful for financial reporting and analysis.
    3. Test and Implement: Before fully implementing the Chart of Accounts, test it with some sample transactions to ensure it works as intended and captures the necessary information accurately.

    By following these steps, you can create a well-structured chart of accounts in osFinancials that supports your business's financial management needs and facilitates accurate reporting and decision-making.
