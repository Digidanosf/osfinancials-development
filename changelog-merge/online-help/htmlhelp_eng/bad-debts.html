    Bad debts

    Introduction to Bad debts 

    Bad debts refer to the portion of a company's accounts receivable that is deemed uncollectible and unlikely to be recovered from customers or clients. When a business extends credit to its customers or sells products or services on credit, there is a risk that some customers may fail to make payment for various reasons, such as financial difficulties, bankruptcy, or disputes. These unpaid debts become bad debts.

    Bad debts are typically recorded as an expense in the financial statements of the business, usually in the form of a provision for bad debts or a specific write-off of the individual customer's outstanding balance. By recognizing bad debts as an expense, the business acknowledges the financial loss it has incurred due to the non-payment of debts.

    Accounting standards, such as IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) and GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles), provide guidelines on how to estimate and account for bad debts. It involves assessing the creditworthiness of customers, analysing the ageing of accounts receivable, and considering historical collection patterns to determine the amount of bad debts or provisions to be recorded.

    Managing bad debts is an important aspect of credit control and financial management for businesses. By monitoring and controlling credit risk, implementing effective credit policies, conducting regular credit assessments, and taking prompt action to recover outstanding debts, businesses can minimize the impact of bad debts on their financial performance.

    Consideration to manage bad debts and provision for bad debts

    It's important to establish a provision for bad debts or irrecoverable debts to accurately reflect the uncertainty surrounding the collection of debts. This provision helps businesses estimate the potential losses and maintain appropriate records of the written-off amounts.

    To effectively manage bad debts and estimate the provision, businesses should consider the following:

    1. Credit control: Carefully assess the creditworthiness of customers before granting credit facilities. Screening debtors and setting credit limits can help mitigate the risk of bad debts.
    2. Regular statements: Send debtor statements on a regular basis to remind customers of their outstanding balances and encourage timely payments.
    3. Age analysis reports: Utilise debtors' age analysis reports to track overdue amounts and follow up with reminders, letters of demand, and other collection efforts.
    4. Early settlement discounts: Consider offering early settlement discounts to incentivize prompt payment from debtors.

    Write-off and provisions for bad debt - scope and accounting standards

    Write-off and provisions for bad debt are applicable to small businesses as well as larger enterprises. The accounting treatment for bad debts and provisions may vary depending on the accounting standards followed by the business, such as IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards), IFRS for SMEs (International Financial Reporting Standards for Small and Medium-sized Entities), and GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles).

    Under these accounting standards, businesses are required to assess and recognize potential losses arising from bad debts and make provisions accordingly. The objective is to ensure that the financial statements reflect the true and fair view of the business's financial position, including the estimation of potential losses due to uncollectible debts.

    The specific guidelines and requirements for recognizing and measuring provisions for bad debts may differ between the accounting standards. IFRS and IFRS for SMEs provide more detailed guidance on the recognition, measurement, and disclosure of bad debts and provisions. GAAP, on the other hand, may have its own specific principles and requirements for the treatment of bad debts.

    It's important for businesses to adhere to the relevant accounting standards applicable in their jurisdiction and industry. This ensures consistency, comparability, and transparency in financial reporting, allowing stakeholders to make informed decisions based on the financial information provided. Consulting with accounting professionals or referring to the specific accounting standards and guidelines is recommended to ensure compliance and accurate financial reporting related to write-offs and provisions for bad debts.

    Example of the accounts for Provision for bad debts and Recovery of bad debts in a chart of accounts

    An example of the account structure for managing accounts receivable, provision for bad debts, and bad debts recovered in the current asset section of based on a 4-digit Chart of Accounts, is as follows: 

    1. G1200-000 Accounts receivable (Debtors control): This account is used to track the total amount owed to your business by your customers. It represents the outstanding receivables and is classified as a current asset. The debit balance of $20,000 indicates the total amount owed by your customers.
    2. G1210-000 Provision for bad debts: This account is used to record the estimated amount set aside for potential bad debts. It is a contra-asset account and is subtracted from the accounts receivable balance. The provision amount represents an estimate of the potential losses from uncollectible debts.
    3. G1220-000 Bad debts recovered: This account is used to record the recovered amount from previously written-off bad debts. It is a current asset account and is used to offset the provision for bad debts when the recovered amount is received.

    By including these accounts in the current assets section of your chart of accounts, you can effectively track accounts receivable, manage provisions for bad debts, and record any recoveries from previously written-off bad debts. This provides a clear picture of your outstanding receivables and the impact of bad debts on your financial statements.

    Please note that the account codes (e.g., G1200-000, G1210-000, G1220-000) may vary depending on your specific accounting system or organizational preferences. It's important to use account codes that align with your chart of accounts structure.
