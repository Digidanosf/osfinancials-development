    Taxes in South Africa

    South Africa (ZA) - Tax system key points

    As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, here are some key points about the tax system in South Africa (ZA):

    1. Value-Added Tax (VAT):
      • VAT is a consumption tax applied to the supply of goods and services in South Africa.
      • The standard VAT rate is 15%, and it applies to most goods and services, with some exceptions.
      • Certain essential items, such as basic foodstuffs and healthcare services, may be zero-rated or exempt from VAT.
      • Registered businesses are required to collect VAT on behalf of the government and submit regular VAT returns.
    1. Income Tax:
      • South Africa has a progressive income tax system, with higher income earners paying higher tax rates.
      • Individual taxpayers are subject to personal income tax, while businesses are subject to corporate income tax.
      • There are various tax deductions, rebates, and credits available to individuals and businesses.
      • Tax rates and brackets may change from year to year, so it's important to stay updated on the latest changes.
    1. Capital Gains Tax (CGT):
      • Capital gains tax is applied to the profit made from the sale of assets, such as property, shares, and investments.
      • Individuals and businesses are required to pay CGT on the disposal of assets.
      • Different rates and exemptions may apply depending on the type of asset and the taxpayer's status.
    1. Dividends Tax:
      • Dividends tax is applied to dividends paid to shareholders.
      • The tax rate is currently 20%, but lower rates or exemptions may apply in certain cases, such as for qualifying companies.
    1. Secondary Tax on Companies (STC):
      • The Secondary Tax on Companies (STC), also known as the "dividend tax," was a tax levied on dividends declared and paid by South African companies to shareholders.
      • As of April 1, 2012, STC was replaced by the Dividends Tax, which is a tax on dividends declared and paid by companies to shareholders.
    1. Estate Duty and Donations Tax:
      • Estate duty is a tax levied on the estate of a deceased person, with rates varying based on the value of the estate.
      • Donations tax is applied to certain gifts and donations made during a person's lifetime.
      • Both taxes are aimed at transferring wealth between generations and discouraging tax avoidance through gifting.
    1. Customs and Excise:
      • Customs and excise duties are applied to imported and locally produced goods, as well as specific goods like alcohol, tobacco, and petroleum products.
      • These taxes are collected by the South African Revenue Service (SARS) at the border or point of production.
    1. Payroll Taxes:
      • Employers are required to deduct Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) tax from employees' salaries and remit it to SARS.
      • Employers also contribute to the Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF) and the Skills Development Levy (SDL).
    1. COID Contributions:
      • COID (Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases) contributions are payments made by employers to provide compensation to employees who are injured, contract diseases, or die in the course of their employment.
      • Employers are required to register with the Compensation Fund and make these contributions to cover potential workplace-related injuries or illnesses.
    1. Tax Filing and Compliance:
      • Individuals and businesses are required to register for tax with SARS and comply with the relevant tax obligations.
      • Tax returns are typically filed annually, and payment due dates may vary depending on the type of tax.

    Please note that tax laws and regulations can change over time, so it's essential to consult the South African Revenue Service (SARS) or a tax professional for the most up-to-date and specific information related to the tax system in South Africa.

    Local and provincial taxes and charges

    In addition to the national taxes mentioned earlier, South Africa has various local and provincial taxes and charges levied by municipalities and provincial governments. These taxes and charges are used to fund local and regional services and infrastructure development. Here are some common examples:

    Local Municipality Taxes and Charges:

    1. Property Rates: Property rates are local taxes levied on property owners by municipalities based on the assessed value of their properties. The rates vary depending on the location and type of property.
    2. Sewerage and Sanitation Charges: Municipalities may charge property owners for the provision of sewerage and sanitation services.
    3. Refuse Removal Charges: Property owners may be charged for the collection and disposal of refuse and waste.
    4. Water and Electricity Charges: Municipalities provide water and electricity services and charge consumers based on their usage.
    5. Special Levies: In certain situations, municipalities may impose special levies on property owners to fund specific projects or improvements within a specific area.
    6. Business Licenses and Permits: Businesses operating within a municipality may be required to obtain licenses or permits, for which fees are charged.

    Provincial Taxes and Charges:

    1. Transfer Duty: In addition to the national transfer duty, some provinces may impose their own transfer duty on the purchase of immovable property.
    2. Vehicle License and Registration Fees: Vehicle owners pay registration fees to the relevant provincial authorities for licensing their vehicles.
    3. Gambling Taxes: Provinces may impose taxes on gambling activities within their jurisdictions.
    4. Environmental Taxes and Levies: Some provinces may introduce environmental taxes and levies to fund environmental conservation and sustainability initiatives.

    It's important to note that the specific local and provincial taxes and charges may vary depending on the region and local regulations. Each municipality and province may have its own set of taxes, charges, and fees, which are used to provide essential services and maintain local infrastructure.

    As these taxes and charges may change over time, individuals and businesses should consult with their local municipality and provincial authorities or seek advice from tax professionals to stay informed about the current local and provincial tax obligations in South Africa.
