    Short-term liabilities (Current liabilities) / Long-term liabilities (Non-current liabilities)

    What is Short-term debt (Current Liability) and Short-term debt (Non-Current Liability)?

    Current liabilities and non-current liabilities are two categories of obligations or debts that a company owes to external parties. These liabilities are reported on the balance sheet or statement of financial position and represent the company's financial obligations as of a specific date.

    1. Current Liabilities: Current liabilities are obligations that are expected to be settled within the company's operating cycle or one year, whichever is longer. Operating cycle refers to the time it takes for a company to convert its inventory into cash through the normal course of business. Current liabilities typically include:
      • Accounts Payable: Money owed by the company to its suppliers or vendors for goods or services purchased on credit.
      • Short-term Loans and Borrowings: Any loans or borrowings due for repayment within the next year.
      • Accrued Liabilities: Expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid, such as wages payable, taxes payable, and utility bills.
      • Short-term Provisions: Amounts set aside to cover expected liabilities or expenses that are likely to be settled within the next year.
      • Dividends Payable: Amounts owed to shareholders for declared dividends that have not yet been paid.
    1. Non-current Liabilities: Non-current liabilities, also known as long-term liabilities, are obligations that are not expected to be settled within the company's operating cycle or one year. Non-current liabilities typically include:
      • Long-term Loans and Borrowings: Loans or borrowings due for repayment beyond the next year.
      • Bonds Payable: Long-term debt securities issued by the company.
      • Deferred Tax Liabilities: Taxes that will become payable in the future due to temporary differences between accounting and tax rules.
      • Long-term Provisions: Amounts set aside to cover long-term liabilities or expenses expected to be settled beyond one year.

    It's essential for investors, creditors, and stakeholders to pay attention to a company's current and non-current liabilities to assess its financial health and liquidity. A company with a large proportion of short-term liabilities compared to its current assets may face challenges in meeting its obligations in the short term. On the other hand, non-current liabilities provide insight into the company's long-term financial obligations and its ability to manage debt over an extended period.

    Example: Corporate Bonds A company issues corporate bonds to raise funds for expansion. The bonds have a maturity period of five years, meaning the company will make periodic interest payments and repay the principal amount at the end of five years. Since the repayment period is beyond one year, these bonds will be classified as "2300 Long-term debt" on the company's balance sheet.

    It's important to note that the specific examples and amounts will vary for each company. The classification of short-term debt and long-term debt depends on the maturity dates of the liabilities and the company's accounting practices. Companies need to ensure accurate and transparent reporting of their debt obligations in their financial statements to provide stakeholders with a clear understanding of their financial position.
