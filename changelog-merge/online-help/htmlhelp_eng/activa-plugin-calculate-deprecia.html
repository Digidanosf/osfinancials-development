    Methods to calculate depreciation

    Calculate Depreciation

    There are various methods according to which depreciation can be calculated. The most commonly used methods are the fixed instalment and the diminishing amount methods.

    Tax Legislation, Companies Act and accounting standards in some countries may prescribe different methods to write off depreciation (wear and tear allowances) over the life span for the types of fixed assets.  

    When you add Fixed assets, you need to decide the method on which depreciation is calculated for the assets. These values only needs to be set once for a Fixed asset on the Details tab of the Activa plugin. The depreciation will be written-off each period over the life span of the fixed asset. 

    Some of these methods is:

    1. Straight-line method (fixed instalment method)
    2. Declining balance method (Reducing-value method)
    3. Double-declining balance
    4. Production method
    5. Sum-of-years digits

    In addition to these methods, items (assets purchased for less than a prescribed amount) may be written-off in full.

    Straight-line method

    According to the fixed instalment method, or cost price method, a fixed amount of depreciation is written-off annually.

    The amount of depreciation will be same every year. It is calculated according to the life span of a fixed asset.

     For example, as prescribed by the South African Revenue Services (SARS) a delivery vehicle’s life span is four (4) years.  The cost price of the asset will be written off over a period of four (4) years. The depreciation can be calculated by dividing the cost price by 4 years, or 25% per annum.

    For example, as prescribed by the South African Revenue Services (SARS) a passenger car’s life span is five (5) years.  The cost price of the asset will be written off over a period of five years. The depreciation can be calculated by dividing the cost price by 5 years, or 20% per annum.

    Diminishing-value method

    If compared to the straight-line (fixed instalment) method, where a fixed amount is written-off over the life span of the fixed asset per year; the diminishing-value method amount of depreciation will differ from year to year and will decrease from year to year.

    According to the diminishing-value method, most of the fixed asset's value will diminish, or be written-off in the first year, and a lesser amount in the second year, and even lesser amounts in the successive years.

    Using the diminishing-value method, depreciation is calculated on the book value (tax value) of the asset at the end of each year. Depreciation is calculated on the purchase (cost) price, less any depreciation already claimed in previous years (accumulated depreciation).

    The book value reduces or diminishes rapidly up to 40% in the first year and a lesser percentage in the second and successive years. This is why it is also referred to as the accelerated method.

    There are two basic formulas that may be used to calculate depreciation when the diminishing balance method is used. These methods are: -

    1. Fixed percentage on the diminishing (reducing) balance method.
    2. The Sum-of-the-digits method.

    Fixed percentage on the diminishing (reducing) - value method

    A fixed percentage of the book value is used to calculate the depreciation, which must be written off each year.

    If the life span of an asset is for instance, five years, and is normally calculated as 20%, the percentage needs to be doubled to 40% in this case. The fixed percentage must be used to calculate the percentage on the reduced (diminished) balance at the beginning of the applicable year.

    According to the straight-line method, the annual depreciation rate over 5 years, is 20% per year.  

    Using the double-declining-balance method, double the annual depreciation rate, from 20% to 40%. The 40% depreciation rate is used to calculate the depreciation for each year on the purchase (cost) price less the accumulated depreciation.

    According to the Diminishing-value method, the book value at the end of the fixed asset’s life span can never be zero. The remaining balance at the end of the fixed asset’s life span (last year of depreciation) is the scrap value.

    This remaining balance, at the end of the fixed asset’s life span, needs to be taken into account when an asset is disposed of (calculate the profit / loss on the sale of the fixed asset).

    Sum-of-the-years-digits method

    If this method is used, the total of the number of years of the life span of the fixed asset is divided by the cost price less, the expected salvage value (trade-in or scrap value) of the fixed asset.

    In the first year, the amount is then multiplied by the highest number of years (last year) of the life span of the fixed asset.

    For the second year, the second last year number of the life span of the asset is used, and so on.

    In the last year, the first year's number is used.

    The sum of the years must be added up.

    For example if the fixed asset is depreciated over a life span of five years, the digits for each of the number of years must be added up.

    For five years we will add up 1+2+3+4 = 10.

    The number 10 is then divided into the cost price of the fixed asset.

    The annual depreciation is calculated on the Purchase (cost) price less the salvage value (trade-in or scrap value) of 10,000, i.e. 40,000 divided by 10 = 4,000. This is the factor, which we will use through the entire life span of the passenger vehicle.

    1. In year 1 - Use 4,000 x 4. This is the very first year in the delivery van's life span.
    2. In year 2 - Use 4,000 x 3. This is the second year in the delivery van's life span.
    3. In year 3 - Use 4,000 x 2. This is the third year in the delivery van's life span.
    4. In year 4 - Use 4,000 x 1. This is the last year in the delivery van's life span.

    Production method

    According to this method, the depreciation is directly related to the use of the asset in the business. Some of the factors used to calculate the life span are the following:

    • The number of units produced.
    • The number of hours spent.
    • The distance travelled (mileage).

    For example, if a printer is purchased for 15500, and has a trade-in or scrap value of 500, and it is expected to print 10000 books, the depreciation will be calculated as follows:

             Price of printer (less the trade-in or scrap value) 15500 – 500 = 15000

    Number of units to be produced during the life span of the printer = 10000

    The depreciation to be written off, will therefore be = 1.50 per unit (or book).

             At the end of the financial year or accounting period, the number of units produced will be multiplied with the factor or 1.50 per unit. For example, if 3000 units (books) were produced during the financial year, the depreciation would be 4500.

    Fixed Assets (Small items) Purchased for Less than a Prescribed Amount

    Amounts of less fixed assets purchased for less than a specified prescribed amount these assets may be written off in full.

    This depreciation amount is prescribed by the legislation, and the amount may differ when the Tax Authorities issues new legislation or practice notes.
