    Transaction cycle

    What is the transaction cycle?

    The transaction cycle in accounting refers to the process of recording financial transactions in a systematic manner. This process typically includes several steps:

    1. Identification: This step involves identifying the financial transactions that need to be recorded. Transactions can include purchases, sales, payments, receipts, and other financial activities.
    2. Recording: Once the transactions have been identified, they are recorded in a journal. This step involves recording the details of each transaction, such as the date, amount, and accounts involved.
    3. Posting: After the transactions have been recorded, they are posted to the appropriate accounts in the general ledger. This step involves transferring the information from the journal to the ledger accounts.
    4. Trial balance: A trial balance is prepared to ensure that the total debits equal the total credits. This step is important to identify any errors that may have been made during the recording or posting process.
    5. Adjusting entries: Adjusting entries are made to update the accounts for any items that were not recorded during the regular transaction cycle. Examples include depreciation, prepaid expenses, and accruals.
    6. Financial statements: Finally, financial statements are prepared to summarize the financial transactions for a specific period. These statements include the income statement and balance sheet. Financial information is the summary of all this financial data into the following two basic reports:
      • Income statement reflecting how well or poor the business or organisation has performed over a specific period.
      • Balance sheet reflecting the business or organisations financial position as at a specific date. 
      • Additional summarised information with respect to certain items and budgets may also be necessary to determine performance or financial position.

    The transaction cycle is an important part of the accounting process as it ensures that all financial transactions are recorded accurately and in a timely manner.

    The transaction cycle and transactions

    Financial data is created through various business transactions, including investments by the owner, purchases of assets, payment of expenses, and sales of goods or services. These transactions impact the accounting equation, which is: 

    Assets = Capital + Liabilities

    This equation must always remain balanced. If assets increase, either capital or liabilities must also increase, and if assets decrease, capital or liabilities must also decrease.

    Each type of transaction affects the equation in a specific manner. For instance, when the owner invests cash, the bank account (asset) and capital increase. If the business purchases an asset with cash, the vehicle (asset) increases and the cash account (asset) decreases. When the business buys assets on credit, the asset account increases and the creditor account (liability) also increases.

    In the same vein, if the business incurs expenses and pays for them with cash, the expense account increases and the cash account (asset) decreases. If expenses are incurred on credit, the expense account increases, and the creditor account (liability) also increases. If the business sells services or goods for cash, the cash account (asset) and the income (sales) account increase. When the business sells goods or services on credit, the debtor account (asset) increases, and the sales account (income) also increases.

    It is essential to comprehend these basic transactions and their impacts on the accounting equation for effective financial management. This enables businesses to track their financial position and performance accurately and make informed decisions based on that information.

    Understanding how these basic transactions affect the accounting equation is essential for proper financial management. It enables businesses to accurately track their financial position and performance, make informed decisions based on that information, and ensure that the accounting equation always remains balanced.
