    Consumable stores on hand : Accounting standards and Accounting method

    The concept of consumable stores on hand is applicable to various types of entities and accounting frameworks, including small and medium entities (SMEs) under both IFRS (full) and IFRS for SMEs. However, the specific treatment and disclosure requirements may vary depending on the accounting framework and the size of the entity.

    Under IFRS (full), consumable stores on hand are typically classified as inventory and are accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. The entity is required to recognize and measure inventory at the lower of cost or net realizable value, and disclose relevant information such as the accounting policies, carrying amounts, and any impairment losses.

    Under IFRS for SMEs, there is a simplified approach to accounting for inventories, which may include consumable stores on hand. The standard provides guidance on the cost formula that can be used to measure inventories, such as the weighted average cost or the first-in, first-out (FIFO) cost. Additionally, the disclosure requirements are less extensive compared to IFRS (full).

    Regarding the accounting method (cash basis or accrual basis), the treatment of consumable stores on hand may differ. The cash basis of accounting recognizes transactions and events only when cash is received or paid, and inventory is not typically recorded as an asset. However, under the accrual basis of accounting, consumable stores on hand would be recognized as inventory and accounted for based on the applicable accounting framework.

    It's important to consult the specific accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction and entity to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of consumable stores on hand.

    What is Consumable stores on hand?

    Consumable stores on hand is considered non-trading stock. Non-trading stock refers to inventory items that are not directly involved in the business's primary trading activities or intended for resale. These items are typically used internally by the business and are consumed in the normal course of operations.

    Consumable stores, such as stationery, office supplies, cleaning materials, or maintenance supplies, fall under the category of non-trading stock. They are not purchased for the purpose of selling to customers but rather for internal use within the business.

    Non-trading stock is distinguished from trading stock, which refers to inventory items that are acquired or produced with the intention of being sold to customers. Trading stock is directly related to the core business activities and represents the goods or products held for sale.

    By categorising Consumable stores on hand as non-trading stock, businesses can differentiate between inventory items used for internal consumption and those held for resale. This classification helps in tracking and managing different types of inventory effectively, ensuring accurate financial reporting and inventory control.

    Is Consumable stores on hand a current asset?

    Consumable stores on hand is generally classified as a current asset on the balance sheet. Current assets are assets that are expected to be converted into cash or used up within a year or the operating cycle of the business, whichever is longer.

    Consumable stores on hand represents inventory items that are readily available for use within the business. These items are expected to be consumed or used up in the normal course of operations within a relatively short period. Examples of consumable stores include office supplies, cleaning materials, or maintenance supplies.

    As a current asset, Consumable stores on hand is reported on the balance sheet under the assets section, typically within the category of inventory or stock. It reflects the value of the inventory items that are not intended for sale but rather used internally by the business.

    It's important to note that the specific classification of assets may vary depending on the accounting framework or company's accounting policies. Therefore, it's advisable to refer to the company's financial statements or consult with an accountant for the accurate classification of Consumable stores on hand in a specific context.

    Adjustments - Consumable Stores on Hand -  how would this be recorded?

    At the end of each financial year, the business should conduct a physical count of its consumable stores on hand and adjust its accounts accordingly. This adjustment is necessary to accurately reflect the value of the assets on the balance sheet.

    If there are any consumable stores on hand that have not been used or consumed by the end of the financial year, they will be carried forward as assets into the next financial year. Conversely, if any of these items are used up or consumed during the year, they should be recognized as an expense in the income statement.

    The journal type

    The recording of the consumable stores on hand would typically be done in the general journal. The general journal is used to record transactions and adjustments that don't fit into the specific journals like sales journal or purchases journal.

    In the general journal, you can create a separate entry specifically for the adjustment of consumable stores on hand. This entry would include the appropriate debit and credit accounts to reflect the changes in the asset balance and any associated expenses or gains.

    Alternatively, some businesses may choose to create a separate journal called the "Adjustment journal" or "Inventory adjustment journal" specifically for recording adjustments related to inventory, including consumable stores on hand. This provides a dedicated journal for capturing these adjustments and helps in organizing the accounting records.

    The choice of using the general journal or a specific adjustment journal may vary depending on the preferences and accounting practices of the business. Both methods are acceptable as long as the adjustments are properly recorded and documented.

    The process

    To record the adjustment for Consumable stores on hand, you can follow these steps:

    1. Determine the value of consumable stores on hand: Conduct a physical count or inventory valuation of the consumable stores available in your business. This will give you the total value of the inventory items that are used up in the normal course of operations.
    2. Create a Consumable Stores on Hand account: In your chart of accounts, set up a new asset account specifically for tracking Consumable stores on hand. You can name it "Consumable stores on hand" or something similar. Assign a suitable account number based on your accounting system's numbering convention. 
    3. Record the adjustment: Make a journal entry to record the adjustment in your accounting system. Debit the Consumable stores on hand account for the value of the consumable stores on hand. This entry recognizes the value of the inventory items that are readily available for use. On the credit side, you can select an applicable expense account that corresponds to the type of consumable items you have, such as Stationery, Office supplies, Cleaning materials, or other appropriate accounts.
