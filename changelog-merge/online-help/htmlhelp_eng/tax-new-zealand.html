    Taxes in New Zealand

    New Zealand (NZ) - Tax system key points

    As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, here are some key points about the tax system in New Zealand (NZ):

    1. Goods and Services Tax (GST):
      • GST is a broad-based consumption tax applied to most goods and services in New Zealand.
      • The standard GST rate is 15% on the value of goods and services consumed.
      • Certain goods and services, such as financial services and residential rent, are exempt from GST.
    1. Income Tax:
      • New Zealand has a progressive income tax system, with higher income earners paying higher tax rates.
      • Individual taxpayers are subject to income tax on their earnings from various sources, including employment, business, and investments.
      • The tax rates and income thresholds for income tax can change annually in the government's budget.
    1. Capital Gains Tax (CGT):
      • As of my last update, New Zealand did not have a comprehensive capital gains tax on all assets.
      • However, gains on certain types of property, such as residential rental properties, may be subject to income tax.
    1. Accident Compensation Levy (ACC):
      • The ACC levy is applied to fund New Zealand's no-fault accident compensation scheme.
      • It is applied to most income and collected alongside income tax.
    1. Customs and Excise:
      • Customs duties and excise duties are applied to imported and locally produced goods, as well as specific products like alcohol, tobacco, and petroleum.
    1. Pay As You Earn (PAYE):
      • PAYE is the system by which employers deduct income tax from employees' salaries and remit it to the Inland Revenue Department (IRD).
    1. KiwiSaver:
      • KiwiSaver is a voluntary retirement savings scheme that includes tax incentives for contributing to a KiwiSaver account.
      • Contributions to KiwiSaver may be deducted from an individual's taxable income.
    1. Fringe Benefit Tax (FBT):
      • FBT is applied to non-cash benefits provided by employers to employees as part of their remuneration package.
      • Employers are responsible for paying FBT on these benefits at a rate equivalent to the top marginal tax rate.
    1. Stamp Duty:
      • As of my last update, New Zealand does not have a comprehensive stamp duty on documents or transactions. However, there may be specific duties or levies in certain circumstances.
    1. Land Transfer Tax:
      • In New Zealand, land transfer tax is known as "Land Transfer Tax" or "LTT."
      • LTT is applied to the transfer of land ownership and is payable by the purchaser.
      • The LTT rates and thresholds can vary based on factors such as the value of the property and the type of transaction.
    1. Provisional Tax:
      • Provisional tax is a system for paying income tax in instalments throughout the year.
      • Individuals and businesses estimate their income for the year and make payments based on these estimates.
      • The actual tax liability is reconciled when the annual tax return is filed.
    1. Gift Duty:
      • As of ChatGPT's last update, New Zealand abolished gift duty in 2011.
      • Previously, gift duty was applied to certain gifts or transfers of property made during a person's lifetime.
    1. Tax Filing and Compliance:
      • Individuals and businesses are required to register for tax with the Inland Revenue Department (IRD) and comply with their tax obligations.
      • Tax returns are typically filed annually, and payment due dates may vary depending on the type of tax.

    Please note that tax laws and regulations can change over time, so it's important to consult the Inland Revenue Department (IRD) or a tax professional for the most up-to-date and specific information related to the tax system in New Zealand.

    In addition to taxes in New Zealand - what is local and provincial taxes and charges?

    In New Zealand, local and provincial taxes and charges are levied by local authorities and regional councils, respectively. These taxes and charges are separate from the national taxes imposed by the central government. Local and regional governments use these revenues to fund various services and projects at the local and regional levels. The specific taxes and charges can vary depending on the region. Here are some common examples of local and provincial taxes and charges in New Zealand:

    Local Government Taxes and Charges:

    • Property Rates: Property rates, also known as property taxes, are levied by local authorities (city or district councils) on real estate properties. The rates are based on the value of the property and are used to fund local services and infrastructure.
    • Water Rates: Local councils may charge water rates to cover the cost of providing water supply and wastewater services to properties.
    • Sewerage Rates: Local councils may charge sewerage rates to cover the cost of providing sewerage and wastewater services.
    • Rubbish and Recycling Collection Fees: Some local authorities charge fees for rubbish and recycling collection services.
    • Licensing Fees: Businesses may be required to obtain licenses from local authorities, and fees are charged for these licenses.
    • Building Consent Fees: Local councils charge fees for processing building consent applications for construction or renovation projects.

    Provincial (Regional Council) Taxes and Charges:

    • Regional Fuel Tax: Some regional councils have the authority to impose a regional fuel tax on petrol and diesel to fund transportation projects in their regions.
    • Regional Rates: Regional councils may levy rates on properties within their regions to fund regional services and projects.
    • Consent Fees: Regional councils may charge fees for resource consents related to activities that have environmental impacts.
    • Water Quality and Resource Management Charges: Some regional councils may charge levies or fees related to water quality monitoring and resource management.

    It's important to note that the specific local and provincial taxes and charges can vary depending on the region and local government policies. Each local authority and regional council has its own tax and charge structure. Businesses and individuals should check with their local council and regional council to understand the local and provincial taxes and charges that apply in their specific area.

    Additionally, tax laws and charges may change over time, so it's essential to stay informed about any updates or changes in the local and regional tax landscape. Consulting with local authorities and professionals can help ensure compliance with local and provincial tax obligations in New Zealand.
