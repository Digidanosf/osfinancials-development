    Australia - Chart of Accounts - Australian Accounting Standards

    What is a suggested Australian Chart of Accounts is based on the Australian Accounting Standards and is used by businesses in Australia?

    The suggested Chart of Accounts for a business in Australia based on the Australian Accounting Standards typically includes the following main categories:

    1. Assets: This category includes accounts related to the resources that a business owns or controls, such as cash, accounts receivable, inventory, and property, plant, and equipment.
    2. Liabilities: This category includes accounts related to the obligations that a business owes to others, such as accounts payable, loans, and other forms of debt.
    3. Equity: This category includes accounts related to the ownership interest in a business, such as share capital and retained earnings.
    4. Revenue: This category includes accounts related to the income earned by a business, such as sales revenue, interest income, and rental income.
    5. Expenses: This category includes accounts related to the costs incurred by a business in generating revenue, such as cost of goods sold, operating expenses, and interest expenses.

    Within each of these main categories, the accounts are usually identified by a four-digit code, with the first two digits indicating the category and the last two digits indicating the specific account within the category.

    The specific accounts included in the Chart of Accounts can vary depending on the nature of the business and its financial reporting requirements. It is important to consult with an accountant or bookkeeper to determine the most appropriate Chart of Accounts for your business.

    What is the recommended financial categories and account groups for accounts in the Australia Chart of Accounts?

    The Australian Chart of Accounts is a standardized list of financial accounts that businesses in Australia can use to organize their financial transactions. Here are some recommended financial categories and account groups that can be used in the Australian Chart of Accounts:

    1. Assets: This category includes all the resources that a business owns and controls. It includes subcategories such as:
      • Current assets (e.g. cash, accounts receivable, inventory)
      • Non-current assets (e.g. property, plant, and equipment)
      • Intangible assets (e.g. patents, trademarks, goodwill)
    1. Liabilities: This category includes all the financial obligations that a business owes to others. It includes subcategories such as:
      • Current liabilities (e.g. accounts payable, taxes payable, short-term loans)
      • Non-current liabilities (e.g. long-term loans, bonds, mortgages)
    1. Equity: This category includes the owners' investment in the business. It includes subcategories such as:
      • Share capital (e.g. common shares, preferred shares)
      • Retained earnings (e.g. accumulated profits or losses)
    1. Income: This category includes all the revenue earned by the business. It includes subcategories such as:
      • Sales revenue (e.g. revenue from the sale of goods or services)
      • Other income (e.g. interest income, rental income)
    1. Expenses: This category includes all the costs incurred by the business. It includes subcategories such as:
      • Cost of sales (e.g. the cost of producing or acquiring the goods sold)
      • Operating expenses (e.g. rent, salaries, utilities)
      • Depreciation and amortization (e.g. the cost of using up fixed assets and intangible assets over time)

    These financial categories and account groups can be further customized and refined to suit the specific needs of each business. It is important to consult with a professional accountant or bookkeeper to ensure that the chart of accounts is set up correctly and accurately reflects the business's financial transactions.

    What is business activity statements (BAS) ?

    Business Activity Statements (BAS) are a reporting requirement for businesses in Australia. They are used to report and pay various tax obligations to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO). BAS statements are typically lodged on a monthly, quarterly, or annual basis, depending on the business's turnover and reporting obligations.

    The BAS includes information about the business's income, expenses, and GST (Goods and Services Tax) collected and paid. It covers various taxes and obligations, including:

    1. Goods and Services Tax (GST): Businesses registered for GST must report the GST they have collected from sales and claim GST credits on eligible business expenses.
    2. Pay as You Go (PAYG) Withholding: This involves reporting and remitting the amounts withheld from employees' wages for income tax purposes.
    3. Pay as You Go (PAYG) Instalments: Businesses making regular payments towards their expected income tax liability may need to report and pay PAYG instalments.
    4. Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT): If a business provides fringe benefits to employees or associates, they may need to report and pay FBT.
    5. Wine Equalization Tax (WET): Applicable to businesses involved in the production or wholesale of wine.
    6. Luxury Car Tax (LCT): This applies to businesses that sell or import luxury cars.

    By lodging a BAS, businesses ensure compliance with their tax obligations and provide the ATO with accurate financial information. The frequency of BAS lodgement depends on the business's turnover, with larger businesses typically required to lodge on a monthly basis.


    Is there a chart of accounts based on Business Activity Statements (BAS) ?

    The Business Activity Statement (BAS) is a reporting requirement in Australia that encompasses various tax obligations. While the BAS provides a structure for reporting and paying taxes, it doesn't prescribe a specific chart of accounts.

    The chart of accounts is a financial organizational tool that helps categorize and record transactions within a business. It provides a systematic framework for classifying and tracking financial activities. The specific accounts included in a chart of accounts can vary depending on the nature of the business, its industry, and reporting requirements.

    However, when preparing the financial statements for BAS reporting, businesses typically utilize a chart of accounts that aligns with the relevant accounting standards and regulations. This includes accounts for income, expenses, assets, liabilities, and equity, which are necessary for generating accurate financial information for BAS reporting purposes.

    While there isn't a specific chart of accounts prescribed by the BAS, businesses can customize their chart of accounts to ensure compliance with the reporting requirements and capture the necessary information for the BAS lodgement. It's essential to consult with accounting professionals or refer to accounting standards and regulations specific to your jurisdiction to design a suitable chart of accounts that meets the BAS reporting needs.

    What accounts is suggested for payroll in Australia?

    In Australia, the following accounts are commonly used to record payroll transactions in the chart of accounts:

    1. Salaries and wages: This account is used to record the gross wages and salaries paid to employees.
    2. Employee benefits expense: This account is used to record the cost of employee benefits such as superannuation contributions, sick leave, annual leave, and long service leave.
    3. Payroll tax expense: This account is used to record the employer's share of payroll taxes, which include taxes such as the Payroll Tax, the Superannuation Guarantee Charge (SGC), and the Medicare Levy Surcharge.
    4. PAYG withholding payable: This account is used to record the amount of tax withheld from employee wages, which must be paid to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) on behalf of the employee.
    5. Superannuation payable: This account is used to record the employer's contributions to employee superannuation funds.
    6. Employee loan payable: This account is used to record any loans or advances made to employees.
    7. Employee deduction payable: This account is used to record deductions from employee wages such as union dues, charitable donations, or voluntary insurance contributions.
    8. Accrued payroll: This account is used to record any payroll expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid, such as salaries and wages for the current pay period that will be paid in the following pay period.
    9. Bank account: This account is used to record the transactions related to the company's payroll bank account, including deposits and withdrawals for payroll-related expenses.

    It is important to tailor the chart of accounts to the specific needs of the business and to ensure compliance with local regulations. Consulting with a professional accountant or payroll provider can be helpful in setting up an effective payroll system and chart of accounts for Australian businesses.
