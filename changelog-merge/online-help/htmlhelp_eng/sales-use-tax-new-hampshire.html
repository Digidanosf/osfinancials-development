    New Hampshire (NH) - Tax system key points

    As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, here are some key points about the tax system in New Hampshire:

    1. No Sales Tax: New Hampshire does not have a statewide sales tax. This means there is no sales tax imposed on goods or services sold within the state. As a result, retail purchases in New Hampshire are generally tax-free at the state level.
    2. No Use Tax: Similarly, New Hampshire does not have a statewide use tax. Use tax is typically applied when residents or businesses purchase taxable items from out-of-state sellers who do not collect the state's sales tax. In New Hampshire, there is no use tax applicable to such transactions.
    3. No Individual Income Tax: New Hampshire is one of the few states that do not impose an individual income tax on wages and salaries. However, it does tax interest and dividends earned by residents at a flat rate.
    4. No General Corporate Income Tax: New Hampshire does not have a general corporate income tax on profits earned by corporations. However, it does impose a Business Profits Tax (BPT) and a Business Enterprise Tax (BET) on certain businesses.
    5. Property Taxes: New Hampshire imposes property taxes on real property, such as land and buildings. Property tax rates are determined at the local level and vary by municipality.
    6. Meals and Rooms Tax: New Hampshire imposes a Meals and Rooms Tax on prepared meals, beverages, and accommodations provided by hotels, restaurants, and other establishments.
    7. No Estate Tax: New Hampshire does not have its own estate tax. Additionally, it does not have a "pick-up" tax, meaning it does not collect a portion of the federal estate tax paid by the estate.
    8. No Vehicle Sales Tax: New Hampshire does not impose a sales tax on vehicle purchases, making it a tax-free state for buying cars.
    9. Keno Tax: New Hampshire has a tax on Keno, a type of lottery game.
    10. Local Taxes: While New Hampshire does not have a broad-based state sales tax, some local jurisdictions in the state may impose certain local taxes or fees.

    It's important to note that tax laws are subject to change, and it's recommended to consult the New Hampshire Department of Revenue Administration or a tax professional for the most up-to-date and specific information related to taxes in New Hampshire.
