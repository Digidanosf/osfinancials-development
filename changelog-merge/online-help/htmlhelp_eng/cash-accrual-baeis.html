  1. Month-end and year-end procedures

    Cash basis and accrual basis

    Accounting method (accrual basis or cash basis)

    It's important to note that the choice of accounting method (accrual basis or cash basis) depends on various factors, including legal requirements, business needs, and financial reporting purposes. Always consult with an accounting professional or refer to the accounting standards applicable to your jurisdiction for guidance on the appropriate accounting treatment based on your specific circumstances.

    It's important to choose the appropriate accounting method (either accrual basis or cash basis) based on the nature and size of your business, as well as any legal or regulatory requirements. Generally, larger businesses and entities are required to use the accrual basis accounting method, while smaller businesses may opt for the cash basis method for simplicity and ease of recording transactions.

    Common year-end adjustments for both cash basis and accrual basis accounting methods

    When using the accrual basis accounting method, year-end adjustments are necessary to ensure that the financial statements reflect the actual income and expenses for the accounting period. Here are some common year-end adjustments for both cash basis and accrual basis accounting methods:

    Cash basis accounting method:

    1. Adjusting entries for depreciation: Depreciation is a non-cash expense that needs to be recorded for fixed assets, such as buildings and equipment.
    2. Adjusting entries for bad debts: If you have any outstanding debts that are unlikely to be paid, you need to write them off as bad debts.
    3. Recording prepaid expenses: If you have made payments for expenses that will benefit future accounting periods, such as insurance premiums, these need to be recorded as prepaid expenses.
    4. Recording unearned income: If you have received payments for goods or services that you have not yet provided, these need to be recorded as unearned income.

    Accrual basis accounting method:

    1. Recording accrued income: If you have provided goods or services but have not yet received payment, you need to record this as accrued income.
    2. Recording accrued expenses: If you have received goods or services but have not yet paid for them, you need to record this as accrued expenses.
    3. Recording prepaid expenses: Similar to cash basis accounting, you need to record any payments made for expenses that will benefit future accounting periods as prepaid expenses.
    4. Recording unearned income: Similar to cash basis accounting, you need to record any payments received for goods or services that have not yet been provided as unearned income.

    It's important to note that each of these adjustments needs to be made before performing a year-end procedure in the osFinancials to ensure that the financial statements are accurate.

    Is the month-end and year-end procedures and principles the same for GAAP and IFRS?

    The month-end and year-end procedures and principles are generally similar under both Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) and International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS), as both sets of standards require companies to prepare financial statements at regular intervals and to adhere to certain accounting principles and practices. 

    However, there may be some differences in specific requirements and reporting standards between GAAP and IFRS, depending on the country or region where the company is based. It is important for companies to understand and comply with the relevant accounting standards and regulations in their jurisdiction.

    Are provision for bad debts, prepaid expenses, accrued expenses, provision for accrued expenses, income received in advance, and accrued income short-term adjustments?

    Yes, provision for bad debts, prepaid expenses, accrued expenses, provision for accrued expenses, income received in advance, and accrued income are typically considered short-term adjustments. These adjustments are made to ensure that the financial statements for a particular accounting period accurately reflect the expenses and revenues that belong to that period. They usually relate to transactions or events that occur during the current accounting period but whose impact will be felt in future periods. These adjustments are usually reversed or adjusted in the following accounting period when the related transactions or events are fully realized.

    After these adjustments were made for a financial year whet entries is necessary for the next financial year?

    After adjustments have been made for the previous financial year, you would typically start the next financial year with opening entries. These opening entries are necessary to bring forward the balances of certain accounts from the previous financial year to the new financial year.

    For example, the balance of the retained earnings account from the previous financial year would be brought forward as the opening balance for the new financial year. Similarly, any other account balances that need to be carried forward to the new financial year would be entered as opening entries.

    The opening entries would be recorded in the general journal and posted to the relevant ledger accounts to update the balances in those accounts. Once the opening entries have been posted, you can start entering new transactions for the new financial year.

    Are these adjustments accrued income, accrued expenses, prepaid expenses, unearned income doe annually at year end or can they be done on a monthly, quarterly or six months basis?

    The timing of these adjustments (accrued income, accrued expenses, prepaid expenses, unearned income) may vary depending on the specific accounting policies of a company and the frequency of their accounting periods.

    For example, some companies may choose to adjust their prepaid expenses or unearned income on a monthly basis, while others may do it on a quarterly or annual basis. Similarly, accrued expenses or accrued income may also be adjusted on a regular basis depending on the accounting period.

    However, it is common practice to ensure that all such adjustments are made at least once a year, during the year-end procedures, to ensure that the financial statements accurately reflect the company's financial position and performance.
