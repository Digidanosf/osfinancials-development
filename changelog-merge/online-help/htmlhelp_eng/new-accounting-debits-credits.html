    Debits and Credits

    What are debits and credits?

    Debits and credits are the two basic and opposite accounting entries used to record financial transactions in double-entry bookkeeping.

    In a financial transaction, there is always an equal amount of debits and credits. Debits represent an increase in assets or a decrease in liabilities or capital, while credits represent a decrease in assets or an increase in liabilities or capital.

    For example, if a company purchases inventory for cash, the entry would be a debit to inventory (an increase in the company's assets) and a credit to cash (a decrease in the company's assets).

    The following example illustrates debits and credits:

    Debit

    Credit

    The account receiving the value is debited. 

    The account giving the value is credited.

    On 1 March your business sells goods to the value of 100 to a customer, Tom on credit and issues a sales invoice for the transaction:

    1. On 1 March the customer receives the goods to the value of 100 from the business.

    1. On 1 March the business gives the goods to the value of 100 to the customer.

    The customer account is debited and the sales account is credited.

    What happens if the customer settles his account with the business on 20 March? Your business will issue a receipt of 100 as a source document.

    1. On 20 March Tom gives the money to the value of 100 to the business.

    1. On 20 March the business receives the money to the value of 100 from Tom.

    You will debit the bank and credit the customer account.

    Only transactions between the business and another entity (i.e. the purchasing of a computer for office use) should be recorded. 

    Personal transactions made by the business owner should not be included in the business's accounting records. 

    This is because the business and the owner are considered separate entities in the eyes of the law.
