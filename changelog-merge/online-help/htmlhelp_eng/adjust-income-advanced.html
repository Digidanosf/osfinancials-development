    Income received in advance / Unearned revenue / Deferred revenue : Accounting standards and Accounting method

    The concept of income received in advance, also known as unearned revenue or deferred revenue, is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following IFRS (full) or IFRS for SMEs. The treatment of income received in advance is generally consistent across the two frameworks.

    Under IFRS (full), income received in advance is recognized as a liability and is accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. It represents cash received from customers or clients for goods or services that are yet to be provided. The liability is recognized because the revenue has not been earned at the time of receipt. As the goods or services are delivered or performed, the liability is reduced, and the corresponding revenue is recognized.

    Similarly, under IFRS for SMEs, there are specific provisions for the recognition, measurement, and presentation of income received in advance. The standard provides guidance on how to account for unearned revenue, including when it should be recognized as a liability and how it should be measured. The disclosure requirements may also differ from IFRS (full), being more streamlined and tailored to the needs of SMEs.

    In terms of the accounting method, the treatment of income received in advance would also differ between the cash basis and accrual basis of accounting. Under the cash basis, income is recognized when cash is received, and therefore, income received in advance would be recognized as revenue immediately upon receipt. In contrast, under the accrual basis, income received in advance is recognized as a liability and is gradually recognized as revenue over time as the goods or services are provided.

    It's important to consult the specific accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction and entity to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of income received in advance based on the chosen accounting framework.

    What is income received in advance?

    Income received in advance, also known as unearned revenue or deferred revenue, refers to payments received from customers or clients for goods or services that have not yet been provided. It represents a liability for the business because the revenue has been received in advance of earning it.

    When a business receives income in advance, it has an obligation to deliver the goods or services at a later date. Until that obligation is fulfilled, the amount received is classified as a liability on the balance sheet.

    Examples of income received in advance include:

    1. Subscriptions: When a customer pays for a subscription service upfront, such as an annual magazine subscription or a software subscription.
    2. Rent: If a tenant pays rent for several months in advance, the portion of rent that corresponds to future periods is considered income received in advance.
    3. Service contracts: When a business enters into a service contract with a customer and receives payment upfront before providing the services.
    4. Membership fees: Membership-based organizations that collect fees in advance from their members.

    To account for income received in advance, the business initially records it as a liability on the balance sheet. As the goods or services are provided over time, the liability is gradually recognized as revenue in the income statement. The amount recognized as revenue is based on the portion of the goods or services delivered or consumed.

    Proper accounting for income received in advance ensures that revenue is recognized in the appropriate accounting periods, aligning with the matching principle in accrual accounting.

    What is the batch type for Income received in advance?

    Accrual or year-end adjustments are typically recorded in the general journal. The general journal is a catch-all journal where transactions that do not fit into specific specialised journals are recorded.

    While it is possible to create a separate batch or journal type such as "Adjustments" specifically for accrual or year-end adjustments, it is not a common practice. The general journal is often used for such adjustments as it allows for flexibility in recording various types of transactions and adjustments that may arise during the accounting period.

    The "Adjustments" batch type or journal can be used to record transactions related to income received in advance instead of using the general journal. The "Adjustments" batch type or journal provides flexibility for recording various types of adjustments and can be customized to suit your specific needs.

    Adjustments - Income received in advance - how would this be recorded?

    Adjustments - Income received in advance or unearned revenue refers to a category of adjustments made in accounting to account for revenue that has been received by a business in advance but has not yet been earned. It represents an obligation for the business to deliver goods or services in the future in order to fulfil the revenue that has been received upfront.

    When a business receives payment from a customer before providing the goods or services, it is considered unearned revenue or income received in advance. The business has a liability to fulfil its obligation to the customer by delivering the promised goods or services.

    The Adjustments - Income received in advance or unearned revenue entry is typically recorded at the time of receiving the payment. It involves debiting the liability account (e.g., Unearned Revenue or Income Received in Advance) and crediting the cash or bank account to reflect the inflow of funds.

    As the business fulfils its obligation and earns the revenue, the unearned revenue is gradually recognized as revenue in the income statement. This is done through adjusting entries, which involve debiting the Unearned Revenue account and crediting the revenue account corresponding to the goods or services provided.

    By making adjustments for income received in advance or unearned revenue, the financial statements accurately reflect the revenue that has been earned during a specific accounting period. It ensures that the recognition of revenue aligns with the actual delivery of goods or services, providing a more accurate representation of the business's financial performance.

    To record the adjustment for income received in advance, follow these steps:

    1. Determine the amount of income received in advance: Identify the total amount of income that has been received from customers but has not yet been earned. This could include prepayments for services or goods to be delivered in the future.
    2. Create an "Income Received in Advance" account: Establish a liability account in your chart of accounts called "Income Received in Advance" or a similar name. Assign it a suitable account number based on your accounting system's numbering convention. This account represents the amount of unearned revenue.

    Sub-accounts: Creating sub-accounts under the main account "Income received in Advance" or "Unearned revenue" in the current liability section of the balance sheet is a common and advisable practice. It provides a structured approach to track and categorize different types of unearned income or revenue received in advance.

    By creating sub-accounts, you can better classify and differentiate the sources of unearned income. This allows for more detailed reporting and analysis of the specific types of unearned income, such as subscriptions, rent received in advance, or any other relevant categories for your business.

    Using sub-accounts also enhances the clarity and transparency of your financial statements. It helps stakeholders understand the composition and magnitude of the unearned income and its impact on the overall financial position of the business.

    When creating sub-accounts, it is important to follow accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction. These standards provide specific requirements and recommendations for the presentation and disclosure of unearned revenue or income received in advance in financial statements.

    Is Adjustments - Income received in advance (Unearned revenue) only applicable to the accrual basis accounting method or does it apply to the cash basis accounting methods?

    The concept of "Income received in advance" or "Unearned revenue" generally applies to the accrual basis accounting method.

    Under the accrual basis, revenue is recognised when it is earned, regardless of when the payment is received. If a customer pays in advance for goods or services that will be provided in the future, the payment is recorded as a liability (unearned revenue) until the revenue is earned.

    In contrast, the cash basis accounting method recognises revenue when cash is received. With cash basis accounting, there is no concept of unearned revenue because revenue is only recorded when the cash is actually received.

    Therefore, the specific account "Adjustments - Income received in advance" would typically be applicable to the accrual basis accounting method, where there is a need to record and track unearned revenue. In cash basis accounting, such adjustments may not be necessary as revenue is recognized upon receipt of cash.

    It's important to note that the choice of accounting method (accrual basis or cash basis) depends on various factors, including legal requirements, business needs, and financial reporting purposes. Always consult with an accounting professional or refer to the accounting standards applicable to your jurisdiction for guidance on the appropriate accounting treatment based on your specific circumstances.
