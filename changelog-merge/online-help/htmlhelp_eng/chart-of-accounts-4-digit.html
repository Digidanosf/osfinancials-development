    Example and notes for a 4-digit Chart of Accounts

    Control accounts when creating a Set of Books from scratch

    In osFinancials, when creating a Set of Books from scratch using the "Customize your own Set of Books (Advanced)?" option in the "Creation wizard", you have the flexibility to define your chart of accounts according to your specific needs and requirements.

    As part of this customization process, you will have the opportunity to set the number of main digits for your accounts, ranging from 3 to 8 digits. Additionally, you will specify the account numbers for three main control accounts: the Creditors control account, Debtors control account, and Retained earnings account.

    1. The Creditors control account is a general ledger account that represents the total amount owed to the company by its creditors or suppliers. It serves as a summary account for all individual creditor accounts and helps track the company's outstanding payables.
    2. The Debtors control account, on the other hand, is a general ledger account that represents the total amount owed to the company by its debtors or customers. It acts as a summary account for all individual debtor accounts and aids in monitoring the company's receivables.
    3. The Retained earnings account, reflects the cumulative net earnings or profits of the company that have been retained and reinvested in the business. It represents the portion of equity that is derived from the company's profitability and serves as a financial record of the accumulated profits over time.

    By defining these main control accounts during the Set of Books creation process, you can structure your chart of accounts and design the account numbering system that aligns with your specific accounting practices and reporting requirements.

    G2000-000

    Creditor's control

    The Creditor's control account, also known as the Accounts payable control account or Creditor's control ledger account, is a general ledger account used to track and summarize the total amount owed to creditors or suppliers by a business.

    It represents the outstanding balances of individual creditor accounts in the accounts payable subsidiary ledger. The subsidiary ledger contains detailed information about each specific creditor, such as their name, contact details, and transaction history.

    The purpose of the Creditor's control account is to provide a summary of all the amounts owed to creditors at any given time. It helps businesses monitor their payable balances, track payment due dates, and ensure accurate financial reporting.

    Transactions related to purchases on credit from suppliers, invoice payments, and adjustments are recorded in both the subsidiary ledger (creditor accounts) and the Creditor's control account in the general ledger. The balances of individual creditor accounts are periodically reconciled with the balance in the Creditor's control account to ensure accuracy.

    The Creditor's control account is an important component of a business's financial records, enabling effective management of accounts payable and ensuring timely payment to creditors.


    G1100-000

    Debtor's control

    The Debtor's control account, also known as the Accounts receivable control account or Debtor's control ledger, is a general ledger account used to track and summarize the total amount owed by debtors or customers to a business.

    Similar to the Creditor's control account, the Debtor's control account represents the outstanding balances of individual debtor accounts in the accounts receivable subsidiary ledger. The subsidiary ledger contains detailed information about each specific debtor, including their name, contact details, and transaction history.

    The primary purpose of the Debtor's control account is to provide a summary of all the amounts owed by customers at any given time. It helps businesses monitor their receivable balances, track payment due dates, and ensure accurate financial reporting.

    Transactions related to sales on credit to customers, receipt of customer payments, and adjustments are recorded in both the subsidiary ledger (debtor accounts) and the Debtor's Control Account in the general ledger. The balances of individual debtor accounts are periodically reconciled with the balance in the Debtor's Control Account to ensure accuracy.

    The Debtor's Control Account plays a crucial role in managing a business's accounts receivable, allowing for effective tracking of customer balances and timely collection of payments. It provides valuable information for cash flow management, credit control, and financial analysis.


    G3900-000

    Retained earnings

    The Retained earnings account, also known as the Accumulated earnings account or Earnings surplus, is a general ledger account that represents the cumulative net earnings or profits of a company that have been retained and reinvested in the business rather than distributed to shareholders as dividends.

    Retained earnings are generated from the company's net income, which is calculated by deducting expenses from revenues. Instead of distributing all of the net income to shareholders, a portion of it is retained within the company. This retained portion is added to the Retained earnings account.

    The Retained earnings account serves as a financial record of the company's accumulated profits over time. It reflects the cumulative value of the company's retained earnings from its inception until the present. The account is adjusted at the end of each accounting period to reflect changes in net income and any dividends declared.

    The Retained earnings account is an important component of the company's equity section on the balance sheet. It represents the portion of equity that is derived from the company's profitability and retained within the business. Retained earnings can be used for various purposes, such as reinvesting in the business, funding expansion projects, reducing debt, or distributing dividends in the future.

    The balance in the Retained earnings account is carried forward from one accounting period to the next. It serves as a source of funding for future growth and helps to enhance the company's financial stability and flexibility. The account also provides insights into the historical profitability and financial performance of the company.

    Basic outline for a 4-digit main accounts chart of accounts

    The outline for the chart of accounts is a general framework that can be applicable to various accounting systems, including IFRS for SMEs. It covers different categories of accounts that are commonly found in financial reporting. While the account ranges and specific names may vary depending on the company and reporting requirements, the general structure remains similar.

    The suggested outline includes:

    1. Asset accounts (1000 - 1999): Accounts representing various types of assets owned or controlled by the business, such as cash, receivables, inventory, and fixed assets. They include cash, short-term investments, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses, property, plant, and equipment, intangible assets, investments, and other assets.
    2. Liability accounts (2000 - 2999): Accounts representing the financial obligations or debts of the business to external parties, including payables, borrowings, and accrued liabilities.  They include accounts payable, accrued expenses, short-term borrowings, long-term borrowings, deferred revenue, and other current and long-term liabilities.
    3. Equity accounts (3000 - 3999): Accounts representing the equity portion of the company's capital structure, depending on the type of business ownership (sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, LLC). For a sole proprietorship, they typically include owner's capital, owner's drawings, and retained earnings. In a partnership, they include partner's capital accounts, partner's drawings, partner's profit and loss allocation, and retained earnings. In a corporation, they include common stock, preferred stock, additional paid-in capital, retained earnings, and treasury stock. In a limited liability company (LLC), they include member's capital accounts, member's drawings, member's profit and loss allocation, and retained earnings.
    4. Revenue accounts (4000 - 4999): Accounts representing the income or revenue generated by the company's primary business activities. They include operating revenue, which is directly related to the core operations, and other revenue, which includes income from sources incidental or peripheral to the main operations.
    5. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) accounts (5000 - 5999): Accounts reflecting the direct costs associated with producing or acquiring the goods or services sold by the company. The specific account names may vary, such as Cost of Sales (COS) or Cost of Services Rendered (SOSR), depending on the nature of the business.
    6. Expense accounts (6000 - 7999): Accounts representing the operating expenses incurred by the business in its day-to-day operations. They include wages, rent, utilities, materials, and other expenses directly related to the revenue-generating activities.
    7. Non-operating revenue accounts (8000 - 8999): Accounts recording income or revenue generated by the business that is not directly related to its core operations. It includes sources of income that are incidental, peripheral, or non-recurring in nature.
    8. Non-operating expenses accounts (9000 - 9999): Accounts reflecting expenses incurred by the business that are not directly related to its core operations. They typically arise from activities outside the normal operations and are separate from operating expenses.

    This summary is a general outline of the typical accounts found within each range. 

    It's important to customize and adjust this chart of accounts and reporting framework to suit your business's specific requirements and comply with the relevant reporting standards, tax regulations, and accounting frameworks in your jurisdiction and country. Factors such as your business ownership type, industry, and other specific needs should be considered when adapting the chart of accounts and reporting framework to ensure accurate and relevant financial reporting for your business.

    • Asset accounts (1000 - 1999): Assets are economic resources that are owned or controlled by a business or individual and are expected to provide future benefits. In accounting, assets are recorded on a balance sheet and represent the value of what an entity owns. Assets can take various forms, including cash, inventory, property, equipment, investments, and intellectual property. 

    Asset accounts in the range of 1000-1999 typically represent various types of assets on a company's balance sheet. Here's a concise summary of these asset accounts:

      1. Cash and Cash Equivalents (1000-1099): This account includes cash on hand, demand deposits, and highly liquid short-term investments that can be readily converted into cash.
      2. Short-term Investments (1100-1199): These accounts cover investments in securities and other financial instruments that are expected to be converted into cash within a relatively short period, typically one year or less.
      3. Accounts Receivable (1200-1299): This account represents amounts owed to the company by its customers or clients for goods sold or services rendered on credit.
      4. Inventory (1300-1399): This account includes the cost of raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods held by the company for sale or production.
      5. Prepaid Expenses (1400-1499): This account represents expenses that have been paid in advance but have not yet been incurred or used. Examples include prepaid insurance, prepaid rent, or prepaid subscriptions.
      6. Property, Plant, and Equipment (1500-1599): This account encompasses the tangible assets used in the business, such as land, buildings, machinery, vehicles, and furniture.
      7. Intangible Assets (1600-1699): These accounts capture non-physical assets, such as patents, copyrights, trademarks, goodwill, and intellectual property, which provide long-term value to the business.
      8. Investments (1700-1799): This account includes long-term investments in equity securities, bonds, and other financial instruments that are not expected to be converted into cash within the next year.
      9. Other Assets (1800-1999): This account serves as a catch-all category for any additional assets that don't fit into the above classifications. It may include items like deferred tax assets, advances to suppliers, or miscellaneous assets.

    It's important to note that the specific account numbers and classifications may vary depending on the company's chart of accounts and reporting framework. The above summary provides a general overview of common asset accounts within the given range.

    • Liability accounts (2000 - 2999): Liabilities are obligations or debts that a business or individual owes to external parties. They represent the claims or obligations that others have on the assets of the entity. In accounting, liabilities are recorded on the balance sheet and reflect the financial obligations that need to be fulfilled in the future.

    The range of accounts from 2000 to 2999 typically represents liability accounts in the chart of accounts. These accounts capture the various types of financial obligations or debts that a business owes to external parties. Here is a concise summary of common liability accounts within this range:

      1. Accounts Payable (2000-2099): This account represents the amount owed to suppliers or vendors for goods or services received on credit. It includes unpaid invoices and bills that are due to be paid.
      2. Accrued Expenses (2100-2199): These are expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid or recorded in the accounting system. They include accrued wages, accrued interest, and accrued utilities.
      3. Short-term Borrowings (2200-2299): This account captures short-term loans or borrowings that are due within one year or the normal operating cycle of the business. It includes bank loans, lines of credit, and short-term notes payable.
      4. Long-term Borrowings (2300-2399): This account represents long-term loans or borrowings that extend beyond the next year. It includes long-term bank loans, bonds payable, and other long-term debt obligations.
      5. Deferred Revenue (2400-2499): Also known as unearned revenue, this account records the amount received in advance from customers for goods or services that are yet to be delivered. It represents an obligation to provide the products or services in the future.
      6. Other Current Liabilities (2500-2599): This category includes various short-term financial obligations that do not fit into specific accounts mentioned above. It may include items such as accrued taxes, customer deposits, and other short-term liabilities.
      7. Other Long-term Liabilities (2600-2699): Similar to other current liabilities, this category captures long-term financial obligations that are not classified under specific accounts. It may include items such as deferred tax liabilities, long-term customer deposits, and other long-term liabilities.

    These are just a few examples of common liability accounts within the 2000-2999 range. The specific accounts and their numbering may vary depending on the organization's chart of accounts and industry-specific requirements.

    • Equity accounts (3000 - 3999): The complexity of required accounts for the equity financial category in the chart of accounts can vary depending on the type of business ownership. Here is a brief summary of the accounts typically used for equity in different types of business ownership:
      1. Sole Proprietorship:
        • Owner's Capital: Represents the owner's investment in the business.
        • Owner's Drawings: Records withdrawals made by the owner for personal use.
        • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits or losses that have been retained in the business.
      1. Partnership:
        • Partner's Capital Accounts: Each partner has a separate capital account to track their investment in the partnership.
        • Partner's Drawings: Records withdrawals made by partners for personal use.
        • Partner's Profit and Loss Allocation: Tracks the allocation of profits or losses among partners based on their agreed-upon sharing ratios.
        • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits or losses that have been retained in the partnership.
      1. Corporation:
        • Common Stock: Represents the ownership interest of common shareholders.
        • Preferred Stock: Represents the ownership interest of preferred shareholders, if applicable.
        • Additional Paid-in Capital: Records the amount received from shareholders in excess of the par value of the shares.
        • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits or losses that have not been distributed to shareholders as dividends.
        • Treasury Stock: Represents shares of the corporation's own stock that have been repurchased and are held by the corporation.
      1. Limited Liability Company (LLC):
        • Member's Capital Accounts: Each member has a separate capital account to track their investment in the LLC.
        • Member's Drawings: Records withdrawals made by members for personal use.
        • Member's Profit and Loss Allocation: Tracks the allocation of profits or losses among members based on their agreed-upon sharing ratios.
        • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits or losses that have been retained in the LLC.

    • Revenue accounts (4000 - 4999): 
      • Operating revenue are the revenue generated through the primary business activities or core operations by a business. It represents the income earned from the sale of goods, provision of services, or other activities directly related to the main operations of the business. 
      • Other revenue refers to income or revenue generated by a company from sources other than its primary business activities or core operations. It includes income that is incidental or peripheral to the main operations of the company. It is often considered as supplementary or ancillary revenue. Examples of accounts that can be categorized as "Other Revenue" and are not necessarily non-operating revenue. These accounts represent income or revenue generated from sources that are not directly related to the company's primary business activities. Here are some examples: 
        1. Dividend Income: Income received from owning shares of other companies, typically in the form of dividends.
        2. Donations or Gifts: Any non-business-related contributions or gifts received.
        3. Gain on Sale of Assets: Profits realized from the sale of non-inventory assets like property, equipment, or investments. When a company sells its assets, such as property, equipment, or investments, at a price higher than their carrying value, the difference between the sale proceeds and the book value is recognized as a gain and recorded as Other Revenue.
        4. Insurance Proceeds / Insurance Reimbursements: Amounts received from insurance claims due to unexpected events or losses. If a company receives insurance reimbursements for claims related to damages, losses, or liabilities, the amount received would be classified as Other Revenue.
        5. Interest Income: Interest earned on bank deposits, loans to others, or any interest-bearing securities.
        6. Foreign Exchange Gains: Gains resulting from favourable currency exchange rate fluctuations. Companies that engage in international transactions may have foreign currency exchange gains when converting foreign currencies back to the reporting currency.
        7. Recovery of Bad Debts: Income obtained from previously written-off bad debts that have now been recovered.
        8. Rental Income: Revenue generated from leasing out property, equipment, or other assets.  If a company owns properties or assets that are rented out to third parties, the rental income generated from those assets would be considered as Other Revenue.
        9. Royalties and Licensing Fees: Income earned from granting the use of intellectual property, trademarks, or patents.If a company licences its intellectual property, such as patents, trademarks, or copyrights, to other entities in exchange for royalties, the income received from those royalties would fall under Other Revenue.
        10. Miscellaneous Income: Any other sources of income that do not fit into the primary revenue categories.

It is essential to segregate Other Income to provide a clear picture of the business's primary revenue-generating activities and any additional income streams.

    G1100-000

    Debtor's control

    Debtor's control represents the total outstanding receivables owed to a company by its customers. When a company extends credit to its customers, it creates accounts receivable, which are recorded as assets on the balance sheet. The debtor's control account is a summary or aggregate account that captures the total amount of accounts receivable owed to the company.

    The debtor's control account is typically used in conjunction with individual customer accounts or subsidiary ledgers. Each customer's outstanding balance is recorded separately in their respective customer accounts, while the debtor's control account provides a summarized view of all the outstanding receivables.

    As payments are received from customers, the debtor's control account is updated to reflect the reduced balances of accounts receivable. The account is debited when new sales are made on credit and credited when payments are received.

    The debtor's control account helps the company monitor and manage its receivables effectively. It provides a centralized view of the overall receivables position, enabling management to assess the creditworthiness of customers, track payment trends, and take appropriate actions for collection.

    It's important to regularly reconcile the debtor's control account with the individual customer accounts to ensure accuracy and identify any discrepancies. Reconciliation helps identify any errors, unrecorded payments, or disputes that need resolution.

    The debtor's control account is a key component of the company's financial statements, particularly the balance sheet, where it is reported as a current asset. It provides important information about the company's liquidity, credit risk, and the value of outstanding receivables.

    It's recommended to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the specific treatment and accounts to be used for the debtor's control account in your business's chart of accounts. They can provide guidance based on your company's specific needs and accounting practices.


    G1110-000

    Provision for doubtful debts

    Provision for doubtful debts is an estimate made by a company to account for potential losses from customers who may default on their payment obligations. When a company extends credit to customers or sells goods or services on credit, there is a risk that some customers may not fulfil their payment obligations, leading to bad debts.

    To account for this risk, companies create a provision for doubtful debts on their balance sheet. The provision is based on historical data, past payment patterns, creditworthiness of customers, and other relevant factors. It represents the amount that the company expects to write off as bad debts or be unable to collect from customers.

    The provision for doubtful debts is typically calculated as a percentage of accounts receivable or based on specific customer balances. The purpose of this provision is to anticipate and account for the potential losses in the financial statements, providing a more accurate representation of the company's financial position.

    The provision for doubtful debts is reported as a contra-asset account on the balance sheet, reducing the value of accounts receivable. It is a conservative accounting approach that recognizes the risk of bad debts and ensures that the financial statements reflect the estimated collectability of receivables.

    When actual bad debts occur, the company writes off the specific amounts from the provision for doubtful debts and reduces the accounts receivable balance accordingly. This adjustment reflects the actual losses incurred by the company.

    It's important for businesses to regularly review and update the provision for doubtful debts to ensure it remains appropriate and reflective of the company's credit risk. Factors such as changes in customer payment behaviour, economic conditions, and specific customer circumstances may require adjustments to the provision.

    It's recommended to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the specific treatment and accounts to be used for the provision for doubtful debts in your business's chart of accounts.


    G1200-000

    Stock / Inventory

    Stock or inventory includes the raw materials, work-in-progress (partially completed goods), and finished goods that a company holds. It represents the goods that are available for sale to customers or are used in the production process.

    Inventory is an essential component of various businesses, such as retailers, manufacturers, wholesalers, and distributors. It enables these businesses to meet customer demand, fulfil orders, and generate revenue.

    The stock/inventory account is used to track the quantity and value of the goods held by the company. It includes the cost of acquiring or producing the inventory, which consists of direct costs (such as materials and labour) and indirect costs (such as overhead expenses).

    Inventory is typically classified into different categories based on its nature or stage of completion. Common inventory categories include raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods. Each category may have its specific accounts or sub-accounts to track the related inventory items.

    The stock/inventory account is updated regularly to reflect the changes in quantity and value of the inventory. This includes recording purchases, sales, returns, adjustments for damaged or obsolete goods, and any other inventory-related transactions.

    Accurate inventory management is crucial for businesses to ensure sufficient stock levels, minimize holding costs, prevent stock-outs or overstocking, and optimize cash flow. It involves inventory valuation methods, such as First-In-First-Out (FIFO), Last-In-First-Out (LIFO), or weighted average cost, to determine the cost of goods sold and the value of remaining inventory.

    The stock/inventory account is reported as a current asset on the balance sheet, representing the value of inventory held at a specific point in time. It's important to conduct regular physical inventory counts or use inventory management systems to reconcile the recorded inventory balances with the actual stock on hand.

    The specific accounts and classifications for stock/inventory may vary depending on the company's industry, size, and accounting practices. It's recommended to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the appropriate structure and accounts for tracking inventory in your business's chart of accounts.


    G1300-000

    Accrued revenue / Outstanding revenue

    Accrued revenue is revenue that has been earned but not yet received or recorded. It occurs when a company provides goods or services to a customer, but the payment or recognition of the revenue is delayed beyond the normal billing cycle. The company recognizes the revenue in its financial statements even though the cash has not been received.

    Examples of accrued revenue include:

    • Services rendered: A service-based company may provide services to a client but bill them at the end of the month or project.
    • Rent income: If a company owns rental properties, it may accrue rent revenue when it's earned, even if the tenant pays at a later date.
    • Interest income: Financial institutions may accrue interest income on loans or investments that have not yet been paid or received.

    • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): COGS specifically refers to the direct costs incurred in producing or acquiring the goods that are sold by a company. It includes the cost of raw materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead directly associated with the production of goods. COGS is primarily used in manufacturing, retail, and distribution businesses.
    • Cost of Services Rendered (SOSR): SOSR is similar to COGS but applies specifically to service-based businesses. It represents the direct costs associated with providing services to customers, such as labour, subcontractor fees, and any other expenses directly related to delivering the services.

    Gross profit is calculated by subtracting the COS, COGS, or SOSR from the revenue generated from the sale of goods or services. It represents the profit generated from the core operations of the business before considering other operating expenses. Gross profit provides insight into the efficiency and profitability of a company's primary revenue-generating activities.

    COST OF GOODS SOLD - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is an expense category in the income statement that calculates the direct costs associated with the production or acquisition of the goods or services sold by a company. It includes the costs directly attributed to the production process, such as raw materials, direct labour, and manufacturing overhead.

    COGS is calculated by subtracting the ending inventory from the sum of the beginning inventory and purchases or production costs during a specific accounting period. The resulting figure represents the cost of the goods that have been sold during that period.

    The concept of COGS is primarily applicable to businesses that deal with physical goods, such as retailers, manufacturers, and wholesalers. For service-based companies, the equivalent concept is often referred to as the Cost of Services Rendered (COSR).

    COGS is a crucial metric in determining a company's gross profit, which is calculated by subtracting COGS from the revenue generated by the sale of goods or services. It represents the profit made by the company before considering other operating expenses.

    In the chart of accounts, Cost of Goods Sold is typically classified as an expense account and categorized under the operating expenses section. It is commonly grouped with other expense accounts related to the cost of sales or production.

    Accurately tracking and managing COGS is essential for businesses to determine their profitability, assess pricing strategies, and make informed decisions about inventory management and production processes.

    It's important to note that the specific accounts and classifications related to COGS may vary depending on the company's accounting practices and industry. It's advisable to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the appropriate structure and accounts for tracking COGS in your business's chart of accounts.


    COST OF SALES - Cost of Sales (COS), also known as Cost of Revenue, is an expense category in the income statement that captures the direct costs directly associated with the sales of goods or services by a company. It includes the costs related to the production, acquisition, or delivery of the products or services that were sold.

    COS encompasses the costs directly attributed to the production or acquisition process, such as raw materials, direct labour, and manufacturing overhead. Additionally, it may include other costs incurred in the sales process, such as shipping or packaging expenses, direct sales commissions, or certain marketing expenses.

    The calculation of COS typically involves determining the direct costs associated with the production or acquisition of the goods or services sold during a specific accounting period. This may involve taking into account the beginning inventory, purchases or production costs, and adjusting for the ending inventory to arrive at the total cost of sales.

    COS is an essential component in calculating a company's gross profit. Gross profit is obtained by subtracting the COS from the revenue generated by the sale of goods or services. It reflects the profitability of a company's core operations before considering other operating expenses.

    In the chart of accounts, Cost of Sales is typically classified as an expense account and categorized under the operating expenses section. It is often grouped with other accounts related to the cost of production, acquisition, or delivery of goods or services.

    It's important to note that the specific accounts and classifications related to Cost of Sales may vary depending on the company's accounting practices and industry. It's advisable to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the appropriate structure and accounts for tracking Cost of Sales in your business's chart of accounts.

    COST OF SERVICES RENDERED (COSR) - Cost of Services Rendered (COSR) refers to the expenses directly associated with providing services to customers or clients. It represents the costs incurred by a business in delivering its services and is analogous to the concept of Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) for a product-based business.

    COSR includes various expenses related to the production or delivery of services, such as labour costs, direct materials or supplies used in providing the services, subcontractor fees, overhead costs, and any other direct costs specifically attributable to the services rendered. These expenses are incurred during the process of providing services to generate revenue.

    By subtracting the COSR from the revenue generated by services, a business can calculate its gross profit or gross margin. This figure represents the profitability of the services provided before accounting for other operating expenses.

    It's important to note that the specific accounts and classifications within COSR can vary depending on the nature of the services and the accounting practices of the business. It's recommended to consult with an accountant or financial professional to determine the appropriate accounts and structure for recording COSR in your specific accounting system.
