    South Africa - Chart of Accounts - Small business

    Sample outline of a Chart of Accounts for a South African Small business?

    For a South African small business, the Chart of Accounts (COA) should be straightforward, yet comprehensive enough to manage the financial transactions and reporting needs of the business. Here's a sample outline of a Chart of Accounts for a South African small business:

    1. Assets 
      • 1.1 Current Assets 
        • 1.1.1 Cash on Hand 
        • 1.1.2 Cash in Bank 
        • 1.1.3 Petty Cash 
        • 1.1.4 Accounts Receivable (Outstanding customer invoices) 
        • 1.1.5 Inventory (if applicable) 
        • 1.1.6 Prepaid Expenses 
        • 1.1.7 Other Current Assets
      • 1.2 Non-Current Assets 
        • 1.2.1 Property and Buildings (if owned by the business) 
        • 1.2.2 Furniture and Fixtures 
        • 1.2.3 Equipment (computers, machinery, etc.) 
        • 1.2.4 Vehicles (if used for business purposes) 
        • 2.5 Other Non-Current Assets 
        • 2.5.1 Long-Term Investments 
        • 2.5.2 Long-Term Notes Receivable 
        • 2.5.3 Deferred Tax Assets 
        • 2.5.4 Security Deposits 
        • 2.5.5 Long-Term Prepaid Expenses 
        • 2.5.6 Mineral Rights 
        • 2.5.7 Leasehold Improvements 
        • 2.5.8 Deferred Charges 
        • 2.5.9 Long-Term Deposits 
        • 2.5.10 Deferred Financing Costs
      • 1.3 Intangible Assets 
        • 1.3.1 Patents 
        • 1.3.2 Licences 
        • 1.3.3 Trademarks 
        • 1.3.4 Copyrights 
        • 1.3.5 Goodwill (if applicable) 
        • 1.3.6 Other Intangible Assets
    1. Liabilities 
      • 2.1 Current Liabilities 
        • 2.1.1 Accounts Payable (Outstanding bills and expenses) 
        • 2.1.2 Accrued Liabilities (e.g., Salaries payable, utilities) 
        • 2.1.3 Short-Term Loans and Borrowings (if applicable) 
        • 2.1.4 Credit Card Payables 
        • 2.1.5 Value Added Tax (VAT) Payable (if applicable) 
          • 2.1.5.1 Output VAT (VAT on Sales) 
          • 2.1.5.2 Input VAT (VAT on Purchases) 
          • 2.1.5.3 Input VAT (VAT on Capital goods) 
          • 2.1.5.4 Zero Rated VAT (Sales with 0% VAT) 
          • 2.1.5.5 VAT Bad Debts (Uncollectible VAT on Sales) 
        • 2.1.6 Payroll or Salaries and Wages Payable 
          • 2.1.6.1 Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) Tax 
          • 2.1.6.2 Pension Fund Contributions Payable 
          • 2.1.6.3 Medical Aid Contributions Payable 
          • 2.1.6.4 Other Payroll Deductions 
        • 2.1.7 Other Current Liabilities
      • 2.2 Non-Current Liabilities 
        • 2.2.1 Long-Term Loans and Borrowings (if applicable) 
        • 2.2.2 Lease Liabilities (if applicable) 
        • 2.2.3 Other Non-Current Liabilities
    1. Equity 
      • 3.1 Owner's Capital 
      • 3.2 Retained Earnings  
      • 3.3 Drawings or Dividends (if applicable) 
      • 3.4 Other Equity Items
    1. Income 
      • 4.1 Sales Revenue (Income from products or services sold) 
      • 4.2 Interest Income (e.g., from bank accounts) 
      • 4.3 Other Income
    1. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) 
      • 5.1 Beginning Inventory (if applicable) 
      • 5.2 Purchases (Cost of goods acquired for resale or materials used) 
      • 5.3 Ending Inventory (if applicable) 
      • 5.4 Cost of Services (Direct costs related to service delivery, if applicable) 
      • 5.5 Gross Profit
    1. Expenses 
      • 6.1 Salaries and Wages 
      • 6.2 Rent and Lease Expenses 
      • 6.3 Utilities (Electricity, Water, etc.) 
      • 6.4 Office Supplies 
      • 6.5 Advertising and Promotion 
      • 6.6 Bank Fees and Interest Expenses 
      • 6.7 Repairs and Maintenance 
      • 6.8 Travel and Transportation Expenses 
      • 6.9 Professional Fees (e.g., accounting, legal) 
      • 6.10 Insurance 
      • 6.11 Depreciation and Amortization 
      • 6.12 Other Operating Expenses
    1. Taxes 
      • 7.1 Value Added Tax (VAT) Payable (if applicable) 
      • 7.2 Pay-As-You-Earn (PAYE) Tax (if applicable) 
      • 7.3 Other Taxes
    1. Other Comprehensive Income 
      • 8.1 Foreign Currency Translation Gains/Losses (if applicable) 
      • 8.2 Other Items
    1. Gains and Losses 
      • 9.1 Gain on Sale of Assets (if applicable) 
      • 9.2 Loss on Impairment of Assets (if applicable)
    1. Inter-Account Transactions 
      • 10.1 Transfers between Bank Accounts (if applicable)
    1. Project or Job Income and Expenses 
      • 11.1 Project A 
        • 11.1.1 Income 
        • 11.1.2 Expenses 
      • 11.2 Project B 
        • 11.2.1 Income 
        • 11.2.2 Expenses 
      • 11.3 Other Projects or Jobs

    Please note that the above COA is a general outline and can be customized to suit the specific operations and financial reporting needs of the South African small business. It is essential to consult with a qualified accountant or financial expert to ensure compliance with South African accounting standards and regulations for small businesses.

    Value Added Tax (VAT)

    For the VAT-related accounts:

    • Output VAT (VAT on Sales): This account tracks the VAT amount charged to customers on sales of goods and services. The percentage for Output VAT is the standard VAT rate, which is prescribed by the South African Revenue Service (SARS). As of my knowledge cutoff in September 2021, the standard VAT rate in South Africa is 15%.
    • Input VAT (VAT on Purchases): This account records the VAT paid on purchases of goods and services from suppliers. Businesses can claim Input VAT as a credit against their Output VAT liability. The percentage for Input VAT is also the standard VAT rate, which is 15%.
    • Input VAT (VAT on Purchases - Capital goods): In South Africa, when a business purchases capital goods (such as machinery, equipment, vehicles, etc.) that are used in the business for an extended period, they are entitled to claim Input VAT on these purchases as part of their Input Tax Credit. Therefore, Input VAT should encompass both VAT on regular purchases (goods and services used in day-to-day operations) and VAT on capital goods.

    It's important to accurately track and account for Input VAT on capital goods to ensure compliance with VAT regulations and to maximize VAT recoveries for the business. As always, businesses should consult with a qualified tax professional to ensure proper accounting treatment and adherence to the latest VAT rules and regulations.

    • Zero Rated VAT (Sales with 0% VAT): Some goods and services are considered zero-rated for VAT purposes. This means that VAT is still applicable, but the rate is 0%. Examples of zero-rated goods may include basic food items, certain medical supplies, and exports of goods. The percentage for Zero Rated VAT is 0%.
    • VAT Bad Debts (Uncollectible VAT on Sales): If a customer fails to pay an invoice, and the debt is considered bad (uncollectible), businesses can claim relief on the VAT previously accounted for on that invoice. This is known as VAT Bad Debts. The percentage for VAT Bad Debts is the standard VAT rate, which is 15%.

    Please note that tax regulations can change, so it's important to verify the current VAT rates and regulations with the South African Revenue Service (SARS) or a qualified tax professional. Additionally, businesses should adjust their Chart of Accounts as needed to comply with any updates in tax laws or accounting standards.
