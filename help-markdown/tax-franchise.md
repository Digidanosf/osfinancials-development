# What is franchise tax?

Franchise tax is a type of tax imposed on certain business entities by some states in the United States. It is not related to the franchise fees paid by businesses to use a company's brand name or business model. Instead, franchise tax is a state-level tax that applies to specific types of corporations and limited liability companies (LLCs) that are registered or doing business in the state.

The franchise tax is often based on the company's net worth or capital, the number of shares issued, or a combination of these factors. Each state that imposes a franchise tax may have its own rules and methods for calculating the tax liability. Some states use the term "*franchise tax*," while others may refer to it as a "*privilege tax*" or a "*business entity tax*."

The purpose of the franchise tax is to generate revenue for the state's government, and it is typically separate from the income tax that businesses may also be required to pay. The franchise tax may apply regardless of whether the business is profitable or not.

It's important to note that not all states impose a franchise tax, and the requirements and rates can vary significantly from one state to another. Some states may have minimum taxes for small businesses, while others may have different tax rates based on the size and type of business entity.

As tax laws and regulations can change over time, businesses should check with the specific state's tax authorities or consult with a tax professional to understand the franchise tax requirements and obligations in the state where they operate or are registered.
