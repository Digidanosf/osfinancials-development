**Bank account type**

* **&#49;1-Proof** -&nbsp; See [http://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elfproef](<http://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elfproef> "target=\"\_blank\"") - Proof, also known as the "11-test" or "Elfproef" in Dutch, is a mathematical test used in the Netherlands to validate the accuracy of bank account numbers. The test is based on the principle of modulo 11 arithmetic and is used to detect errors in bank account numbers, such as transpositions or missing digits.

The Proof test involves multiplying each digit of the bank account number by a corresponding weight, then summing the products and calculating the remainder when divided by 11. If the remainder is zero, the bank account number is considered valid. If the remainder is not zero, the bank account number is considered invalid and may contain an error that needs to be corrected.

The Proof test is commonly used in the Netherlands by banks, businesses, and government agencies to validate the accuracy of bank account numbers before processing transactions. The test helps to prevent errors and ensure that funds are transferred to the correct account. However, it should be noted that the Proof test is not foolproof and may not detect all errors in bank account numbers.

**BIC** - (Bank Identifier Code) - BIC stands for Bank Identifier Code, which is a unique identifier assigned to financial institutions, primarily for international money transfers. The BIC is also known as the SWIFT code, as it is assigned by the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication (SWIFT).

The BIC consists of a series of eight or eleven alphanumeric characters that identify a specific financial institution. The first four characters of the BIC identify the bank or financial institution, while the next two characters identify the country code. The last two or five characters are optional and represent the branch code of the financial institution.

The BIC is used in conjunction with the International Bank Account Number (IBAN) to facilitate international money transfers. Together, the IBAN and BIC provide a standardized and secure means of identifying the correct recipient and financial institution for international transactions.

The BIC is widely used around the world and is recognized by most financial institutions and banks. It is an essential part of the global financial system and facilitates the efficient and secure transfer of funds across borders.

**IBAN** - (International Bank Account Number) See [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_Bank\_Account\_Number](<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_Bank\_Account\_Number> "target=\"\_blank\"") IBAN stands for International Bank Account Number, which is a standardized international format for bank account numbers used for international money transfers. The IBAN was developed by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) to facilitate cross-border transactions and to make international payments more efficient.

The IBAN is a unique identifier that includes the country code, check digits, and the account number. The country code and check digits help to validate the accuracy of the account number and ensure that the money is transferred to the correct recipient. The IBAN is used in conjunction with the Bank Identifier Code (BIC) to facilitate international money transfers.

The IBAN is used in many countries around the world, particularly in Europe, but it is not used universally. Some countries, such as the United States, Canada, and Australia, do not use IBANs for their domestic banking transactions. However, when making international payments to countries that use IBANs, it is generally required to include the recipient's IBAN in order for the transaction to be processed successfully.

**SWIFT** - (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) See [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SWIFT](<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SWIFT> "target=\"\_blank\"") / [http://www.theswiftcodes.com/](<http://www.theswiftcodes.com/> "target=\"\_blank\"") SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) is a global network used by banks and other financial institutions to securely transmit financial messages and information. The SWIFT network provides a standardized and secure means for financial institutions to communicate with each other, enabling them to send and receive payments, foreign exchange trades, securities transactions, and other financial messages.

SWIFT operates a messaging platform that allows banks to exchange financial information with one another in a secure and standardized format. Each financial institution that participates in the SWIFT network has a unique Bank Identifier Code (BIC), which identifies the institution's name, location, and country code. The SWIFT network is used by over 11,000 financial institutions in more than 200 countries, making it the largest financial messaging network in the world.

SWIFT messages are sent through the network in a standardized format that includes information about the sender, the recipient, and the transaction. This information is transmitted securely and is only accessible to the authorized parties involved in the transaction. SWIFT messages can be used for a variety of financial transactions, including payments, foreign exchange trades, securities transactions, and other financial messages.

**SEPA** - (Single Euro Payments Area) - See - [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single\_Euro\_Payments\_Area](<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single\_Euro\_Payments\_Area> "target=\"\_blank\"") - SEPA stands for Single Euro Payments Area, which is a European Union initiative aimed at harmonizing electronic euro payments across participating countries. The SEPA initiative allows individuals and businesses to make payments in euros to any other bank account located in the SEPA area, regardless of whether the recipient's bank is located in their home country or in another SEPA country.

SEPA covers the 27 EU member states, as well as Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland, Monaco, and San Marino. SEPA has standardized the format of euro payments across participating countries, which has made cross-border transactions more efficient and cost-effective. SEPA payments are processed using the International Bank Account Number (IBAN) and the Bank Identifier Code (BIC).

# Other common banking abbreviations/acronyms

Here are some other common banking abbreviations/acronyms:

1. **ATM**: Automated Teller Machine - A machine that allows customers to perform various banking transactions, such as withdrawing cash, depositing money, transferring funds, and checking account balances, without the need for a bank teller.
1. **ACH**: Automated Clearing House - A payment system that allows electronic funds transfers between bank accounts in the United States.
1. **BSB**: Bank State Branch - A unique identifier used in Australia to specify both the bank and the branch where an account is held.
1. **CC**: Credit Card - A payment card issued by a bank or financial institution that allows users to make purchases and borrow money up to a certain credit limit.
1. **CEO**: Chief Executive Officer - The highest-ranking executive in a company or organization responsible for making major corporate decisions and managing overall operations.
1. **CFPB**: Consumer Financial Protection Bureau - An agency of the United States government responsible for protecting consumers in the financial sector by enforcing consumer protection laws and providing education and resources to consumers.
1. **CTR**: Currency Transaction Report - A report that financial institutions are required to file with the government for any cash transaction over $10,000 in the United States.
1. **EFT**: Electronic Funds Transfer - The electronic transfer of funds from one bank account to another, usually initiated through online banking or a mobile app.
1. **FDIC**: Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation - A United States government agency that provides insurance to depositors in case their bank fails, up to a certain amount.
1. **KYC**: Know Your Customer - The process banks and other financial institutions use to verify the identity of their customers and assess their potential risk for illegal activities such as money laundering and terrorism financing.
1. **POS**: Point of Sale - The location where a customer makes a payment for goods or services, typically at a physical retail store or restaurant.
1. **RTGS**: Real-Time Gross Settlement - A payment system that allows for the real-time transfer of funds between bank accounts, typically used for high-value transactions.
1. **PIN**: Personal Identification Number - A numeric code used to verify the identity of a customer when making a transaction, typically associated with a debit or credit card.
1. **OD**: Overdraft - A financial arrangement where a bank allows a customer to withdraw more money than they have in their account, up to a certain limit, usually with associated fees and interest.
1. **NEFT**: National Electronic Funds Transfer - A payment system used in India that allows for the electronic transfer of funds between bank accounts.
1. **MICR**: Magnetic Ink Character Recognition - A technology used by banks to encode and process checks with a magnetic ink line at the bottom of the check.
1. **IMPS**: Immediate Payment Service - A payment system in India that allows for the instant transfer of funds between bank accounts, typically through a mobile app.
