# Types of fixed assets

***

# Fixed assets&nbsp;

Fixed assets or tangible assets is a long-term asset which is used in a business to generate income over the life span of the fixed asset.&nbsp;

# Fixed Asset Register

The fixed asset register (FAR) (Inventory of fixed assets) is supplementary accounting records and the accounting method used to record and keep track of the assets of a business. The fixed asset registers is used or the to keep record and track the fixed assets used in the the process of the business activities production of goods or rendering services. These assets are not sold as trading stock in the normal course of the business activities.

For example, a hardware store may sell tools to its customers but may also purchase tools to produce goods for customer requirements. For example, if it sells boards and circular saws to its customers, it may need a circular saw to cut these boards to size according to the customer’s specific requirements. The circular saw it uses to cut the boards are fixed assets and needs to be recorded in the fixed asset register. The boards which is sold to customers, are trading stock and needs to be recorded in the stock (inventory) records.

The fixed asset register needs to record the following for each fixed asset for each accounting period:

* The value of assets – The value (opening balance) as at the start of the financial (tax) year. It should also include the depreciation written-off as well as the value of the assets as at the end of the financial (tax) year (closing balance).
* Date of acquisition – The date on which the asset is procured (purchased).
* Period - The acceptable period over which the write-off of depreciation is prescribed.
* Other details necessary to calculate depreciation and tax allowances.

Some reasons to keep accurate records of the fixed assets (non-current assets) of the business are;

* Fixed asset management and preventative maintenance.
* Tracking and deterring theft of fixed assets.
* Tax purposes – The Depreciation (Wear and Tear Allowances) which the tax authorities allow you to claim as a business expense. &nbsp;
* Insurance – Accurate records and valuations to assist with insuring the assets. These records may help to prevent the asses from being over-insured or under-insured. This may result in disputes if penalties being imposed by the insurance company when a claim is processed. &nbsp;

There are two groups of Fixed assets; i.e.;

1. Tangible assets (which can be physically touched). In these cases you may write-off depreciation or wear and tear over the lifespan of the Fixed assets. Some examples, are;
- &nbsp;

  - Land and buildings (This asset type usually appreciates in value over the years).
  - Furniture and fittings
  - Office equipment
  - IT equipment (computers, etc.)
  - Motor vehicles
  - Plant and machinery
  - Tools

2. Intangible assets (which cannot be physically touched). The allowances and depreciation is referred to as amortization for intangible assets. Some examples, are; &nbsp;
- &nbsp;

  - Goodwill
  - Intellectual capital
  - Intellectual property
  - Brand
  - Copyright
  - Patent
  - Patent valuation
  - Trademark

3. Capital Gains Tax (CGT)
3. Accelerated Depreciation may apply to businesses, for example, Urban Development Zones (UDZ).&nbsp;
