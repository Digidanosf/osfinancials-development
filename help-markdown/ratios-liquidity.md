# Liquidity Ratios

***

Liquidity ratios are financial metrics used to assess a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations and convert assets into cash to fund its immediate needs. These ratios provide insight into the company's short-term liquidity position and its capacity to handle financial challenges or unexpected expenses. Three common liquidity ratios are:

1. [**Current Ratio (Working Capital Ratio)**](<ratios-working-capital-ratio.md>): The Current Ratio (or Working Capital Ratio) measures the company's ability to pay off its current liabilities using its current assets. The formula is:

***Current Ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities*

A current ratio above 1 indicates that the company has more current assets than current liabilities and is generally considered financially healthy.

2. [**Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio)**](<ratios-quick-ratio.md>): The Quick Ratio is a more stringent liquidity ratio that excludes inventory from current assets. It focuses on the company's ability to pay off its current liabilities using its most liquid assets. The formula is:

***Quick Ratio** = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities*

A quick ratio of 1 or higher is generally considered financially healthy.

3. [**Cash Ratio**](<ratios-cash-ratio.md>): The Cash Ratio is the most conservative liquidity ratio, as it only considers the company's cash and cash equivalents to pay off current liabilities. The formula is:

***Cash Ratio** = Cash and Cash Equivalents / Current Liabilities*

A cash ratio of 1 or close to 1 indicates that the company can cover its short-term obligations using only its cash reserves.

Liquidity ratios are essential for creditors, investors, and other stakeholders to assess the company's short-term financial health and its ability to weather financial downturns or unexpected events. It's crucial to interpret these ratios in the context of the company's industry, business model, and specific financial circumstances. Different industries may have varying liquidity requirements, and a single ratio may not provide a complete picture of the company's overall financial condition. Therefore, it's essential to analyze liquidity ratios alongside other financial ratios and indicators for a comprehensive evaluation of the company's financial health.
