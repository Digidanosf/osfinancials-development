# Income received in advance / Unearned revenue / Deferred revenue : Accounting standards and Accounting method

The concept of income received in advance, also known as unearned revenue or deferred revenue, is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following IFRS (full) or IFRS for SMEs. The treatment of income received in advance is generally consistent across the two frameworks.

Under IFRS (full), income received in advance is recognized as a liability and is accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. It represents cash received from customers or clients for goods or services that are yet to be provided. The liability is recognized because the revenue has not been earned at the time of receipt. As the goods or services are delivered or performed, the liability is reduced, and the corresponding revenue is recognized.

Similarly, under IFRS for SMEs, there are specific provisions for the recognition, measurement, and presentation of income received in advance. The standard provides guidance on how to account for unearned revenue, including when it should be recognized as a liability and how it should be measured. The disclosure requirements may also differ from IFRS (full), being more streamlined and tailored to the needs of SMEs.

In terms of the accounting method, the treatment of income received in advance would also differ between the cash basis and accrual basis of accounting. Under the cash basis, income is recognized when cash is received, and therefore, income received in advance would be recognized as revenue immediately upon receipt. In contrast, under the accrual basis, income received in advance is recognized as a liability and is gradually recognized as revenue over time as the goods or services are provided.

It's important to consult the specific accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction and entity to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of income received in advance based on the chosen accounting framework.

# What is income received in advance?

Income received in advance, also known as unearned revenue or deferred revenue, refers to payments received from customers or clients for goods or services that have not yet been provided. It represents a liability for the business because the revenue has been received in advance of earning it.

When a business receives income in advance, it has an obligation to deliver the goods or services at a later date. Until that obligation is fulfilled, the amount received is classified as a liability on the balance sheet.

Examples of income received in advance include:

1. **Subscriptions**: When a customer pays for a subscription service upfront, such as an annual magazine subscription or a software subscription.
1. **Rent**: If a tenant pays rent for several months in advance, the portion of rent that corresponds to future periods is considered income received in advance.
1. **Service contracts**: When a business enters into a service contract with a customer and receives payment upfront before providing the services.
1. **Membership fees**: Membership-based organizations that collect fees in advance from their members.

To account for income received in advance, the business initially records it as a liability on the balance sheet. As the goods or services are provided over time, the liability is gradually recognized as revenue in the income statement. The amount recognized as revenue is based on the portion of the goods or services delivered or consumed.

Proper accounting for income received in advance ensures that revenue is recognized in the appropriate accounting periods, aligning with the matching principle in accrual accounting.

# Is Adjustments - Income received in advance (Unearned revenue) only applicable to the accrual basis accounting method or does it apply to the cash basis accounting methods?

The concept of "*Income received in advance*" or "*Unearned revenue*" generally applies to the accrual basis accounting method.

Under the accrual basis, revenue is recognised when it is earned, regardless of when the payment is received. If a customer pays in advance for goods or services that will be provided in the future, the payment is recorded as a liability (unearned revenue) until the revenue is earned.

In contrast, the cash basis accounting method recognises revenue when cash is received. With cash basis accounting, there is no concept of unearned revenue because revenue is only recorded when the cash is actually received.

Therefore, the specific account "*Adjustments - Income received in advance*" would typically be applicable to the accrual basis accounting method, where there is a need to record and track unearned revenue. In cash basis accounting, such adjustments may not be necessary as revenue is recognized upon receipt of cash.

It's important to note that the choice of accounting method (accrual basis or cash basis) depends on various factors, including legal requirements, business needs, and financial reporting purposes. Always consult with an accounting professional or refer to the accounting standards applicable to your jurisdiction for guidance on the appropriate accounting treatment based on your specific circumstances.
