# Balance sheet accounts - Chart of accounts

***

Every transaction has an impact on the assets, liabilities, capital, income, or expense items of an entity. For instance, sales increase assets such as bank balance or debtors and income. On the other hand, expenses like advertising decrease assets (cash in the bank) or increase liabilities (creditors). Income increases the profit, whereas expenses decrease it. Since most transactions affect both the Income statement and the Balance sheet, it is necessary to consider the entire accounting equation or expand it.

The expanded accounting equation is as follows:

| ![Image](<lib/admon-scale.png>) | ***&nbsp;***&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; ![Image](<lib/admon-material-accounts-assets.svg>) ***ASSETS&nbsp; &nbsp; =*** ![Image](<lib/admon-material-accounts-income.svg>) ***INCOME +*** ![Image](<lib/admon-material-accounts-capital.svg>) ***CAPITAL** **+*** ![Image](<lib/admon-material-accounts-liabilities.svg>) ***LIABILITIES -*** ![Image](<lib/admon-material-accounts-expenses.svg>) ***EXPENSES*** |
| --- | :---: |


This equation shows that the assets of an entity are equal to the sum of its liabilities, capital, income, and expenses. The equation must always balance, which means that the total value of the assets must be equal to the sum of liabilities, capital, income, and expenses. This is a fundamental principle in accounting, and it ensures that the financial statements are accurate and reliable.
