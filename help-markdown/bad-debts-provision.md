# What is provision for bad debts?

Provision for bad debts, also known as an allowance for doubtful accounts or provision for credit losses, is an accounting concept that involves estimating and setting aside an amount of money to cover potential losses from customers' unpaid debts. It is a financial provision made by a company to account for the possibility that some portion of its accounts receivable may not be collectible.

The provision for bad debts is created to reflect the expected credit losses in a company's financial statements. It is based on the principle of conservatism, which states that companies should recognise potential losses as soon as they are probable, but not necessarily wait until they are certain.

The provision for bad debts is typically determined by considering factors such as the creditworthiness of customers, historical collection patterns, ageing of accounts receivable, economic conditions, industry trends, and any specific circumstances that may affect the collectability of outstanding debts.

Creating a provision for bad debts allows the company to match anticipated losses with the revenue earned in the same accounting period, ensuring a more accurate representation of the company's financial position and performance.

The provision for bad debts is reported as an expense on the income statement, reducing the company's profit and taxable income. It is also presented as a contra-asset on the balance sheet, offsetting the accounts receivable to reflect the net realizable value.

By establishing a provision for bad debts, companies demonstrate prudence in recognising and accounting for potential credit losses, improving the accuracy and reliability of their financial statements. It also helps in assessing the true value of accounts receivable and evaluating the overall credit risk of the business.

# Summary of key points for provision of bad debts

The outline for the process of creating a provision for bad debts or doubtful debts in your accounting practices, is as follows:

1. **Identifying irrecoverable debts**: Before calculating the provision for bad debts, it is important to identify the debts that are deemed irrecoverable and should be written off as bad debts. This assessment helps separate the debts that are unlikely to be collected from those that still have a chance of recovery.
1. **Methods for calculating the provision for bad debts**:
- &nbsp;

  - **Age analysis schedules**: One method is to estimate or calculate the provision based on the age analysis schedules of your debtor accounts. This involves categorizing outstanding debts by their age and applying specific percentages or rates based on historical data or industry norms.
  - **Total outstanding debt**: Another method is to base the provision on the total outstanding debt, considering factors such as the overall creditworthiness of your customers and any specific risks associated with certain accounts.
  - **Total credit sales**: You can also calculate the provision based on the total sales made on credit, taking into account the historical percentage of bad debts experienced in relation to credit sales.

3. **Revising the provision for bad debts amount**: In subsequent years, it is necessary to review and revise the provision amounts established in previous years. Changes may be required to increase or decrease the provision based on changes in credit sales, outstanding debts, economic conditions, or other relevant factors. Consistency in the chosen method is essential to ensure accurate and comparable provisions across different periods.
3. **Impact on Income statement**: The provision for bad debts or doubtful debts directly affects the Income Statement (Profit or Loss) of your business. It represents an expense incurred to account for potential losses from irrecoverable debts and reflects the prudent recognition of these risks in your financial statements.

By following these procedures and consistently applying the chosen method for calculating provisions, you can ensure the accuracy of your financial reporting and reflect the potential impact of bad debts on your business's profitability. It is recommended to consult with an accounting professional or adhere to accounting standards and regulations applicable in your jurisdiction to ensure compliance and accuracy in recording provisions for bad debts.

# What is the frequency for provision for bad debts?

The frequency for creating a provision for bad debts can vary depending on the company's accounting policies and the specific circumstances. There is no fixed rule or standard frequency mandated by accounting regulations such as IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) or GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles).

Typically, the provision for bad debts is established at the end of the accounting period, which is usually annually for most companies. It is common for companies to review their accounts receivable and estimate the potential losses at the end of each financial year. This allows them to adjust the provision based on the ageing of the debts and changes in the creditworthiness of customers.

However, some companies may choose to review and update the provision more frequently, such as semi-annually, quarterly, or even on a monthly basis. This can be particularly relevant for businesses with higher credit risk customers or industries where payment delays or defaults are more prevalent.

The frequency of provisioning is determined by factors such as the nature of the business, the volume and credit terms of sales, historical collection patterns, economic conditions, and the accuracy of credit risk assessments. It is important for companies to maintain consistency in their approach to provisioning and ensure that it aligns with their financial reporting policies and requirements.

Ultimately, the frequency of provisioning for bad debts should strike a balance between timely recognition of potential losses and the practicality of assessing and estimating the credit risk on a regular basis.

# How is Provision for bad debts calculated?

## Common approaches to provision of bad debts

The provision for bad debts is typically calculated by estimating the amount of accounts receivable that is expected to become uncollectible. This provision is created to account for the possibility of customers defaulting on their payment obligations. While the exact calculation method may vary depending on the company's specific policies and circumstances, here are a few commonly used approaches:

1. **Ageing analysis**: This method involves categorizing accounts receivable based on their age and assessing the likelihood of collection for each category. Typically, the older the debt, the higher the probability of it becoming uncollectible. The company assigns specific percentages or rates of uncollectibility to each age category and applies them to the corresponding outstanding balances to calculate the provision.
1. **Percentage of total receivables**: Under this approach, a predetermined percentage is applied to the total accounts receivable balance to estimate the provision. The percentage used may be based on historical data, industry averages, or management's judgment.
1. **Specific analysis**: In certain cases, a company may individually evaluate each customer account to determine the likelihood of non-payment. Factors such as the customer's payment history, financial condition, and creditworthiness are considered to estimate the provision for each specific account.
1. **Historical loss rate**: This method involves analysing historical bad debt write-offs over a certain period and applying the average loss rate to the current outstanding balances. The historical loss rate can be calculated by dividing the total bad debts written off in a specific period by the total credit sales for the same period.

## Common methods for calculation of provision for bad debts

The provision for bad debts is calculated based on the total outstanding debtors' balances. Before calculating the provision, it's necessary to identify and write off any irrecoverable bad debts that may have occurred throughout the financial year.

Typically, the provision for bad debts is established at the end of the financial year before closing the books. However, it's important to review and adjust the provision from year to year. This may involve increasing or decreasing the provision based on changes in circumstances.

There are three common [methods for calculating the provision for bad debts](<bad-debts-provision-calculate.md>) or doubtful debts:

1. **Age analysis**: This method involves analysing the age of outstanding debtor accounts and estimating the provision based on different age categories. Typically, older debts have a higher probability of becoming uncollectible, so higher provision rates are applied to those categories.
1. **Total outstanding debt**: This method estimates the provision based on the total amount of outstanding debt owed by customers. A certain percentage or rate is applied to the total outstanding balance to calculate the provision.
1. **Total credit sales**: This method estimates the provision based on the total sales made on credit. A percentage or rate is applied to the total credit sales to determine the provision amount.

These methods are commonly used, and the choice of which method to use may depend on various factors such as the company's industry, historical collection data, and management's judgment. It's important to note that companies may also use a combination of these methods or other approaches based on their specific circumstances.
