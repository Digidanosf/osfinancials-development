# What is Online Sales Tax and Online Purchase Tax?

"*Online Sales Tax*" and "*Online Purchase Tax*" are terms used to refer to the taxation of transactions involving goods or services conducted over the internet. Here's what each term typically means:

1. **Online Sales Tax**:
- &nbsp;

  - Online sales tax refers to the requirement for online retailers and e-commerce businesses to collect and remit sales tax on transactions conducted over the internet.
  - Prior to the "*Wayfair*" decision by the US Supreme Court in 2018, many online retailers were not required to collect sales tax from customers if they did not have a physical presence (such as a brick-and-mortar store) in the customer's state.
  - The "*Wayfair*" decision allowed states to impose sales tax obligations on remote sellers (online retailers) based on their economic activity in the state, even if they lack a physical presence.
  - As a result, online retailers may need to collect and remit sales tax on transactions made by customers located in states where the retailer exceeds certain sales or transaction thresholds.

2. **Online Purchase Tax**:
1) &nbsp;

   - Online purchase tax can refer to the tax that customers are required to pay on their online purchases if the seller does not collect sales tax at the time of purchase.
   - Some online retailers may not automatically calculate and charge sales tax on transactions. In such cases, customers are often still legally obligated to pay the equivalent use tax directly to their state's tax authority.
   - Online purchase tax is essentially the same as use tax. It's the tax that a customer owes on a taxable purchase when the seller didn't charge them sales tax, typically because the seller doesn't have a tax obligation in the customer's state.

Both online sales tax and online purchase tax aim to ensure that online transactions are subject to the same taxation principles as traditional in-person transactions. It's important for individuals and businesses to be aware of their obligations regarding these taxes, as tax laws can vary by jurisdiction and are subject to change. If you have questions about your specific situation, consulting a tax professional or the relevant tax authority is recommended.

Outside of the United States, similar concepts to online sales tax and use tax exist in various forms in different countries. These concepts may have different names and structures depending on the jurisdiction. Here are a few examples:

**European Union (EU)**: The EU has implemented Value Added Tax (VAT) regulations that apply to digital goods and services sold to consumers within the EU, regardless of the seller's location. This is often referred to as the "VAT on e-services" or "VAT MOSS" (Mini One Stop Shop) system.

**Canada**: Canada imposes the Goods and Services Tax (GST) and the Harmonized Sales Tax (HST) on digital products and services sold to Canadian consumers, regardless of the seller's location.

**Australia**: Australia has a Goods and Services Tax (GST) that applies to digital products and services imported by Australian consumers.

**United Kingdom**: The UK has implemented rules similar to the EU's VAT MOSS system for digital services provided to UK consumers.

**South Africa**: South Africa has implemented Value Added Tax (VAT) on electronic services provided to South African consumers, regardless of the supplier's location.

**New Zealand**: New Zealand has a Goods and Services Tax (GST) on remote services and digital products imported by New Zealand consumers.

These examples showcase that many countries have adapted their tax systems to address the taxation of digital products and online services. The specifics of the regulations, thresholds, and requirements can vary significantly from one jurisdiction to another. If you're doing business internationally or purchasing goods and services from abroad, it's important to understand the relevant tax laws and consult with tax professionals or local tax authorities to ensure compliance.
