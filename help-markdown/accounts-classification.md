# Basic outline for a 4-digit main accounts chart of accounts

The outline for the chart of accounts is a general framework that can be applicable to various accounting systems, including IFRS for SMEs. It covers different categories of accounts that are commonly found in financial reporting. While the account ranges and specific names may vary depending on the company and reporting requirements, the general structure remains similar.

The suggested outline includes:

1. **Asset accounts (1000 - 1999)**: Accounts representing various types of assets owned or controlled by the business, such as cash, receivables, inventory, and fixed assets. They include cash, short-term investments, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses, property, plant, and equipment, intangible assets, investments, and other assets.
1. **Liability accounts (2000 - 2999)**: Accounts representing the financial obligations or debts of the business to external parties, including payables, borrowings, and accrued liabilities.&nbsp; They include accounts payable, accrued expenses, short-term borrowings, long-term borrowings, deferred revenue, and other current and long-term liabilities.
1. **Equity accounts (3000 - 3999)**: Accounts representing the equity portion of the company's capital structure, depending on the type of business ownership (sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation, LLC). For a sole proprietorship, they typically include owner's capital, owner's drawings, and retained earnings. In a partnership, they include partner's capital accounts, partner's drawings, partner's profit and loss allocation, and retained earnings. In a corporation, they include common stock, preferred stock, additional paid-in capital, retained earnings, and treasury stock. In a limited liability company (LLC), they include member's capital accounts, member's drawings, member's profit and loss allocation, and retained earnings.
1. **Revenue accounts (4000 - 4999)**: Accounts representing the income or revenue generated by the company's primary business activities. They include operating revenue, which is directly related to the core operations, and other revenue, which includes income from sources incidental or peripheral to the main operations.
1. **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) accounts (5000 - 5999)**: Accounts reflecting the direct costs associated with producing or acquiring the goods or services sold by the company. The specific account names may vary, such as **Cost of Sales (COS)** or **Cost of Services Rendered (SOSR)**, depending on the nature of the business.
1. **Expense accounts (6000 - 7999)**: Accounts representing the operating expenses incurred by the business in its day-to-day operations. They include wages, rent, utilities, materials, and other expenses directly related to the revenue-generating activities.
1. **Non-operating revenue accounts (8000 - 8999)**: Accounts recording income or revenue generated by the business that is not directly related to its core operations. It includes sources of income that are incidental, peripheral, or non-recurring in nature.
1. **Non-operating expenses accounts (9000 - 9999)**: Accounts reflecting expenses incurred by the business that are not directly related to its core operations. They typically arise from activities outside the normal operations and are separate from operating expenses.

This summary is a general outline of the typical accounts found within each range.&nbsp;

# Structure - Sequences of Accounts

It's important to customize and adjust this chart of accounts and reporting framework to suit your business's specific requirements and comply with the relevant reporting standards, tax regulations, and accounting frameworks in your jurisdiction and country. Factors such as your business ownership type, industry, and other specific needs should be considered when adapting the chart of accounts and reporting framework to ensure accurate and relevant financial reporting for your business.

The organization of accounts, including expense accounts, in a financial chart of accounts can vary widely from one organization to another. While there is no universal rule that dictates the order of account names in an alphabetical sequence, it's essential to structure the chart of accounts in a way that makes sense for the specific needs of your organization and facilitates financial reporting.

Common approaches to organizing expense accounts in a chart of accounts include:

1. **Numerical Sequence**: Some organizations prefer to assign account codes or numbers sequentially, making it easier to manage and reference accounts systematically. This approach doesn't necessarily require alphabetical order.
1. **Alphabetical Order**: You can choose to list account names in alphabetical order. This can be helpful for quickly locating specific accounts and may align well with the alphabetical arrangement of income statement categories like "Salaries and Wages," "Amortization," "Depreciation," and "Professional Fees."
1. **Functional or Grouping Approach**: Many organizations group accounts based on their function or type. For example, you might have a group for all personnel-related expenses, a group for depreciation and amortization, a group for professional fees, and so on. Within each group, you can choose to order accounts alphabetically or based on their importance or frequency of use.
1. **Customized for Specific Needs**: Some organizations customize their chart of accounts to align with their unique business operations or industry requirements. This may result in a hybrid approach that combines numerical sequencing, alphabetical ordering, and functional grouping.

Ultimately, the goal of structuring a chart of accounts is to make financial reporting, analysis, and decision-making more efficient and meaningful for your organization. You should choose an approach that suits your organization's specific needs, accounting practices, and reporting requirements. The key is to maintain consistency and clarity so that financial information can be easily understood and utilized.
