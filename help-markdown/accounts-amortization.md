# Amortization

***

# What is Amortization?

Amortization is a financial term that refers to the process of spreading the cost of an intangible asset over its useful life. It is a systematic allocation of the asset's cost, similar to depreciation for tangible assets. Intangible assets are assets that lack physical substance but have value due to their contractual or legal rights, intellectual property, or other factors.

The purpose of amortization is to match the cost of acquiring or creating an intangible asset with the benefits it provides over time. As the asset generates benefits, its cost is gradually recorded as an expense on the income statement. This helps in accurately reflecting the asset's consumption or decline in value over time.

Common examples of intangible assets subject to amortization include:

1. **Patents**: Exclusive rights granted to inventors to protect their inventions from being used, made, or sold by others without permission.
1. **Copyrights**: Exclusive rights granted to authors, artists, or creators to protect their original works (e.g., books, music, software).
1. **Trademarks**: Distinctive symbols, names, or logos that identify and distinguish products or services from those of competitors.
1. **Franchise Rights**: Rights granted by a franchisor to a franchisee to operate under their brand and business model.
1. **Licenses**: Permissions granted by an authority to carry out specific activities or use specific resources.

The process of amortization involves dividing the cost of the intangible asset (minus any estimated residual value) over its estimated useful life. The result is the annual amortization expense, which is recognized in the financial statements over the asset's useful life.

It's essential to note that amortization is different from depreciation, which is the systematic allocation of the cost of tangible assets (such as buildings, machinery, and equipment) over their useful life. Both amortization and depreciation are crucial accounting practices that help organizations accurately represent the consumption of assets and their impact on financial performance.

# What is the transactions for amortization?

The transactions for amortization involve recording the periodic allocation of the cost of an intangible asset as an expense over its useful life. Let's take an example of a patent to understand the transactions for amortization:

Assuming a company purchased a patent for $60,000 with an estimated useful life of 5 years and no estimated residual value. The company would record the following transactions for amortization:

1. **Initial Purchase of Patent**:

| **Patent Account (Intangible Asset)** | **Cash/Bank Account (Payment)** |
| --- | --- |
| **Debit**: $60,000 | **Credit**: $60,000 |


2. **Annual Amortization Expense (Straight-line method)**:

The straight-line method evenly distributes the cost of the patent over its useful life. So, the annual amortization expense would be $60,000 / 5 = $12,000.

| **Amortization Expense Account (Operating Expense)** | **Accumulated Amortization Account (Contra-Asset)** |
| --- | --- |
| **Debit**: $12,000 | **Credit**: $12,000 |


At the end of Year 1, the company's financial statements would show the following balances:

**Balance Sheet**

* **Intangible Assets**:

  * **Patent**: $48,000 ($60,000 - $12,000)

**Income Statement**

* **Operating Expenses**:

  * **Amortization Expense**: $12,000

The process of amortization will continue each year until the end of the asset's useful life or until the asset is fully amortized.

Please note that the above example uses the straight-line method of amortization, which allocates an equal amount of the asset's cost each year. Other methods like the declining balance method or units-of-production method can also be used depending on the organization's accounting policies and the asset's pattern of usage or consumption.
