# Depreciation : Accounting standards and Accounting method

Depreciation is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS), including IFRS (full) and IFRS for SMEs.

Under IFRS (full) and IFRS for SMEs, entities are required to account for depreciation of their tangible assets. Depreciation is considered an important aspect of financial reporting as it helps to accurately present the financial position and performance of an entity by allocating the cost of assets over their useful lives.

As for the accounting method, depreciation is primarily associated with the accrual-based accounting method. In accrual accounting, expenses are recognized when incurred, and revenues are recognized when earned, regardless of the timing of cash flows. Depreciation is recorded as an expense over the asset's useful life, even if the cash outflow for the asset's acquisition occurred in a different accounting period.

On the other hand, the cash-based accounting method does not typically involve the recognition of depreciation. Cash-based accounting recognizes revenues and expenses when cash is received or paid, respectively, rather than when they are earned or incurred. Since depreciation is a non-cash expense, it is not recognized in the cash-based accounting method.

However, it's important to note that entities following the cash-based accounting method may still be required to account for depreciation for taxation purposes or for other reporting needs outside of their cash-based financial statements.

# What is depreciation?

Depreciation is an accounting method used to allocate the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. It represents the gradual decrease in the value of an asset due to factors such as wear and tear, obsolescence, or the passage of time. Depreciation is a non-cash expense, meaning it does not involve an actual outflow of cash but is recorded to reflect the decrease in the asset's value over time.

Depreciation is important in financial reporting as it helps to match the cost of an asset with the revenue it generates during its useful life. By spreading the cost over multiple accounting periods, depreciation allows businesses to reflect the consumption of the asset's value in the periods it is used to generate revenue.

There are various methods for calculating depreciation, including straight-line depreciation, declining balance depreciation, and units of production depreciation. The choice of depreciation method depends on factors such as the nature of the asset, its expected useful life, and applicable accounting standards.

It's worth noting that depreciation is primarily applicable to tangible assets such as buildings, vehicles, machinery, equipment, and furniture. Intangible assets, such as patents or trademarks, are subject to a similar concept called amortization.

# Is Depreciation a long-term adjustment?

Depreciation is a long-term adjustment. It involves allocating the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. The purpose of depreciation is to recognize the gradual wear and tear, obsolescence, or decrease in the asset's value over time. By spreading the cost of the asset over its useful life, depreciation helps to match the expense of using the asset with the revenues it generates.

Since depreciation is related to the usage of long-term assets, it is considered a long-term adjustment. It affects the financial statements over multiple accounting periods, reflecting the ongoing consumption of the asset's economic benefits. The accumulated depreciation is typically presented as a contra-asset account on the balance sheet, reducing the carrying value of the related asset.

Depreciation is important for accurately reporting an entity's financial position and profitability, as it recognizes the cost of utilizing long-term assets in generating revenue. It is a key component in determining the true cost and value of an entity's assets and impacts various financial ratios, such as return on assets and net income.

# What accounts is required to record and manage depreciation?

To record and manage depreciation, the following accounts are typically used:

1. **Accumulated Depreciation**: This is a contra-asset account that is used to accumulate the total depreciation expense recognized on an asset over its useful life. It is presented on the balance sheet and reduces the carrying value of the related asset.
1. **Depreciation Expense**: This is an expense account on the income statement that represents the periodic depreciation charge for the accounting period. It is used to record the portion of an asset's cost that is expensed during the period.
1. **Asset Accounts**: Each individual asset that is subject to depreciation should have its own asset account. These accounts represent the cost or value of the asset and are typically classified as long-term assets on the balance sheet.

The recording of depreciation involves debiting the Depreciation Expense account and crediting the Accumulated Depreciation account. This allocation process continues over the useful life of the asset until the asset's carrying value is reduced to its estimated residual value.

Managing depreciation involves periodically reviewing and updating the estimated useful lives, residual values, and depreciation methods for assets. It also includes conducting periodic impairment assessments to ensure that the carrying value of the assets is not higher than their recoverable amounts.

It's important to note that the specific accounts and procedures for recording and managing depreciation may vary based on the accounting standards followed (such as IFRS or GAAP) and the entity's internal policies and practices.

# Is Asset accounts for each Asset main account structured into sub-accounts?

It is a common practice to use sub-accounts within the Fixed assets category in the Chart of Accounts to track the cost of the asset and the accumulated depreciation.

Typically, the main account under the Fixed assets category represents the net value or carrying value of the asset. This main account reflects the original cost of the asset minus the accumulated depreciation. It provides a snapshot of the asset's value at a specific point in time.

The sub-accounts within the Fixed assets category are used to track specific details related to the asset. In this case, the \**Asset at cost*" sub-account is used to record the original cost or value of the asset. The "Accumulated depreciation" sub-account is a contra account that is credited with the amount of depreciation expense recognized over time. By having a contra account, the net value of the asset is calculated by subtracting the accumulated depreciation from the asset's cost.

This approach allows for better visibility and tracking of the asset's cost, accumulated depreciation, and net value. It also simplifies the process of recording depreciation expenses and provides a clear picture of the asset's value on the balance sheet.

# Depreciation - when is it recorded, annually, 6 months, 3 months or monthly?

The frequency of recording depreciation depends on the accounting policy and the specific requirements of the reporting entity. In general, depreciation is recorded periodically over the useful life of the asset. The most common frequency for recording depreciation is monthly or annually.

**Monthly depreciation**: Some businesses choose to record depreciation on a monthly basis. This approach allows for more precise allocation of the asset's cost over time and provides a more granular view of the asset's impact on the financial statements. Monthly depreciation is commonly used for assets with shorter useful lives or when more accurate tracking of depreciation expense is desired.

**Annual depreciation**: Many businesses opt to record depreciation on an annual basis. This means that the total depreciation expense for the year is recorded in a single transaction. Annual depreciation is commonly used for assets with longer useful lives or when the periodic impact of depreciation on the financial statements is considered less significant.

**Other frequencies**: In certain cases, businesses may choose to record depreciation on a different time frame, such as every six months or every three months. These alternative frequencies are less common but may be employed based on specific reporting or operational needs.

It's important to note that the chosen frequency of depreciation should be consistent and in line with the accounting policies and practices of the entity. The frequency should also adhere to the relevant accounting standards, such as International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) or Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), which provide guidance on depreciation accounting.

This includes IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) and its specific application for small and medium-sized entities (IFRS for SMEs). IFRS for SMEs provides simplified and streamlined accounting requirements for entities that meet the definition of a small or medium-sized entity. While it contains specific provisions tailored for SMEs, it still includes guidance on depreciation accounting. Therefore, the chosen frequency of depreciation should align with the requirements and principles outlined in IFRS for SMEs when applicable.

It is important for entities to carefully consider and apply the relevant accounting standards, such as IFRS for SMEs, to ensure accurate and consistent financial reporting, including the recording of depreciation. Compliance with the applicable accounting standards helps maintain transparency and comparability in financial statements, which is essential for users of financial information.

# What transactions is required to process the procurement of a fixed asset?

To process the procurement of a fixed asset, you would typically need to perform the following transactions:

1. **Purchase of the Fixed asset**: Create a purchase transaction to record the acquisition of the fixed asset. This transaction includes the purchase price of the asset, any applicable taxes, transportation costs, and other directly attributable costs. Debit the Fixed asset account to increase its value and credit the appropriate Cash (bank account) or Accounts payable account (creditor/supplier account to reflect the payment made.
1. **Recording Depreciation**: After the fixed asset is acquired, you need to record depreciation expense over its useful life. This transaction transfers a portion of the asset's value from the Fixed Asset account to the Accumulated depreciation account. The specific depreciation method and useful life of the asset will determine the amount of depreciation expense to be recorded periodically.
1. **Revaluation**: If the fixed asset's value changes significantly over time, you may need to perform a revaluation transaction to adjust its carrying value. This transaction involves updating the Fixed asset account with the new value of the asset and adjusting the Accumulated Depreciation account accordingly.
1. **Disposal**: When the fixed asset is no longer in use or is sold, a disposal transaction is recorded. This transaction removes the asset's value from the Fixed asset account and its accumulated depreciation from the Accumulated Depreciation account. Any proceeds from the sale of the asset are recorded in a separate account, such as Gain on Disposal of Fixed assets or Loss on Disposal of Fixed assets.

These transactions ensure that the procurement, depreciation, and disposal of fixed assets are accurately recorded in the financial statements, reflecting the asset's value, depreciation over time, and any changes in its carrying value.
