# Accrued or outstanding expenses : Accounting standards and Accounting method

The concept of accrued or outstanding expenses is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following IFRS (full) or IFRS for SMEs. The treatment of accrued expenses may vary slightly between the two frameworks.

Under IFRS (full), accrued expenses are recognized as liabilities and are accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. Accrued expenses are expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid or recorded in the financial statements. Examples of accrued expenses include accrued salaries, accrued interest, or accrued utilities. These expenses are recognized as a liability and are typically recorded when the related goods or services have been received, regardless of whether the cash payment has been made.

Under IFRS for SMEs, there are specific provisions for the recognition, measurement, and presentation of accrued expenses. The standard provides guidance on how to account for accrued expenses, including when they should be recognized as liabilities and how they should be measured. The disclosure requirements may also differ from IFRS (full), being more streamlined and tailored to the needs of SMEs.

Regarding the accounting method (cash basis or accrual basis), the treatment of accrued expenses would generally be different. The cash basis of accounting recognizes expenses and liabilities only when cash is paid, and therefore, accrued expenses would not be recorded as liabilities under the cash basis. In contrast, under the accrual basis of accounting, accrued expenses are recognized as liabilities when the related expenses have been incurred, regardless of the cash payment.

It's important to consult the specific accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction and entity to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of accrued or outstanding expenses based on the chosen accounting framework.

# What is accrued or outstanding expenses?

Accrued or outstanding expenses refer to expenses that have been incurred but have not yet been paid or recorded in the accounting books by the end of the accounting period. These expenses are recognised as liabilities because they represent an obligation of the business to make future payments.

Accrued expenses can include various types of costs, such as salaries and wages, interest, utilities, rent, taxes, and professional fees. They typically arise when the expense is recognized in the accounting period, but the payment is due in a subsequent period.

For example, if a business receives an invoice for utility expenses at the end of the month but has not yet paid the bill, the utility expense is considered an accrued expense. The business has incurred the expense during the month, and even though the payment has not been made, it is recorded as a liability in the financial statements.

Accrued expenses are typically recorded through an adjusting entry at the end of the accounting period to ensure that the expense is properly recognized in the period it relates to. This helps in matching expenses with the revenues they generate, providing a more accurate representation of the financial position and performance of the business.

Accrued expenses are reversed in the following accounting period when the actual payment is made. The reversal ensures that the expense is not double-counted, and the liability is removed from the books.

Accrued or outstanding expenses are relevant for both cash basis and accrual basis accounting methods, as they reflect the timing of the expense recognition and payment, regardless of the method used to track cash flows.

# Adjustments - Accrued expenses (Outstanding expenses) - how would this be recorded?

To record the adjustment for accrued expenses (outstanding expenses), follow these steps:

1. **Identify the accrued expenses**: Determine the expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid by the end of the accounting period. This could include items like salaries, rent, utilities, or any other expenses that are accrued.
1. **Create an Accrued Expenses account**: Establish a liability account in your chart of accounts called "Accrued Expenses" or a similar name. Assign it a suitable account number based on your accounting system's numbering convention.

# Treatment of Accrued expenses (Outstanding expenses) in the new financial year?

When transitioning to a new financial year, the treatment of accrued expenses (outstanding expenses) depends on whether the expenses have been paid or settled by the end of the previous financial year. Here are two scenarios to consider:

1. **Unpaid Accrued Expenses**:
- &nbsp;

  - If the accrued expenses from the previous financial year remain unpaid at the end of that year, they will continue to be recorded as liabilities in the new financial year.
  - Create a new entry in the new financial year to recognize the outstanding expenses and update the balances accordingly.
  - The journal entry typically involves debiting the appropriate expense account and crediting the accrued expenses account to clear the previous year's accruals.
  - This adjustment ensures that the expenses are properly recorded in the new financial year, reflecting the actual liability owed.

2. **Paid Accrued Expenses**:
1) &nbsp;

   - If the accrued expenses from the previous financial year were paid or settled in the new financial year before finalizing the accounts, no adjustment is needed.
   - The expenses would have been recorded in the previous financial year when they were accrued, and the subsequent payment clears the liability in the new financial year.
   - Ensure that the payment is correctly recorded in the accounts, typically by debiting the corresponding expense account and crediting the cash or bank account.

In both cases, it's essential to review and reconcile the accrued expenses accounts from the previous financial year with the actual payments and expenses incurred in the new financial year to ensure accurate financial reporting.&nbsp;
