# Accrued income / Outstanding income / Income receivable : Accounting standards and Accounting method

Accrued income, also referred to as outstanding income or income receivable, is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following IFRS (full) or IFRS for SMEs. The recognition and treatment of accrued income are generally consistent across these frameworks.

Under IFRS (full), accrued income is recognized as an asset and is accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. It represents revenue that has been earned but not yet received in cash or its equivalent. This typically occurs when goods or services have been provided to customers, but the payment is not yet due or has not been received. Accrued income is recognized to reflect the economic benefit that the entity has earned.

Similarly, under IFRS for SMEs, there are specific provisions for the recognition, measurement, and presentation of accrued income. The standard provides guidance on when accrued income should be recognized as an asset and how it should be measured. The disclosure requirements may also differ from IFRS (full), tailored to the needs of SMEs.

Regarding the accounting method, the treatment of accrued income would also differ between the cash basis and accrual basis of accounting. Under the cash basis, income is recognized when cash is received, and therefore, accrued income would not be applicable. In contrast, under the accrual basis, accrued income is recognized as an asset even if cash has not been received yet, based on the concept of earning revenue.

It's important to note that the recognition and measurement of accrued income may vary based on the specific circumstances and industry practices. It's advisable to consult the applicable accounting standards and guidelines, as well as any specific requirements or interpretations for your jurisdiction and entity, to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of accrued income based on the chosen accounting framework.

# What is Accrued income / Outstanding income?

Accrued (outstanding) income refers to income that has been earned by a business but has not yet been received or recorded as revenue. It represents the amount of income that is due to the business for goods sold or services rendered but for which the payment has not been received.

Accrued income typically arises when a business follows the accrual basis of accounting, which recognises revenue when it is earned, regardless of when the payment is actually received. This means that even if the payment for a sale or service is expected to be received in the future, the revenue is still recognized in the period when it was earned.

Examples of accrued income include:

1. **Services rendered but not yet billed**: If a business provides services to a customer but has not yet issued an invoice for those services, the income earned from those services would be considered accrued income.
1. **Rent or interest income**: If a business owns rental properties or lends money and the payment for rent or interest is due at the end of the month or at a later date, the income for that period would be accrued until the payment is received.
1. **Sales made on credit**: When a business sells goods or services on credit, meaning the customer is allowed to pay at a later date, the revenue from those sales is recognized as accrued income until the payment is received.

Accrued income is recorded as a current asset on the balance sheet until it is received. It represents the right to receive payment for goods sold or services rendered. Once the payment is received, the accrued income is reversed, and the corresponding revenue is recorded.

# What is the batch type for accrued (outstanding) income?

Accrual or year-end adjustments are typically recorded in the general journal. The general journal is a catch-all journal where transactions that do not fit into specific specialised journals are recorded.

While it is possible to create a separate batch or journal type such as "*Adjustments*" specifically for accrual or year-end adjustments, it is not a common practice. The general journal is often used for such adjustments as it allows for flexibility in recording various types of transactions and adjustments that may arise during the accounting period.

# Treatment of accrued (outstanding) income in the new financial year&nbsp;

The treatment of accrued (outstanding) income in the new financial year depends on the accounting method used by the company. In some cases, it may be necessary to reverse the accrued income recorded in the previous financial year, while in other cases, it may not be required.

1. **Accrual Basis Accounting**:
- &nbsp;

  - If the company follows the accrual basis of accounting, where revenues are recognized when earned and not when cash is received, the accrued income recorded in the previous financial year should be reversed.
  - At the start of the new financial year, the accrued income recorded in the previous year is no longer outstanding because it has been earned and should now be recognized as revenue.
  - The reversal entry would typically involve debiting the Accrued income account and crediting the appropriate revenue account(s) to recognise the revenue earned.

2. **Cash Basis Accounting**:
- &nbsp;

  - If the company follows the cash basis of accounting, where revenues are recognised only when cash is received, there is no need to reverse the accrued income recorded in the previous financial year.
  - Accrued income is not recognised until the cash is actually received in the new financial year.
  - The recognition of revenue will occur when the payment is received, and the journal entry would typically involve debiting the Cash account and crediting the appropriate revenue account(s) to recognize the revenue earned.

It's important to note that the specific accounting treatment may vary based on the company's accounting policies, local regulations, and specific circumstances. It is recommended to consult with a qualified accountant or accounting professional to determine the appropriate treatment of accrued income in your specific situation.
