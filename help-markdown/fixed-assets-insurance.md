# Insurance for fixed assets

***

# Is insurance commonly obtained for fixed assets?

Insurance is commonly obtained for fixed assets. Fixed assets represent significant investments for businesses, and insuring them helps protect against potential risks and losses. Insurance coverage for fixed assets can include various types of policies, such as property insurance, casualty insurance, liability insurance, or specialized coverage for specific types of assets.

Insurance for fixed assets typically covers events like fire, theft, natural disasters, vandalism, and other unexpected incidents that could cause damage or loss to the assets. The insurance policy helps provide financial compensation or replacement for the damaged or lost assets, allowing the business to recover from such incidents without significant financial burden.

Insurance coverage for fixed assets is particularly important for assets of high value or critical importance to the business's operations. It helps mitigate the financial risks associated with potential damage or loss, giving the business peace of mind and financial protection.

# Can the proper records maintained help to be penalised by insurance companies regarding to be over-insured and under-insured?

Maintaining proper records of fixed assets can help prevent penalties related to over-insurance or under-insurance by insurance companies. Accurate and up-to-date records provide essential information about the value and characteristics of fixed assets, which is crucial for determining appropriate insurance coverage.

When insuring fixed assets, it is important to accurately assess their value and determine the appropriate coverage amount. Over-insuring can lead to unnecessarily high insurance premiums, while under-insuring can result in inadequate compensation in the event of a loss.

By maintaining comprehensive records of fixed assets, including details such as acquisition cost, depreciation, improvements or upgrades, and current market value, businesses can provide accurate information to insurance companies during the underwriting process. This allows insurance companies to assess the value of the assets and recommend appropriate coverage limits.

Proper records also help in regular insurance reviews and updates. As fixed assets may change in value or undergo significant changes over time, periodically reviewing and updating insurance coverage ensures that the coverage remains aligned with the assets' current value and risk exposure.

In case of a claim, thorough and well-maintained records can serve as evidence to support the insurance claim and help expedite the claims settlement process. Detailed records provide a clear picture of the assets' value and condition before the loss occurred, facilitating a smoother claims process and reducing the likelihood of disputes or penalties.

Overall, accurate and comprehensive records of fixed assets play a vital role in maintaining appropriate insurance coverage, avoiding penalties related to over-insurance or under-insurance, and ensuring a fair and efficient claims process.

# How is insurance premiums is this recorded?

Recording insurance premiums involves categorizing them as an expense and reflecting them in the financial records of a business. Here's how insurance premiums are typically recorded:

1. **Create an expense account**: Set up a specific expense account in the Chart of Accounts to track insurance-related expenses. This account could be named something like "Insurance Expense" or "Insurance Premiums."
1. **Record the premium payment**: When the insurance premium is paid, create a journal entry to record the transaction. Debit the Insurance Expense account for the amount of the premium, reflecting the increase in expenses. Credit the Cash or Bank account to show the decrease in cash as a result of the payment.

Under the cash basis accounting method, expenses are recognized when cash is paid out, and income is recognized when cash is received.&nbsp;

Since insurance premiums are typically paid upfront for a specific coverage period, they would be recorded as an expense in full when the payment is made. There is no need for adjusting entries to allocate the expense over the coverage period because the cash basis accounting method does not consider the passage of time or the matching of expenses to the corresponding revenue.&nbsp;

On the other hand, under the accrual basis accounting method, expenses are recognized when they are incurred, regardless of when the cash payment is made. Adjusting entries are used to properly allocate expenses over the accounting periods to reflect the matching principle, which states that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the related revenue. Therefore, under accrual accounting, insurance premiums would be allocated over the coverage period through adjusting entries at the end of each accounting period.

It's important to note that the choice between the cash basis and accrual basis accounting methods depends on the specific circumstances and accounting requirements of the business. The accrual basis is generally considered more accurate and in line with generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) and international financial reporting standards (IFRS).

**Periodic adjustments**: Insurance premiums are often paid for a specific coverage period (e.g., one year) and are typically recorded as an expense evenly over that period. To reflect this, you may need to make adjusting entries at the end of each accounting period to allocate the insurance expense for the portion of coverage used during that period.&nbsp;

For example, if you have an annual insurance premium of $1,200, and your accounting period is one month, you would record a monthly insurance expense of $100. At the end of each month, you would make an adjusting entry to recognize $100 as insurance expense and reduce the prepaid portion of the premium.

Reporting: The insurance expense recorded will be reflected in the income statement as part of the operating expenses for the specific period. It will contribute to the calculation of net income and the overall financial performance of the business.

By properly recording insurance premiums as expenses, businesses can accurately track and report their insurance-related costs, which provides insights into their financial performance and aids in budgeting and decision-making processes.
