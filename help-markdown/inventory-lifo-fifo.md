# What is LIFO and FIFO?

LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) and FIFO (First-In, First-Out) are two commonly used inventory valuation methods that businesses use to determine the cost of goods sold (COGS) and the value of ending inventory. These methods are used to account for the flow of inventory items in and out of a business.

1. **LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)**: In the LIFO method, the assumption is that the last items added to the inventory are the first ones sold. In other words, the most recently acquired inventory is considered to be sold first. As a result, the cost of goods sold (COGS) is calculated using the cost of the most recent purchases, while the ending inventory is valued at the cost of the earliest purchases. LIFO is often used in periods of rising prices because it results in a higher cost of goods sold, which can lead to lower taxable income.
1. **FIFO (First-In, First-Out)**: In the FIFO method, the assumption is that the first items added to the inventory are the first ones sold. In other words, the oldest inventory is considered to be sold first. As a result, the cost of goods sold (COGS) is calculated using the cost of the oldest purchases, while the ending inventory is valued at the cost of the most recent purchases. FIFO is often used in periods of rising prices because it results in a lower cost of goods sold, which can lead to higher taxable income.

**To better understand LIFO and FIFO, let's consider an example**:

**Example**: Suppose a company purchases 100 units of a product at different prices:

* January 1: 50 units at $10 each
* March 1: 30 units at $12 each
* June 1: 20 units at $15 each

At the end of the year, the company has sold 80 units of the product. Using LIFO and FIFO, we calculate the cost of goods sold and the value of ending inventory:

* **LIFO Calculation**: COGS = (20 units x $15) + (30 units x $12) + (30 units x $10) = $600 Ending Inventory = 20 units x $10 = $200
* **FIFO Calculation**: COGS = (50 units x $10) + (30 units x $12) = $900 Ending Inventory = 20 units x $15 = $300

As you can see, the LIFO method results in a lower ending inventory value but a higher cost of goods sold compared to the FIFO method. The choice between LIFO and FIFO can have significant effects on a company's financial statements and tax liabilities, and the method selected must be consistently applied over time. Different countries may have specific regulations on which inventory valuation method businesses are allowed to use for tax reporting purposes.

# Inventory valuation - which is followed by various accounting standards?

Various accounting standards allow businesses to use different inventory valuation methods, but the most commonly followed methods are:

1. **International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)**: IFRS allows businesses to use either the FIFO or weighted average cost methods for inventory valuation. Under IFRS, LIFO is not allowed.
1. **Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) - United States**: In the United States, businesses following US GAAP have the option to use any of the following inventory valuation methods: FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average cost. LIFO is a popular choice among US companies for tax purposes.
1. **ASPE (Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises) - Canada**: In Canada, businesses following ASPE can use the FIFO, LIFO, or weighted average cost methods for inventory valuation. However, LIFO is less common in Canada due to its impact on tax calculations.
1. **UK GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Practice) - United Kingdom**: UK GAAP allows businesses to use either the FIFO or weighted average cost methods for inventory valuation. LIFO is not commonly used in the UK.
1. **Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS)**: Ind AS allows businesses to use any of the following inventory valuation methods: FIFO, weighted average cost, and specific identification.
1. **Chinese Accounting Standards (CAS)**: In China, businesses are required to use the FIFO method for inventory valuation.

It's important to note that while these accounting standards provide guidance on inventory valuation methods, businesses must consistently apply the chosen method and disclose the method used in their financial statements. Additionally, some countries or industries may have specific regulations or restrictions on the inventory valuation method used for tax reporting purposes. As a result, businesses should consult with accounting professionals or tax advisors to ensure compliance with applicable regulations and accounting standards.
