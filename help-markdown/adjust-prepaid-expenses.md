# Prepaid expenses : Accounting standards and Accounting method

The concept of prepaid expenses, also known as expenses paid in advance, is applicable to both small and medium entities (SMEs) and larger entities following IFRS (full) or IFRS for SMEs. The treatment of prepaid expenses may vary slightly between the two frameworks.

Under IFRS (full), prepaid expenses are recognized as assets and are accounted for using the accrual basis of accounting. Prepaid expenses represent payments made in advance for goods or services that will be received in the future. Examples of prepaid expenses include prepaid rent, prepaid insurance premiums, or prepaid subscriptions. These expenses are initially recorded as assets and are gradually recognized as expenses over the period to which they relate.

Under IFRS for SMEs, there are specific provisions for the recognition, measurement, and presentation of prepaid expenses. The standard provides guidance on how to account for prepaid expenses, including when they should be recognized as assets and how they should be measured. The disclosure requirements may also differ from IFRS (full), being more streamlined and tailored to the needs of SMEs.

Regarding the accounting method (cash basis or accrual basis), the treatment of prepaid expenses would generally be different. The cash basis of accounting recognizes expenses and assets only when cash is paid or received, and therefore, prepaid expenses would not be recorded as assets under the cash basis. In contrast, under the accrual basis of accounting, prepaid expenses are recognized as assets when the payment is made, and the related expenses are gradually recognized over the period to which they relate.

It's important to consult the specific accounting standards and guidelines applicable to your jurisdiction and entity to ensure proper treatment and disclosure of prepaid expenses based on the chosen accounting framework.

# What is Prepaid expenses?

Prepaid expenses refer to expenses that have been paid in advance by a business but have not yet been consumed or used up. These expenses are considered assets because they represent future economic benefits that the business will receive.

When a business pays for an expense in advance, it initially records the payment as a prepaid expense on the balance sheet. This is because the payment has not yet been "*used*" or "*consumed*" to generate an expense in the relevant accounting period. Instead, it represents a future benefit that will be realised over time.

Common examples of prepaid expenses include:

1. **Insurance premiums**: If a business pays for insurance coverage for a future period, the portion of the premium that corresponds to the period yet to be covered is considered a prepaid expense.
1. **Rent**: If a business pays rent in advance for a future period, the portion of the rent that corresponds to the period yet to be occupied is considered a prepaid expense.
1. **Subscriptions**: If a business pays for a subscription service or membership in advance, the portion of the subscription fee that covers the future period is treated as a prepaid expense.
1. **Supplies**: If a business purchases supplies or inventory in advance and hasn't yet used them in its operations, the cost of those supplies is recorded as a prepaid expense.

Prepaid expenses are typically classified as current assets on the balance sheet if they will be consumed or used within one year. If the prepaid expense extends beyond one year, it is classified as a long-term asset.

As time passes and the prepaid expense is "used up" or consumed, the amount is gradually recognized as an expense in the appropriate accounting period. This is done through adjusting entries, which reduce the prepaid expense asset account and record the corresponding expense in the income statement.

Proper management and accounting for prepaid expenses are important for accurate financial reporting and ensuring that expenses are appropriately matched with the periods in which they provide economic benefits to the business.

The "*Adjustments - Prepaid expenses*" category or journal type allows for the recording of adjusting entries related to prepaid expenses. These entries are made to reflect the portion of the prepaid expense that has been consumed or expired during a specific accounting period.

# Treatment of Prepaid expenses in the new financial year?

In the new financial year, the treatment of prepaid expenses depends on whether the prepaid expenses are fully consumed or if there is still an unexpired portion remaining.

1. **Fully consumed prepaid expenses**: If the prepaid expenses have been fully consumed or used up during the previous financial year, there is no further adjustment needed in the new financial year. The prepaid expenses account will be reduced to zero, and the entire amount would have been recognized as an expense in the previous period.
1. **Unexpired portion of prepaid expenses**: If there is an unexpired portion of prepaid expenses remaining at the end of the previous financial year, it represents a future economic benefit to the business. In the new financial year, you need to recognize the portion of the prepaid expenses that corresponds to the current accounting period as an expense.

To record the adjustment for the unexpired portion of prepaid expenses in the new financial year, follow these steps:

* Identify the remaining unexpired portion of prepaid expenses.
* Debit the relevant expense account for the portion of the prepaid expense that corresponds to the current accounting period.
* Credit the Prepaid expenses account for the same amount.

This entry recognises the portion of the prepaid expense that has now become an expense in the new period. The Prepaid expenses account will reflect the reduced unexpired portion.

Remember to review and adjust the prepaid expenses account periodically throughout the new financial year to reflect any changes or updates in the prepaid expenses.

# Prepaid expenses - is this applicable to cash basis accounting method?

Prepaid expenses are typically not applicable to the cash basis accounting method.

In the cash basis accounting method, revenue and expenses are recognised when cash is received or paid, respectively. This means that expenses are recorded when the actual payment is made, rather than when the economic benefit is consumed or received.

Prepaid expenses, on the other hand, represent expenses that have been paid in advance but have not yet been consumed or utilised. They are recognised as assets on the balance sheet until they are gradually expensed over the period in which the related benefit is received.

Since the cash basis accounting method focuses on cash transactions, it does not recognise prepaid expenses as assets or allow for their gradual recognition as expenses over time. Instead, expenses are recorded when the cash is paid, regardless of the period to which they relate.

Prepaid expenses are more commonly associated with the accrual basis accounting method, where revenue and expenses are recognized when earned or incurred, regardless of the timing of cash flows.
