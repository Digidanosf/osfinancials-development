# What types of discount received is there?

There are various types of discounts that a business can receive, depending on the nature of the transaction and the terms negotiated with the supplier. Here are some common types of discounts received:

1. **Trade discounts**: Trade discounts are reductions in the listed or catalog price of goods or services offered by suppliers to businesses. These discounts are usually based on the volume or quantity of purchases made or on established trade agreements. Trade discounts are not recorded as income or revenue but are rather treated as a reduction in the cost of purchases.
1. **Cash discounts**: Cash discounts, also known as prompt payment discounts or early payment discounts, are incentives offered to customers for paying their invoices early or within a specified time period. For example, a supplier may offer a discount of 2% if payment is made within 10 days of the invoice date. Cash discounts are recorded as a reduction in the expense or accounts payable when the payment is made.
1. **Quantity discounts**: Quantity discounts are discounts provided based on the quantity of goods or services purchased. These discounts encourage customers to buy larger quantities, often resulting in lower per-unit costs. Quantity discounts can be negotiated with suppliers based on the volume of purchases or predetermined quantity thresholds.
1. **Seasonal or Promotional discounts**: Seasonal or promotional discounts are temporary price reductions offered by suppliers to promote sales during specific periods or for specific products or services. These discounts are often associated with holidays, special events, or product launches. They can be a percentage reduction or a specific amount off the regular price.
1. **Rebates**: Rebates are discounts provided by suppliers after the completion of a specific condition or action. For example, a supplier may offer a rebate to a customer who achieves a certain sales target or purchases a specific quantity of goods within a specified period. Rebates are typically claimed by the customer and are recorded as a reduction in expenses or accounts payable.

It's important for businesses to carefully track and record the different types of discounts received, as they can impact the cost of goods sold, expense recognition, and overall financial performance. Proper documentation and accounting treatment are necessary to accurately reflect the discounts received in the financial statements.

# What is discount received?

Discount received refers to a reduction in the purchase price of goods or services granted by a supplier to a customer as an incentive or reward for prompt payment or other favourable terms. It is a form of trade discount offered to encourage early settlement of an invoice or to promote customer loyalty.

When a business receives a discount from a supplier, it means that the supplier is reducing the amount payable by the business for the goods or services provided. The discount received is typically expressed as a percentage or a specific amount deducted from the original invoice amount.

For accounting purposes, the discount received is recorded as a reduction in the cost of the purchases or expenses incurred by the business. It is treated as a reduction in the expense rather than as income. The discount received is usually recognized as a separate line item in the financial statements or recorded as a deduction on the purchase invoice.

The accounting entry to record a discount received is typically as follows:

1. **When the purchase is initially recorded**:&nbsp;
- &nbsp;

  - **Debit**: Purchases or Expense Account&nbsp;
  - **Credit**: Accounts Payable or Cash (depending on the payment method)

2. **When the discount is received and applied**:&nbsp;
- &nbsp;

  - **Debit**: Accounts Payable (for the discounted amount)&nbsp;
  - **Debit**: Discount Received (for the amount of the discount)&nbsp;
  - **Credit**: Cash (if the payment is made in cash) or Accounts Payable (if the payment is made through credit)

By recording the discount received separately, businesses can track and analyse the impact of discounts on their financial performance and evaluate the effectiveness of their discount policies or supplier relationships.

- &nbsp;

  - **Debit**: Accounts Payable (for the reduced amount after the trade discount)
  - **Credit**: Trade Discounts Received (for the amount of the trade discount)

In this entry, the accounts payable is reduced by the amount of the trade discount, reflecting the lower amount payable to the supplier.
