# What is the difference of Accounting terminology used in GAAP and IFRS?

GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) and IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) are two sets of accounting standards used by companies to prepare financial statements. While there are many similarities between GAAP and IFRS, there are also some key differences in the accounting terminology used by the two standards:

1. **Revenue recognition**: GAAP and IFRS have different rules for when revenue can be recognized. Under GAAP, revenue can be recognized when it is earned, while under IFRS, revenue can be recognized only when it is probable that economic benefits will flow to the company.
1. **Inventory valuation**: GAAP allows for the use of LIFO (last-in, first-out) inventory accounting, while IFRS does not. Instead, IFRS requires companies to use the FIFO (first-in, first-out) or weighted average cost methods.
1. **Financial statement presentation**: GAAP and IFRS have different requirements for the presentation of financial statements. For example, GAAP requires a separate statement of comprehensive income, while IFRS allows for comprehensive income to be included in the statement of profit and loss or in a separate statement.
1. **Goodwill impairment**: GAAP and IFRS have different rules for testing for goodwill impairment. Under GAAP, goodwill is tested for impairment at least annually, while under IFRS, it is tested only when there is an indication of impairment.
1. **Lease accounting**: GAAP and IFRS have different rules for accounting for leases. Under GAAP, leases are classified as either operating or finance leases, while under IFRS, leases are classified as either finance leases or operating leases.

These are just a few examples of the differences in accounting terminology used by GAAP and IFRS. It is important for companies to be aware of these differences and to ensure that they are using the appropriate accounting standards for their specific needs and requirements.
