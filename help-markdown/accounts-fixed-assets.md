# What is a brief description of Fixed Assets and how is it classified?

Fixed assets, also known as long-term assets or non-current assets, are tangible or intangible assets that a company holds for long-term use in its business operations rather than for sale. These assets are not intended for conversion into cash or consumption within one operating cycle, and their useful life typically extends beyond one year.

## Brief Description of Fixed Assets:

Fixed assets can take various forms and play a crucial role in a company's operations. Some common types of fixed assets include:

1. **Tangible Fixed Assets**: These are physical assets that have a tangible form. Examples include:
- &nbsp;

  - **Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP\&E)**: Includes land, buildings, machinery, vehicles, and equipment used in the production and delivery of goods and services.
  - **Furniture and Fixtures**: Includes office furniture, fixtures, and fittings used in the workplace.
  - **Computer Hardware**: Includes desktops, laptops, servers, and other computer equipment used in the company's operations.

2. **Intangible Fixed Assets**: These are non-physical assets that lack a physical form but have long-term value to the company. Examples include:
- &nbsp;

  - **Intellectual Property**: Includes patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets that grant the company exclusive rights to its creations or innovations.
  - **Goodwill**: Represents the excess of the purchase price over the fair value of net assets acquired in a business combination.

## Classification of Fixed Assets:

Fixed assets are classified based on their nature and use. They are typically grouped into the following categories:

1. **Tangible Fixed Assets**: This category includes all physical assets, such as property, plant, equipment, and vehicles.
1. **Intangible Fixed Assets**: This category includes non-physical assets, such as intellectual property, goodwill, and software.

Fixed assets are recorded on the company's balance sheet under the non-current assets section. They are reported at historical cost less accumulated depreciation (for tangible assets) or amortization (for intangible assets). Depreciation and amortization are used to allocate the cost of fixed assets over their estimated useful lives to reflect their gradual consumption or obsolescence.

Properly managing fixed assets is crucial for companies to ensure they can effectively conduct their business operations, maintain competitiveness, and generate future revenue. Additionally, keeping accurate records of fixed assets helps in financial reporting, taxation, and decision-making processes.

# List of accounts in charts of accounts for plant, property and equipment

Here's an alphabetical list of common plant, property, and equipment (PP\&E) accounts that you might find in a chart of accounts, along with brief descriptions of each account:

1. **Accumulated Depreciation - Buildings**: Represents the cumulative depreciation of buildings over their useful life.
1. **Accumulated Depreciation - Equipment**: Represents the cumulative depreciation of equipment over its useful life.
1. **Accumulated Depreciation - Vehicles**: Represents the cumulative depreciation of vehicles over their useful life.
1. **Buildings**: Represents the cost of buildings owned by the organization for operational or investment purposes.
1. **Equipment**: Represents the cost of equipment, such as machinery and tools, used in the organization's operations.
1. **Land**: The cost of land owned by the organization for operational or investment purposes.
1. **Leasehold Improvements**: Represents the cost of improvements made to leased property.
1. **Vehicles**: The cost of vehicles owned by the organization for business purposes.

Please note that the specific PP\&E accounts used in a chart of accounts can vary based on the organization's industry, size, and accounting practices. Additionally, the chart of accounts should be tailored to provide accurate and relevant information for financial reporting and analysis. Proper accounting of PP\&E is essential for assessing an organization's capital investments and determining the depreciation expense, which impacts the income statement and overall financial health.
