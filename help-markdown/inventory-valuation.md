# What is inventory valuation?

Inventory valuation is the process of determining the monetary value of a company's inventory (i.e., the goods held for sale or used in the production of goods for sale) at a specific point in time. It is a crucial aspect of financial reporting and is essential for calculating the cost of goods sold (COGS) and the value of ending inventory on the balance sheet.

There are several methods used to value inventory, and the choice of method can have significant effects on a company's financial statements and profitability. The most common inventory valuation methods include:

1. **First-In, First-Out (FIFO)**: This method assumes that the oldest inventory items are sold first. The cost of goods sold is based on the cost of the earliest purchases, and the ending inventory is valued at the cost of the most recent purchases.
1. **Last-In, First-Out (LIFO)**: This method assumes that the most recent inventory items are sold first. The cost of goods sold is based on the cost of the most recent purchases, and the ending inventory is valued at the cost of the earliest purchases. LIFO is not allowed in all accounting standards and is less commonly used in some countries due to tax implications.
1. **Weighted Average Cost**: This method calculates the average cost of all inventory items available for sale during a specific period and applies that average cost to the cost of goods sold and ending inventory.
1. **Specific Identification**: This method values each individual item of inventory separately based on its actual cost.

The choice of inventory valuation method can have significant impacts on a company's financial statements, tax liabilities, and profitability. It is essential for businesses to consistently apply the chosen method and disclose the method used in their financial statements to ensure transparency and comparability of financial information.

Inventory valuation is especially important in industries where inventory represents a significant portion of the assets, such as retail, manufacturing, and wholesale businesses. Properly valuing inventory allows businesses to determine the true cost of goods sold, track inventory turnover, and make informed decisions about pricing, production, and inventory management.
