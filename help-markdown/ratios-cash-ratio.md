# Cash Ratio

***

The Cash Ratio, also known as the Cash Asset Ratio or the Cash Coverage Ratio, is a liquidity ratio that assesses a company's ability to pay off its current liabilities using only its cash and cash equivalents. It provides a conservative measure of a company's short-term liquidity, focusing solely on its most liquid assets.

The formula for calculating the Cash Ratio is as follows:

***Cash Ratio** = (Cash and Cash Equivalents) / Current Liabilities*

**Where**:

* &nbsp;
  * **Cash and Cash Equivalents** include cash on hand and highly liquid assets that are easily convertible to cash within a short period, typically within 90 days. Examples include cash in hand, cash in bank accounts, and short-term investments with maturities of 90 days or less.
  * **Current Liabilities** represent the company's obligations that are due within one year from the reporting date. Examples include accounts payable, short-term debt, and other short-term liabilities.

The Cash Ratio indicates how well a company can meet its short-term debt obligations without relying on other current assets, such as accounts receivable or inventory. A higher Cash Ratio implies a stronger ability to pay off current liabilities using only cash and cash equivalents, which is generally considered favourable as it shows a conservative approach to liquidity management.

However, it's essential to interpret the Cash Ratio in the context of the specific industry and the company's unique circumstances. Industries with stable and predictable cash flows may have lower Cash Ratios, while those with uncertain or cyclical cash flows may aim for higher Cash Ratios to ensure they can meet their short-term obligations in challenging times.

Like any financial ratio, the Cash Ratio should be analysed alongside other liquidity ratios and financial performance indicators to gain a comprehensive understanding of a company's financial health and liquidity position.
