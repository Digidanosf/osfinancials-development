# Which accounting standards is applicable to businesses in Canada?

As of ChatGPT's last update in September 2021, the accounting standards applicable to businesses in Canada are primarily governed by the Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises (ASPE) and the Accounting Standards for Not-for-Profit Organizations (ASNPO). These standards are issued by the Accounting Standards Board (AcSB) of the Canadian Institute of Chartered Accountants (CICA).

1. **Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises (ASPE**): ASPE applies to private companies, which are typically smaller and privately held businesses. ASPE provides specific accounting principles and guidelines for preparing financial statements in accordance with Canadian Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) for private enterprises.
1. **Accounting Standards for Not-for-Profit Organizations (ASNPO)**: ASNPO sets out the accounting principles for not-for-profit organizations, including charities and other non-profit entities. It provides guidance on how to report financial transactions and activities for these types of organizations.

In addition to ASPE and ASNPO, publicly accountable enterprises in Canada, such as publicly traded companies and certain financial institutions, are required to follow the International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) as issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB). IFRS is also used in many other countries worldwide.

It's important to note that accounting standards and regulations can change over time, so I recommend checking with the Canadian Accounting Standards Board or consulting with a qualified accounting professional for the most up-to-date information on accounting standards applicable to businesses in Canada.

# What accounts is suggested for payroll in Canada?

There are several accounts that businesses in Canada may use to properly track and manage payroll-related transactions. Some suggested accounts include:

1. **Salaries and Wages Expense**: This account is used to record the gross wages and salaries paid to employees during a specific period.
1. **CPP Payable:** This account is used to track the employer and employee portions of the Canada Pension Plan contributions withheld from employees' paychecks.
1. **EI Payable**: This account is used to track the employer and employee portions of Employment Insurance premiums withheld from employees' paychecks.
1. **Income Tax Payable**: This account is used to track the amounts of federal and provincial income tax withheld from employees' paychecks.
1. **XPP Payable**: This account is used to track any extra payroll expenses incurred by a business, such as overtime pay or bonuses, that have not yet been paid to employees.
1. **Employee Benefits Payable**: This account is used to track any employee benefits owed to employees, such as vacation pay, sick pay, or health insurance premiums.

Properly tracking payroll-related transactions in these accounts is important for accurately reporting payroll expenses, remitting payroll taxes to the government, and ensuring timely payment of employee wages and benefits. It is important to consult with an accountant or bookkeeper to determine the most appropriate accounts for your business's specific payroll needs.

## Is there any sub accounts suggested to control Payroll expenses in the balance sheet?

Yes, there are several sub-accounts that businesses in Canada may use to control payroll expenses in the balance sheet. Some suggested sub-accounts include:

1. **Gross Payroll**: This account is used to track the total amount of salaries and wages paid to employees during a specific period, before any deductions are made for payroll taxes, benefits, or other expenses.
1. **Payroll Taxes Payable**: This account is used to track the amounts of CPP, EI, and income tax withheld from employees' paychecks and owed to the government.
1. **Employee Benefits Payable**: This account is used to track the amounts owed to employees for benefits such as vacation pay, sick pay, or health insurance premiums.
1. **XPP Payable**: This account is used to track any extra payroll expenses incurred by a business, such as overtime pay or bonuses, that have not yet been paid to employees.
1. **Net Payroll**: This account is used to track the amount of salaries and wages paid to employees after all deductions have been made for payroll taxes, benefits, and other expenses.
1. **Wages Payable**: This account is used to track the amounts owed to employees for regular wages or salaries.

Properly tracking payroll expenses in these sub-accounts can help businesses to better control their payroll costs, manage their cash flow, and ensure compliance with payroll regulations. It is important to consult with an accountant or bookkeeper to determine the most appropriate sub-accounts for your business's specific payroll needs.

## XPP Payable and CPP Payable is there a difference?

Yes, there is a difference between XPP Payable and CPP Payable.

XPP Payable refers to the extra payroll expenses that have been incurred by a business but have not yet been paid to its employees, such as overtime pay, bonuses, or other forms of additional compensation.

On the other hand, CPP Payable refers to the amounts of Canada Pension Plan contributions that have been withheld from employees' paychecks but have not yet been remitted to the government. Both employers and employees are required to contribute to the CPP program through CPP premiums.

While XPP Payable and CPP Payable are both payroll-related liabilities that businesses must track and remit on time, they represent different types of expenses. XPP Payable is an additional expense incurred by the business, while CPP Payable represents the employer and employee contributions to a mandatory government program.
