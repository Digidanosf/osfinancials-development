# Current Ratio / Working Capital Ratio

***

The Working Capital Ratio, also known as the current ratio, is a financial metric used to assess a company's short-term liquidity and its ability to meet its current obligations. It measures the company's ability to pay off its current liabilities using its current assets. The formula for calculating the Working Capital Ratio is as follows:

***Working Capital Ratio** = Current Assets / Current Liabilities*

**Where**:

* &nbsp;
  * **Current Assets** refer to assets that are expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year from the reporting date. Examples include cash, accounts receivable, inventory, and short-term investments.
  * **Current Liabilities** refer to obligations that are due within one year from the reporting date. Examples include accounts payable, short-term debt, and other short-term liabilities.

The ideal range for the Working Capital Ratio varies across industries, but generally, a ratio of 1.5 or higher is considered healthy. A ratio below 1 implies that the company may have difficulties meeting its short-term obligations, while a ratio significantly higher than 1 may indicate that the company is not efficiently using its short-term assets.

It's essential to interpret the Working Capital Ratio in the context of the specific industry and the company's unique circumstances. A high ratio may not always be desirable, as it could signify excess idle assets, while a lower ratio may not necessarily mean financial distress if the company can manage its short-term obligations effectively.

As with any financial metric, it's crucial to analyze the Working Capital Ratio in conjunction with other financial ratios and performance indicators to gain a comprehensive understanding of a company's financial health.
