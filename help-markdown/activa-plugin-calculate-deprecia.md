## Straight-line method

According to the fixed instalment method, or cost price method, a fixed amount of depreciation is written-off annually.

The amount of depreciation will be same every year. It is calculated according to the life span of a fixed asset.

&nbsp;For example, as prescribed by the South African Revenue Services (SARS) a delivery vehicle’s life span is four (4) years.&nbsp; The cost price of the asset will be written off over a period of four (4) years. The depreciation can be calculated by dividing the cost price by 4 years, or 25% per annum.

For example, as prescribed by the South African Revenue Services (SARS) a passenger car’s life span is five (5) years.&nbsp; The cost price of the asset will be written off over a period of five years. The depreciation can be calculated by dividing the cost price by 5 years, or 20% per annum.

# Diminishing-value method

If compared to the straight-line (fixed instalment) method, where a fixed amount is written-off over the life span of the fixed asset per year; the diminishing-value method amount of depreciation will differ from year to year and will decrease from year to year.

According to the diminishing-value method, most of the fixed asset's value will diminish, or be written-off in the first year, and a lesser amount in the second year, and even lesser amounts in the successive years.

Using the diminishing-value method, depreciation is calculated on the book value (tax value) of the asset at the end of each year. Depreciation is calculated on the purchase (cost) price, less any depreciation already claimed in previous years (accumulated depreciation).

The book value reduces or diminishes rapidly up to 40% in the first year and a lesser percentage in the second and successive years. This is why it is also referred to as the accelerated method.

There are two basic formulas that may be used to calculate depreciation when the diminishing balance method is used. These methods are: -

1. Fixed percentage on the diminishing (reducing) balance method.
1. The Sum-of-the-digits method.

## Fixed percentage on the diminishing (reducing) - value method

A fixed percentage of the book value is used to calculate the depreciation, which must be written off each year.

If the life span of an asset is for instance, five years, and is normally calculated as 20%, the percentage needs to be doubled to 40% in this case. The fixed percentage must be used to calculate the percentage on the reduced (diminished) balance at the beginning of the applicable year.

According to the straight-line method, the annual depreciation rate over 5 years, is 20% per year. &nbsp;

Using the double-declining-balance method, double the annual depreciation rate, from 20% to 40%. The 40% depreciation rate is used to calculate the depreciation for each year on the purchase (cost) price less the accumulated depreciation.

## Sum-of-the-years-digits method

#### Sum-of-the-Years-Digits Method of Depreciation

The **sum-of-the-years-digits (SYD)** method is an accelerated depreciation technique that allocates a larger depreciation expense in the earlier years of an asset's useful life and less in the later years. This method is useful for assets that lose value more quickly in their initial years.

#### Steps to Calculate Depreciation Using SYD

1. **Determine the Lifespan of the Asset**: Identify the total number of years the asset will be depreciated.
1. **Calculate the Sum of the Digits**: For an asset with a lifespan of n years, the sum of the digits is calculated as follows:&nbsp;

*Sum=1+2+3+…+n=n(n+1)2Sum=1+2+3+…+n=2n(n+1)​*&nbsp;

Sum=1+2+3+…+n=2n(n+1)​

*Sum=1+2+3+4+5=15Sum=1+2+3+4+5=15*

## Depreciation – Fixed Percentage on Diminishing Balance Method

The **diminishing balance method** (also known as the declining balance method) is an accelerated depreciation method where a fixed percentage is applied to the book value of the asset each year. This results in a higher depreciation expense in the earlier years and progressively smaller expenses in later years.

### Calculation Steps

1. **Determine the Depreciation Rate**: In this case, the depreciation rate is **40%**.
1. **Calculate Depreciation for Each Year**: The depreciation for each year is computed as: Depreciation Amount=Book Value at Beginning of Year×Depreciation RateDepreciation Amount=Book Value at Beginning of Year×Depreciation Rate
1. **Update the Book Value**: After calculating the depreciation expense for the year, subtract it from the book value to get the new book value for the next year: New Book Value=Previous Book Value−Depreciation AmountNew Book Value=Previous Book Value−Depreciation Amount

## Production method

The **Units of Production Method** is a depreciation approach that allocates the cost of a fixed asset based on its actual usage rather than the passage of time. This method is particularly useful for assets whose wear and tear is more closely related to their output or production levels, making it a popular choice in manufacturing and similar industries.

### Key Features

* **Usage-Based Depreciation**: Unlike other methods that spread depreciation evenly over time, the Units of Production Method ties the depreciation expense directly to the asset's output. This means that the more an asset is used, the higher the depreciation expense for that period.
* **Calculation**: To calculate depreciation using this method, you first determine the asset's **depreciable cost** (the initial cost minus its salvage value) and then divide it by the total expected production over the asset's useful life. The formula can be expressed as:

*Depreciation Expense = (Depreciable Cost / Total Estimated Production) × Actual Production for the Period*

This formula ensures that the depreciation expense reflects the actual usage of the asset.

### Applications

The Units of Production Method is particularly effective for assets like machinery, vehicles, or equipment that have a direct correlation between their usage and wear. For example, if a piece of machinery is expected to produce 100,000 units over its lifetime and costs $50,000 with a salvage value of $5,000, the depreciation per unit would be calculated as follows:

1. **Depreciable Cost**: $50,000 - $5,000 = $45,000
1. **Depreciation per Unit**: $45,000 / 100,000 units = $0.45 per unit

If the machinery produces 10,000 units in a year, the depreciation expense for that year would be:

*Depreciation Expense = $0.45 × 10,000 = $4,500*

This method allows businesses to match their expenses with the revenues generated from the asset, providing a more accurate financial picture.

### Conclusion

In summary, the Units of Production Method is a valuable tool for businesses that want to align their asset depreciation with actual usage. By focusing on output rather than time, this method provides a more precise reflection of an asset's value over its useful life.

## Fixed Assets (Small items) Purchased for Less than a Prescribed Amount

### Writing Off Small Fixed Assets

When it comes to small fixed assets purchased for less than a specified prescribed amount, businesses may have the option to **write off these assets in full**. This means that instead of depreciating the asset over its useful life, the entire cost can be expensed in the year of purchase. The specific threshold for this write-off is determined by legislation and can vary based on updates from tax authorities.

### Depreciation Legislation

The **depreciation amount** for fixed assets is often prescribed by tax legislation, which can change over time. Therefore, businesses must stay informed about any new legislation or practice notes issued by tax authorities that may affect how they account for these assets. In summary, small fixed assets can be fully written off if they fall below a certain cost threshold, and businesses should regularly check for updates in tax regulations that might influence their accounting practices.
